Grounding Dynamic State-Change Concepts on Multimodal Sensing for Closed-Loop Manipulation

Brainstorm seed · 2026-06-25 · status: brainstormed · anchor: BLADE · Index

Thesis. Existing work that grounds concepts on non-visual sensing grounds static properties (hardness, roughness, material) and uses them for one-shot selection (grasp this object this way). But manipulation is transformation: washing, heating, wetting, dissolving, loosening. The concepts that matter there are dynamic — about object state and state-transition — and the signal that reveals them often lives in a non-visual modality (thermal for "hot enough", acoustic for "coming to a boil", friction-tactile for "slippery now that it's wet"). The proposal: ground named, learned, transferable dynamic state-change concepts on multimodal non-visual sensing, monitor them over time, and use their value as the trigger for reasoned closed-loop correction — across a family of transformation tasks.

The reframe: ground verbs, not adjectives

The cleanest way to state the gap relative to the corpus. Tactile-language models like Octopi and TVL ground adjectives — is_rough, is_soft, is_metal — time-invariant unary predicates that enter a plan as preconditions for selection. Manipulation needs verbs: heating, wetting, loosening, dissolving, coming_to_a_boil. In BLADE's ontology, prior multimodal grounding does precondition predicates; this proposal grounds effect predicates and process-monitors — the temporal derivative of state. The robot does not just need to know an object is slippery; it needs to detect that it became slippery, and reason about what to do differently.

A taxonomy of dynamic manipulation concepts

These are not all the same; the distinctions drive the method and the benchmark design.

  1. Threshold / progress states — water hot enough, dish clean enough, dough kneaded enough. A latent quantity must cross a goal; monitor it, decide continue / intensify / stop. (Weiyu's kettle: "if water is too cold, make it hotter.") Closest existing perception: The Sound of Water (fill level from pour sound), Making Sense of Audio Vibration (liquid height) — but both regress a scalar, with no named concept and (mostly) no loop.
  2. Strategy-switching transitions — the dish became slippery after getting wet. A state change that should trigger a qualitatively different policy, not a monotonic gain bump. Weiyu's example is sharp because the correct response is more grip force AND gentler handling — non-monotonic and multi-attribute, requiring an understanding of why (wet → slippery, and often more fragile/deformable when wet). A reactive slip controller cannot produce "be gentler"; only reasoning over a grounded state-change concept can.
  3. Process-onset events — coming to a boil, the pour started splashing, the seal broke. Temporal patterns over a stream, frequently legible only in one non-visual modality (sound for boiling, contact-mic for splash/impact). Closest: HEAR (boil/pour stages from streaming audio), SonicSense (in-hand acoustics, but static state).
  4. Latent cross-modal states — water temperature, internal fill level, sealed-vs-open. Invisible; must be inferred from thermal / acoustic / tactile. The thermal branch is nearly empty: Heat-Induced Food State Changes grounds a dynamic heat state in language but from vision (VLM), not thermal sensing; the thermal material-recognition line is static.

Why this is a BLADE-shaped problem

BLADE learns predicates with preconditions and effects and grounds them with visual classifiers. The effects are exactly state-changes — but they are read off vision. This proposal is "BLADE for the verbs, on the modalities where the verbs actually register." Concretely, three things change:

  • The classifier's input is a time-series over a non-visual stream (a tactile/audio/thermal window), not a single RGB frame.
  • The predicate is temporal — it asserts a transition (became_slippery, reached_boil), so its semantics are over an interval, not an instant.
  • Its value is a correction trigger — a violated/changed effect predicate drives replanning or a control-parameter adjustment, closing a loop that BLADE leaves open.

Three framing spines (keep all alive)

Per Weiyu's call, hold all three; they are not mutually exclusive and could be three contributions of one project or a staged plan.

Spine 1 — Planner-level predicates (BLADE lineage; strongest novelty)

Learn multimodal, temporal effect/precondition predicates; monitor them online; a violated predicate triggers symbolic replanning. Differentiates hardest from VLAs and from REFLECT (whose symbolic layer is an LLM text summary, not grounded predicates). Highest systems cost. Contrast points already in corpus: Grounding Predicates through Actions (change predicates, but vision + offline), VisualPredicator (neuro-symbolic predicates via VLM, vision).

Spine 2 — Policy-level reasoned correction

The grounded state-change feeds an LLM/VLM that adjusts control parameters (force, impedance, heat, speed) or switches strategy. Faster to demo; more crowded. Closest: Tactile-VLA (force-word grounding + CoT increases force on a failed wipe — but the concept is a force-adverb, and the correction is monotonic), CLTP (LLM emits "reduce gripping force" from tactile, but static contact geometry), HEAR (audio stages condition continuous action). The differentiator: a non-monotonic, multi-attribute correction reasoned from a named state-change ("it's wet → gentler and firmer").

Spine 3 — Perception + transferable benchmark

Nail multimodal dynamic-concept grounding and ship a benchmark spanning a family of transformations (pour / boil / wash / knead / heat), leaving the loop for v2. Lowest systems risk; clean dataset contribution; attacks the field's biggest weakness — everything is one task. Contrast: Look for the Change / ChangeIt and Continuous Object State Recognition are single-modality (vision) and either passive or one-domain.

Novelty landscape — what's done, what's open

Honest verdict from a four-scout sweep (state-change recognition / acoustic process monitoring / tactile-thermal dynamic state / multimodal-temporal predicate grounding): not done as a whole, but no longer wide open. A frontier wave (Nov 2025 → Mar 2026) is converging right next to this idea; it must be positioned against deliberately. Each closest paper drops at least one of the four required axes.

The defensible gap (one line): learned, named, transferable dynamic state-change predicates — with precondition/effect semantics — grounded on non-visual multimodal sensing (thermal, acoustic, tactile/friction), monitored online, used as the trigger for reasoned closed-loop correction across a family of transformation tasks. Existing closed-loop semantic-correction work is vision-only at the symbol level (Code-as-Monitor, DoReMi, Inner Monologue) or non-symbolic at the multimodal level (REFLECT's text, FINO-Net's classifier); existing tactile-language work grounds only static properties for one-shot grasping (Octopi); existing change-concept grounding is vision-only and disembodied (DCL, Look for the Change).

Candidate task suite (generality via concept)

The strongest defense against "HEAR already did boil/pour" is to show the concept transfers. A suite where the same learned change-predicates recur across physically different processes:

  • Heat the water / boil the kettle — thermal + acoustic; hot_enough, coming_to_a_boil; correction = adjust heat / stop.
  • Wash the slippery dish — friction-tactile; became_slippery, fragile_when_wet; correction = more grip + gentler (non-monotonic).
  • Pour to a level / pour without splashing — acoustic; nearly_full, splashing; correction = slow / stop / re-aim.
  • Knead / mix to consistency — tactile-force + sound; kneaded_enough, dough_stiffening; correction = continue / stop.
  • Tighten / seat a part — force + acoustic click; seated, cross-threaded; correction = back off / re-seat.

The claim to defend: one grounding+monitoring mechanism yields predicates that transfer across these, because they share an abstract structure (threshold / transition / onset) even though the physics differ.

The sweet spot — cross-modal inferred concepts [N]

These exist only in the joint signal — no single sensor reports them — so resolving them needs fusion + reasoning. This is where the idea most clearly beats both single-sensor perception and end-to-end policies, and it's almost entirely unclaimed:

  • "Slippery and fragile now that it's wet" — tactile friction↓ + thermal (cooler) + vision (sheen). Drives the non-monotonic more-grip-yet-gentler correction.
  • "Soaked / saturated" — moisture + force (heavier) + tactile (squish).
  • "Sealed and full" vs "open and full" vs "sealed and empty" — active-acoustic tap × weight × vision: a 2×2 of states from three sensors.
  • "Oily vs wet vs icy" — three low-friction causes that feel alike tactilely but separate with thermal (icy=cold, oily=neutral, wet=evaporative-cool) + smell. A clean disambiguation task.
  • "Cooked but not burnt" — thermal + sizzle audio + smell + color. The target is a band, not a threshold.
  • "Simmering (not still, not boiling)" — an intermediate process state from audio + thermal; where scalar regressors fail and concepts win.

The throughline: the harder and more useful concepts are dynamic AND inferred (the CHANGE/PROGRESS × [N] corner), and they're nearly all open.

Open research questions

  1. Representation. How to represent a temporal change-predicate over a multimodal window so it is both classifier-learnable and planner-legible? (Event boundary + pre/post embedding? A learned monitor with a calibrated threshold? A code-as-monitor over sensor features rather than vision?)
  2. Acquisition. Where does the grounding signal come from — language-annotated multimodal demos (à la BLADE/Touch100k), LLM/VLM priors about "what boiling sounds like" (à la Teaching Physical Awareness through Sounds), or self-supervised onset detection from causal ordering (à la Look for the Change)?
  3. Usage / loop. Predicate → symbolic replan (Spine 1) vs. predicate → control-param correction (Spine 2)? When is each right? Does interactive perception (act to disambiguate the state) belong in the loop?
  4. Reasoned correction. How to get the non-monotonic, causal correction ("wet → gentler and firmer") rather than a reflex — and how to evaluate that it's reasoning, not pattern-matching?
  5. Transfer. Do change-predicates learned on one process generalize to another with the same abstract type? This is the generality-via-concept bet.

Positioning sentence (for a paper intro / proposal)

Robots that transform the world — cooking, cleaning, assembling — must track concepts that are about change, and much of that change registers in touch, sound, and heat rather than in pixels. We learn named, transferable predicates for object state-transitions, ground them on multimodal non-visual sensing, and monitor them online so that a violated effect becomes the trigger for reasoned closed-loop correction — extending compositional predicate-based manipulation from the visual properties of objects to the multisensory dynamics of processes.
