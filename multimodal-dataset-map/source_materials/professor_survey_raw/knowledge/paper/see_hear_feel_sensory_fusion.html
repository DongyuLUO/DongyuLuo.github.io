See, Hear, and Feel: Smart Sensory Fusion for Robotic Manipulation

Hao Li*, Yizhi Zhang*, Junzhe Zhu, Shaoxiong Wang, Michelle A. Lee, Huazhe Xu, Edward Adelson, Li Fei-Fei, Ruohan Gao†, Jiajun Wu† · Stanford / MIT · CoRL 2022 · arXiv:2212.03858 · PDF

One-liner. A behavior-cloning system that fuses camera (see), contact microphone (hear), and GelSight tactile (feel) through a multisensory self-attention model, showing that each modality dominates at a different phase of contact-rich tasks — vision for global pose, audio for invisible contact events, touch for local geometry/force — and that fusing all three sharply beats any subset.

Problem & motivation

Humans use all their senses; robot manipulation policies mostly use one or two modalities (usually vision, occasionally touch). Vision reliably captures the global setup but suffers from occlusion exactly when the end-effector is in contact; touch gives precise local contact geometry but only once contact happens; audio sends immediate feedback about contact events that may be visually invisible (e.g., a bead dropping into a cup, a peg striking a hard vs. soft bump). Prior work had combined vision+audio or vision+touch, but no prior work had simultaneously incorporated visual, acoustic, and tactile signals and studied their respective roles on challenging manipulation tasks. The paper claims to be the first such in-depth study.

Method

The system is an imitation-learning (behavior-cloning) pipeline with a multisensory self-attention fusion model, dubbed MULSA (Multisensory Self-Attention), shown in Fig 2b.

Sensing setup. A Franka Emika Panda arm carries: a third-person RGB camera (see, global view of robot + end-effector); a piezo contact microphone attached to the object of interest (hear, picks up vibration from any surface contact, minimal environmental noise); and GelSight vision-based tactile sensors on the gripper fingertips (feel, high-resolution contact geometry + force via an elastomer + embedded camera).

Model input. At each timestep the model takes {XV, XA, XT} — visual, acoustic, tactile. Audio is encoded as a mel spectrogram (treated as a 2D image). Each modality appends a short history of N=6 observations, so visual/tactile frames span a 3-second window aligned to the 0.5s audio segment used per step.

Feature extraction. Three separate ResNet-18 backbones (input channels modified per modality) produce a set of feature embeddings {e1,...,eN} of dimension D for each of v, a, t.

Modality-temporal fusion. The pooled set of features across all modalities and timesteps {v1...vN, a1...aN, t1...tN} is fused with standard multi-head self-attention (Fig 3). Three attention types emerge: (1) cross-modality attention (a feature attends to a different modality at the same time step), (2) cross-time attention (same modality, different time step, to capture temporal change), and (3) combined cross-modality-and-cross-time. The concatenated self-attention output m = [o1|o2|...|o3N] feeds an MLP that predicts the action û.

Training. Pure behavior cloning — cross-entropy loss between the human demonstrator's action u (a discrete, task-dependent action) and the predicted û. Actions are picked from a discrete set: for dense packing, an (x,y,z) end-effector displacement each ∈ {positive, negative, zero}; for pouring, a 2D (x, φ) action (horizontal align + rotation angle), each ∈ {positive, negative, zero}.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected human-demonstration data on two real tasks. Dense packing: 3D-printed cylinder peg + base with four bump types (hard slanted, soft slanted, left flat, back flat), 10 demo episodes per base; plus a harder real-world variant fitting a glass into a cluttered box (thin metal board, stuffed toy, towel, plastic plate as obstacles). Pouring: pour 1 mm beads from an in-hand cup to a fixed cup to a 40 g target, starting from 60 g or 100 g, 10 demos.
  • Hardware / simulator: Real robot only (no simulator). Franka Emika Panda 7-DoF arm, IK solver, 10 Hz Cartesian command, 200 Hz low-level loop. Intel RealSense D435 (320×240 @ 60Hz); HOYUJI TD-11 piezo contact microphone @ 44.1 kHz via TASCAM US-4×4HR (resampled to 16 kHz); GelSight tactile (400×300 @ 30Hz). Audio → mel spectrogram 64×50 over 0.5s (STFT Hann window 25ms, hop 10ms, 64 mels). Electronic weight scale under the fixed cup for pouring measurement.
  • Baselines: Direct Concat (concatenate all modality features, standard practice); Du et al. [6] "Play it by ear" (audio-visual LSTM imitation); and ablations of the authors' own model — MULSA(V) vision-only, MULSA(V+A), MULSA(V+T), MULSA(A+T, with a heuristic visual aligning policy).
  • Compute: not reported.

Note the loss cases: in pouring, V+A+T actually does NOT win every column — its 60g result (1.06) beats V+T (2.81), but the headline win over Direct Concat (2.46) is the real story since Concat is the fair fusion baseline. In dense packing, vision-only collapses (0.25), confirming the policy can't run on vision alone. Attention visualization (Fig 5) is the qualitative payoff: during dense packing, attention rides on vision before contact, swings to audio+touch the moment the peg strikes a bump (vision is occluded by the box walls), then returns to vision to push down. During pouring, attention shifts to audio to hear the first bead drop and to time the stop — matching the authors' intuition that the stop-pouring moment "heavily relies on the acoustic feedback."

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • The framework is pure behavior cloning, requiring collected human demos; the way a robot perceives multisensory signals may differ from how humans leverage them in demonstration. RL is suggested as future work to let the robot discover the best fusion automatically.
  • The robot/sensor setup is task-specific (microphone glued to the object, GelSight on fingers); a more general, easily-adapted setup — e.g., a tailored gripper with mic + GelSight built in — is flagged as future work.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Tiny-sample statistics. Dense-packing success rates are over only 10 test trials per base, and the real-world Table 2 is a single 0.80 with no variance. Several cells in Table 1 are exactly 0.00 or 1.00, i.e., 0/10 or 10/10 — clean but statistically thin; a 0.80 vs 0.75 gap is one or two trials.
  • The contact mic is attached to the manipulated object, not the robot. That is a strong assumption: in the wild you can't usually instrument the object you're inserting. ManiWAV (same batch) directly tackles this by mounting the mic on the gripper instead — a more deployable design.
  • Discrete action space. Actions are quantized to {+, −, 0} per axis. This sidesteps continuous force/velocity control; the "feel" modality informs a categorical decision rather than a modulated force — so the paper demonstrates multisensory perception more than multisensory control.
  • Audio is rendered as an image and fed to a ResNet like the other two modalities — convenient, but it discards the option of native waveform/temporal modeling, and the 0.5s window may blur fast transients that are the whole point of "invisible contact events."
  • No simulator and no released large dataset — reproduction requires rebuilding the exact hardware rig.

Why I care

This is one of the cleanest empirical demonstrations of my batch thesis: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable. Here the model literally cannot distinguish a hard slanted bump from a soft one without audio (surface_is_hard), cannot judge insertion tilt without touch (peg_is_tilted), and cannot decide when to stop pouring without sound (cup_is_full). The attention-shift visualization (Fig 5) is a concrete picture of which sense grounds which predicate at which phase — precisely the information a BLADE-style predicate classifier would need but couldn't get from a third-person camera alone.

Relative to BLADE: BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers fθ(p): O → {T,F} over RGB observations, and its authors flag that contact-rich tasks need "richer contact detection." This paper is direct evidence that the observation space O should be multisensory: the predicates living in prea/effa for contact-rich behaviors (is_inserted, is_full, surface_is_rough) are audibly/tactilely grounded. A natural extension of BLADE would route each learned predicate to the modality that actually carries its truth value — and MULSA's per-timestep self-attention is one mechanism for learning that routing. The discrete-action, imitation-only design here is a much shallower control stack than BLADE's diffusion-policy + bi-level planner, so this is a perception-side complement, not a planning competitor.
