Learning Compositional Behaviors from Demonstration and Language

Weiyu Liu*, Neil Nie*, Ruohan Zhang, Jiayuan Mao†, Jiajun Wu† · Stanford / MIT · CoRL 2024 · arXiv:2505.21981 · PDF · project page

Note: This is Weiyu's own paper (first author, equal contribution with Neil Nie). The summary is faithful to what the paper claims; "Why I care" is framed as anchoring future work rather than learning from external work.

One-liner. BLADE automatically recovers PDDL-style behavior abstractions (preconditions, effects, a contact-primitive body) from language-annotated demos by querying an LLM, learns visual classifiers for the predicates and diffusion-policy controllers for the bodies, then composes them via bi-level planning at test time — generalizing to novel initial states, perturbations, geometric constraints, and partial observability without any manually labeled symbolic state.

Problem & motivation

Long-horizon manipulation needs composition: chain short-horizon learned behaviors via planning in an abstract action space. The hard part is automatically acquiring those abstractions — preconditions and effects grounded in raw perception and low-level control — without manual state labels or hand-written symbolic definitions. Prior work either hand-defines transition models, learns flat dynamics that don't generalize long-horizon, or limits hierarchical policies to following sequentially specified subgoals. Symbolic-grounding approaches like Migimatsu & Bohg (cited as [66]; not yet ingested) require labeled symbolic states, and prior LLM-for-planning work (SayCan, Text2Motion) assumes state abstractions are already given.

Method

Three stages, shown in Fig 2 of the paper:

1. Behavior description learning. Each demo trajectory is segmented by contact primitives using proprioception (gripper open/close state) and object segmentation. The 7 primitives are: open-gripper, close-gripper, move-to(x), grasp(x,y), place(x,y), move(x), push(x). The LLM is then prompted with: (i) environment description, (ii) the segment's language annotation plus those of preceding and following segments, (iii) all contact-primitive sequences associated with the language label across the dataset, (iv) PDDL syntax instructions and one example. The LLM proposes a PDDL operator extended with a body field (the contact-primitive sequence). Operators are refined by abstract verification: simulating accumulated effects of prior segments and checking each operator's preconditions hold — resample if not. This is done at the behavior level (for bad pre/eff) and the contact-primitive level (e.g., grasp can't be immediately followed by another grasp).

2. Classifier and policy learning. Once operators are fixed, BLADE auto-labels every observation in the dataset. Before behavior a starts at step ot: predicates in prea are true and those in effa are false; after a finishes at ot': effa labels are true and propagated forward until explicitly altered by another behavior. Key contrast with Migimatsu & Bohg [66]: they use only the first and last frame of each segment; BLADE uses every frame, vastly increasing classifier training diversity. Predicates are learned as neural-net classifiers fθ(p): O → {T, F}. In the real world, an open-vocabulary object detector ([68]) crops the input to the relevant object before the classifier (e.g., only faucet pixels feed turned-on(faucet)). For each behavior, a diffusion policy [1] is trained as the low-level controller.

3. Bi-level planning and execution. At test time the LLM translates the natural-language goal into a first-order logic formula over the learned predicates. The fast-forward heuristic [69] (planner-agnostic in principle) plans a behavior sequence in symbolic space. Execute the first behavior's diffusion policy, replan after each completion — which is how perturbation recovery, partial observability, and execution noise are handled. Geometric/visibility constraints are encoded as preconditions (e.g., opening a cabinet door requires the door visible and nothing blocking it); when violated, the planner generates moves to satisfy them automatically. Partial observability uses a most-likely-state assumption.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: CALVIN benchmark (split D, ~6 hours of teleop play data, 34 behavior types) with 6 newly-designed generalization tasks across three categories — Abstract Goal, Geometric Constraint, Partial Observability — each requiring 3–7 chained behaviors. 20 initial states × 3 seeds per task.
  • Hardware / simulator: CALVIN simulator + Franka Emika 7-DoF arm with parallel jaw gripper. Five RealSense RGB-D cameras (1 wrist-mounted, 4 workspace). Real-world domains: Make Tea and Boil Water, 85 teleoperated demos each via 3D mouse.
  • Baselines: HULC [72] (hierarchical policy from language-annotated play, CALVIN-native); SayCan [6] (short-horizon LLM feasibility); Robot-VILA [57] (VLM action prediction); Text2Motion / T2M-Shooting [51] (learned long-horizon feasibility model; tested with both GT and learned state classifiers).
  • Compute: not reported.

Key wins:

  • +65% over HULC on Abstract Goal (despite same play-data input) — HULC's hindsight-relabeling can't chain multi-behavior plans (e.g., 7 behaviors to place all blocks in a closed drawer).
  • +49% over Robot-VILA on Abstract Goal — VILA frequently predicts infeasible first actions (e.g., always tries to open drawer first).
  • Only method that handles Geometric Constraint and Partial Observability in a non-trivial way, because constraints are encoded as preconditions the planner can resolve.
  • Ablation (Table 2): the every-frame predicate labeling beats Migimatsu & Bohg's first/last-frame approach by +20.7% F1 overall, +16.3% on object states, +38.6% on spatial relations — which translates directly into the planning success gap.

Real world (Fig 5): BLADE completes ≥4/6 tasks for all four generalization types (unseen initial conditions, state perturbation, geometric constraints, partial observability) in both Make Tea and Boil Water. Robot-VILA fails most generalization tasks — e.g., places a teabag without locating the cup-in-cabinet, or loops on grab-kettle when the kettle is occluded.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Segmentation depends on gripper state — caging grasps and contact-rich tasks would need richer contact detection.
  • Predicate names are assumed given in natural language; BLADE learns dependencies between actions but not predicate invention. VLM-driven predicate invention is flagged as future work.
  • Noisy state classification causes planning failures — need planners robust to estimation noise.
  • Diffusion policies generalize only over local pose variation; novel compositions still bottleneck on policy generalization.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Scalability of LLM operator proposal is untested past 34 behavior types — how does prompt size and consistency degrade at, say, 200?
  • What happens when two LLM-proposed operators have mutually inconsistent preconditions? Abstract verification catches per-trajectory inconsistency, but cross-operator conflicts aren't explicitly addressed.
  • Continuous parameters (pour amount, grasp pose, force) sit entirely inside the diffusion policy — the abstraction layer is purely categorical, which may be a limitation for force-modulated tasks.
  • Real-world results are reported as count-of-success out of 6, not success rate with seeds — smaller statistical claim than the simulation table.

Why I care

This is the anchor paper for the line of work I want to keep pushing: auto-generated structured abstractions over imitation-learned skills. Several directions worth tracking forward from this:

  • Predicate invention — the most-cited limitation by the authors. Worth a topic page once 2–3 papers are ingested (Konidaris et al. [32], Silver et al. [33], Han et al. [36] are obvious candidates).
  • Operator scaling — revisit when papers on LLM-PDDL grounding at larger action libraries appear.
  • Force/continuous parameters — potential new contribution area, marrying the symbolic body with continuous constraint specs.
  • Connections to VLA models — the "structure-as-prior vs scale-everything" debate; BLADE is a clean counterpoint to RT-2-style end-to-end policies. A topic page comparing the two paradigms would situate future work.
