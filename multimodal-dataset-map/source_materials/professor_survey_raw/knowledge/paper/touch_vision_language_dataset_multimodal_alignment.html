A Touch, Vision, and Language Dataset for Multimodal Alignment

Letian Fu*, Gaurav Datta*, Huang Huang*, William Chung-Ho Panitch*, Jaimyn Drake*, Joseph Ortiz, Mustafa Mukadam, Mike Lambeta, Roberto Calandra, Ken Goldberg · UC Berkeley / Meta AI / TU Dresden · ICML 2024 · arXiv:2402.13232 · PDF

One-liner. TVL builds the first open-vocabulary touch–vision–language dataset (44K in-the-wild DIGIT–webcam pairs, 10% human-labeled and 90% GPT-4V pseudo-labeled), then trains a tactile encoder into CLIP's latent space via pairwise contrastive alignment across all three modalities — showing that touch carries semantic content that pure vision-language models miss (+29% tactile classification, +12% over GPT-4V on a new tactile-description benchmark).

Problem & motivation

Touch is a core human sensing modality but is essentially absent from multimodal generative language models. Two obstacles: (1) natural-language labels for tactile data are scarce, and (2) aligning tactile readings with both vision and language is hard. Prior tactile datasets use closed-vocabulary human annotations (e.g., 40-way material classification) collected in controlled lab settings; the authors say they are aware of no tactile dataset with open-vocabulary language labels. The paper's thesis is that the tactile modality "captures more than tactile-visual associations" — it has diverse semantic content and deep integration with language — so binding touch directly to language (not just to vision, as ImageBind does) should yield richer representations.

Method

1. Data collection (TVL dataset). A handheld 3D-printed device (Fig 2) co-mounts a DIGIT vision-based tactile sensor and a Logitech BRIO webcam, recording synchronized vision+tactile at 30 Hz while a human presses and slides across surfaces "in the wild". TVL amalgamates two sub-datasets: SSVTP (Kerr et al. 2023, 4,587 robot-collected image–touch pairs; UR5 captures a top-down image then presses DIGIT onto the prearranged location) and the newly-collected HCT (Human-Collected Tactile): 39,154 in-contact pairs + 169,292 out-of-contact pairs from 5 humans over 20 hours, across 1,486 touch trajectories. Visual data are captured at an oblique angle so the sensor and contact point stay in the camera's field of view, preserving touch–vision synchronicity.

2. Labeling. SSVTP (strong visual-tactile alignment) is human-labeled: annotators pick up to 5 adjectives from a 400-word tactile vocabulary (ajbarnett 2023). HCT in-contact frames are pseudo-labeled by GPT-4V, prompted with the full image plus a crop centered on the contact point (the crop aligns GPT-4V's outputs with human ones). Trajectories where GPT-4V refuses (motion blur, low light) are backfilled from other in-contact frames in the same trajectory, else dropped. Net: 4,587 human + 39,154 pseudo-labeled = 43,741 labeled image–touch pairs; 254 unique tactile adjectives; 99%/1% train/test split with the test set (402 pairs) human-labeled.

3. Tactile encoder (contrastive). In contrast to ImageBind, which binds every modality only to vision, TVL computes contrastive loss between each pair of modalities — tactile–text, tactile–vision, and vision–text — per batch (Fig 4, left). The tactile encoder is a ViT (Tiny 5.7M / Small 22M / Base 86M) trained with InfoNCE; vision and text encoders are the frozen OpenCLIP encoders, and the projectors are removed so the tactile encoder projects directly into CLIP's shared latent space. To fight overfitting on the small 44K set, a fraction γ = 10% of training data are out-of-contact frames labeled "background", and words in each tactile description are randomly shuffled/subsampled.

4. TVL-LLaMA (generative model). Following ImageBind-LLM / LLaMA-Adapter, they swap ImageBind's encoders for the TVL encoders, average the tactile and vision latents into a single token, and finetune LLaMA-2 7B with a zero-initialized gate + LoRA to generate tactile descriptions from vision+tactile inputs (Fig 4, right). Trained on TVL + Alpaca + LLaVA 150K to avoid the model refusing to answer tactile questions (a safety-finetuning artifact).

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: TVL dataset (44K pairs = SSVTP 4,587 + HCT 39,154 in-contact); 254 tactile adjectives; 99%/1% split (402-pair human-labeled test set). New TVL Benchmark: open-vocabulary 402-way tactile classification (top-1/top-5, tactile–vision and tactile–language) + a tactile-semantic description task scored 1–10 by text-only GPT-4 against human ground truth.
  • Hardware / simulator: DIGIT vision-based tactile sensor + Logitech BRIO webcam on a 3D-printed handheld rig (HCT); UR5 robot for SSVTP. No simulator.
  • Baselines: Encoder — OpenCLIP (vision-text) and SSVTP pretrained tactile-vision encoder. Generative — LLaVA-1.5 7B/13B, ViP-LLaVA 7B/13B, LLaMA-Adapter, BLIP-2 OPT-6.7b, InstructBLIP 7B/13B, GPT-4V, and SSVTP-LLaMA (an ablation trained on SSVTP tactile+vision encoders only, no language alignment).
  • Compute: not reported (encoder sizes given: ViT-Tiny 5.7M, ViT-Small 22M, ViT-Base 86M; LLM is LLaMA-2 7B + LoRA).

Results

Tactile encoder (Table 1). The TVL ViT-Tiny encoder reaches 36.7% / 70.3% top-1/top-5 on tactile–text and 79.5% / 95.7% on tactile–vision. OpenCLIP cannot do tactile–text at all, and the SSVTP encoder — trained on the small in-lab set — collapses on the in-the-wild TVL test set (0.2% / 0.3% tactile–vision), motivating the need to scale tactile–vision data. The TVL encoder inherits OpenCLIP's vision–text score (28.4% / 64.9%) unchanged.

All TVL-LLaMA variants beat GPT-4V (the label-generating teacher) by ≥+12% on the overall TVL score, and beat open-source VLMs by larger margins, all statistically significant (paired t-test vs GPT-4V, p < 0.05). The +29% headline is the encoder's tactile classification gain over visual-tactile / generic vision-language encoders.

Where it loses / nuances. The "+29%" claim is about the encoder, but the student/teacher dynamic flips by metric: ViT-Base has the best validation accuracy yet ViT-Small/Tiny win on the test set because the GPT-4V training labels are less detailed/accurate than the human-annotated test labels (Table 3a) — a distribution shift the authors flag explicitly. On synchronized tactile–vision data, ViT-Base does win (81.7% vs 79.5%). Ablations (Table 3): disabling the tactile-text loss (ImageBind-style) drops tactile-text accuracy 36.3% → 20.3% (Table 3b); dropping vision modality during contrastive training drops tactile-text to 29.9% and tactile-vision to 1.0% (Table 3c); adding 10% out-of-contact data and prompt formatting give only marginal gains (3d, 3e); and the GPT-4V-pseudo-labeled HCT alone gives comparable performance to full supervision (Table 3f), supporting the self-supervised/pseudo-label scaling story.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • The "distinct nature of touch compared to vision" limits the accuracy of tactile labels derived solely from vision (GPT-4V) — the teacher is fundamentally blind to touch.
  • The data-collection hardware may not give the camera an unoccluded view of the contacted surface (the sensor itself occludes it), increasing touch–vision alignment difficulty and degrading pseudo-label quality.
  • The dataset is small (44K); they call for scaling touch-vision-language data to improve multimodal alignment.

What I noticed reading it:

  • The whole supervision signal is bounded by a vision-only teacher. GPT-4V cannot perceive touch, so "soft/plush/cushioned" is inferred from looks, not feel — the mislabeled examples in Fig 3 (e.g., screwdriver labeled "hard, smooth, reflective" with no compliance info) show the ceiling here. The model can only learn the visually-correlated slice of tactile semantics.
  • The benchmark is graded by GPT-4 (text-only) for descriptive overlap against human adjectives — an LLM-as-judge metric on a small 402-pair test set. Beating GPT-4V on a benchmark whose ground truth is partly GPT-4V-derived needs care; the human-labeled SSVTP portion is the cleaner signal and is where TVL-LLaMA's margin is largest (6.16 vs 5.02).
  • DIGIT is a single sensor type with a deformable gel; "tactile" here is high-resolution surface texture/geometry from one sensor, not force, compliance, temperature, or slip. The semantic richness claim is real but narrower than "touch" in general.
  • Encoder scaling is non-monotonic on the test set (Tiny > Base) purely because of teacher-label noise — a caution for anyone hoping to scale tactile encoders on pseudo-labeled data without better labels.

Why I care

This is squarely on the batch thesis that many manipulation predicates (surface_is_rough, is_deformable, material_is_compliant) are not visually evaluable — they live in touch. TVL is the dataset/encoder backbone that makes "tactile–language" a thing: it shows touch can be projected into the same CLIP latent space as vision and language, which is exactly the substrate a BLADE-style predicate classifier would need if the predicate were tactile rather than visual. In BLADE, predicate classifiers fθ(p): O → {T,F} crop the RGB observation to the relevant object; TVL is the proof-of-concept that the same move works on a tactile observation, with language as the supervision channel. The honest caveat is that TVL is a perception/representation paper, not a manipulation or planning paper — there is no robot policy, no long-horizon task, no precondition/effect reasoning. Its relevance to my line is upstream: it is the touch-grounding layer that a future tactile-predicate extension of BLADE would build on. The GPT-4V-as-teacher limitation also rhymes with BLADE's reliance on an LLM for symbolic structure — in both cases the LLM/VLM injects a semantic prior that is only as good as what it can perceive, which for touch is a hard ceiling.

Related

Papers cited that should likely be ingested next:

  • Kerr et al. 2023 — SSVTP (self-supervised visuo-tactile pretraining) — the robot-collected sub-dataset and tactile-encoder predecessor TVL builds on and ablates against (SSVTP-LLaMA).
  • Girdhar et al. 2023 — ImageBind (one embedding space to bind them all) — the bind-everything-to-vision baseline TVL improves on with pairwise alignment; direct architectural foil.
  • Lambeta et al. 2020 — DIGIT (low-cost compact tactile sensor) — the sensor used for all tactile data; foundational hardware dependency.
  • Yang et al. 2024 — UniTouch (Binding Touch to Everything) — concurrent work binding touch to vision for open-vocabulary classification; the closest competitor in framing.
  • Han et al. 2023 — ImageBind-LLM — the multimodal-LLM finetuning recipe TVL-LLaMA adapts.

Newly ingested in the 2026-06-24 batch — directly relevant:

  • Octopi and Octopi-1.5 — same cluster (touch–language grounding); tactile-language models that reason about object properties, the closest siblings to TVL-LLaMA.
  • UniTouch — concurrent touch-binding model; direct comparison on open-vocabulary tactile classification.
  • Touch100k and TLV — later/parallel touch-language-vision datasets; TVL is the open-vocabulary precursor they scale.
  • Sparsh and AnyTouch — self-supervised / unified tactile representation models; the representation-learning neighbors that drop the language-alignment objective TVL centers.
  • Touch and Go and ObjectFolder — prior human-collected / multisensory vision-touch datasets TVL extends toward open-vocabulary language.
