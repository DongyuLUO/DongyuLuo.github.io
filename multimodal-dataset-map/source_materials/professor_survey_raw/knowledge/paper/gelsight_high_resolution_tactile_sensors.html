GelSight: High-Resolution Robot Tactile Sensors for Estimating Geometry and Force

Wenzhen Yuan, Siyuan Dong, Edward H. Adelson · MIT CSAIL · 2017 · Sensors (MDPI), 17(12):2762 · MDPI · PDF

One-liner. The canonical write-up of GelSight — a vision-based tactile sensor that turns a soft elastomer + camera into a high-resolution depth camera for touch, reconstructing micron-scale contact geometry from photometric stereo and reading force/shear/slip from the motion of embedded markers; this is the hardware substrate that most modern visuo-tactile learning (DIGIT, TACTO, Sparsh, TVL, ...) is built on.

Problem & motivation

Most pre-2017 robot tactile sensors (resistive, capacitive, piezoelectric arrays) measured a force / pressure distribution over a fingertip at coarse spatial resolution (e.g., the commercial Pressure Profile Systems array at 5 mm). The authors argue that for robots to manipulate like humans, geometry sensing matters as much as force: a fine, deformable surface that reads local shape and texture lets a robot recover object identity, material, contact area, and shear — properties a sparse force grid cannot. The paper's framing question is "what kind of tactile signal is needed for robots to better understand the environment?", and its answer is high-resolution geometry from a soft, vision-instrumented contact surface.

Sensor design / formalism

Core idea (retrographic sensing). A slab of clear elastomer is coated on its outer face with an opaque, reflective membrane of known, consistent reflectance. When an object presses in, the membrane deforms to a relief replica of the object's surface but keeps its reflectance — so the optical signal depends only on local surface shape, not the object's own color or material. A camera behind the gel images this membrane under controlled illumination from multiple directions.

Shape via photometric stereo. Model the membrane as a height field z = f(x,y) with normal N = (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y, −1). Under known, evenly-distributed lighting the shading at a pixel depends only on the local gradient, so the RGB image I(x,y) = R(∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y) (Eq. 1–2), where the three color channels carry illumination from three distinct directions. A calibrated lookup table R−1 (built by pressing a sphere of known diameter, which spans all surface-normal directions) inverts intensity→gradient; the height map is then recovered by integrating the gradient field, solved fast via a Poisson equation ∇2f = g (Eq. 3–4) with a DST-based fast Poisson solver so it runs online. Resolution: 1–2 μm when optimized for metrology; 20–100 μm for compact robot-finger versions — far finer than human skin.

Force / torque / slip via markers. Black dots are printed between the elastomer base and the reflective membrane (Fig. 3). Their in-image motion du = u − u0 tracks lateral surface displacement. The pattern of the marker motion field encodes the kind of load (normal → radial spread; shear → uniform translation; in-plane torque → rotation; tilt → gradient), and the magnitude is roughly proportional to the force/torque (Fig. 7). Because the displacement→force relation is nonlinear and shape-dependent, the authors use a CNN (VGG-16, ImageNet-pretrained, last layer → 4 outputs Fx,Fy,Fz,Tz) on the difference image to regress force/torque for unseen object shapes. Slip / incipient slip is read from inhomogeneity of the marker field: peripheral markers lag central ones before gross slip, quantified by the entropy of the displacement distribution (Eq. 5, Fig. 8–9).

Hardware lineage. The paper reviews several builds: the original desktop cube (Johnson & Adelson 2009/2011, semi-specular or side-lit matte coating, photometric stereo over 3–6 colored LED directions); the first fingertip GelSight (Li et al. 2014, Baxter gripper, 35×35×60 mm, 4-color line-array LEDs, semi-specular coating, hand-soldered — sensitive but over-complicated to fabricate); and the improved fingertip sensor (Dong et al. 2017, hexagonal, 3-color RGB collimated LEDs at 71°, matte coating, mostly Formlabs-printed parts, open-sourced CAD — better precision, far simpler to reproduce). Section 4.3 details elastomer fabrication: clear silicone/TPE base (Shore A 5–20; fingertip gel ~Shore 00-45, neo-Hookean μ ≈ 0.145 MPa), water-transfer-printed markers, and airbrushed/brushed metal-flake or aluminum-powder reflective coatings.

Coverage / setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: No public benchmark. Force CNN trained on ~28,815 GelSight images of 6 balls (ø 12–87 mm), 5 cylinders (ø 10–70 mm), 2 flat rigid surfaces; tested on 6705 images of 3 novel objects (ball ø25, cylinder ø22, flat plane).
  • Hardware / simulator: Multiple GelSight builds (desktop cube; fingertip versions for Baxter and WSG-50 grippers). Logitech C310 USB webcam, 640×480 @ 30 Hz; ground-truth force from an ATI Nano-17 force/torque sensor. No simulator (this is the real-hardware paper).
  • Baselines: Conceptual comparison to prior tactile sensors (resistive/capacitive arrays, BioTac, OptoForce, TacTip, fluid-filled vision-based sensors); for force, GelSight is calibrated against the ATI Nano-17 rather than compared to a competing learned baseline.
  • Compute: not reported (VGG-16 fine-tuning; no training-time or hardware details given).

Key insights / results

Sensitivity. Minimum distinguishable normal force is mostly <0.05 N (Table 1) — finer than the ATI Nano-17 reference can even resolve in most contact conditions, so the table is floored at <0.05 N. GelSight detects smaller contact forces than the commercial F/T sensor used to calibrate it.

Shape. Measured surface normals correlate well with ground truth from a calibration ball, especially the yaw (gradient direction: R2 ≈ 0.99) more than the pitch (gradient magnitude: R2 ≈ 0.78–0.83) (Fig. 13). Reconstructions capture both overall shape and fine texture (fingerprint ridges, a coin's relief).

Marker displacement is linear with shear force even into partial slip (Fig. 7c), and displacement-field entropy rises monotonically with partial slip, giving a usable slip-warning signal (Fig. 8). Applications surveyed: USB-cable insertion via in-hand localization, fabric/material recognition from texture, hardness estimation from press-in dynamics, and slip-aware grasping.

Limitations & open questions

Author-stated. (1) The force CNN does not generalize force cleanly across geometries — predictions stay biased by contact shape; robust force needs either a much broader (possibly simulated) dataset or a better feature extractor. (2) Slip/partial-slip analysis works best on flat or low-curvature contacts with enough markers in the patch; for complex/textured objects you must combine marker motion with shape motion. (3) Fabrication of the sensitive fingertip versions is hard to standardize (the 2017 hex design was specifically built to fix this).

What I noticed reading it. (a) This is a review/synthesis of the GelSight lineage, not a clean single-system evaluation — "the GelSight sensor" silently refers to several different builds across sections, so quoted numbers (resolution, force R2) are not all from one device. (b) Force ground truth is bootstrapped from one ATI Nano-17, whose own floor (0.05 N) caps the reported sensitivity — the "more sensitive than ATI" claim is asserted qualitatively, not quantified below that floor. (c) Force/slip results are on a tiny set of canonical shapes (balls/cylinders/planes); the admitted shape-bias means out-of-distribution force reads should be treated as unreliable. (d) Marker tracking and photometric-stereo calibration are per-sensor and hand-tuned, foreshadowing the later push toward simulators (TACTO, Taxim) and learned representations (Sparsh, T3) that decouple downstream tasks from each physical gel's idiosyncrasies.

Why I care

This is a method/hardware anchor, not a manipulation-policy or planning paper — but it underwrites a thesis I care about for BLADE: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable. is_grasped, is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, surface_is_rough, about_to_slip live in contact geometry, shear, and slip — exactly the signals GelSight makes machine-readable at high resolution. BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers; the open question this paper plants is whether the same precondition/effect abstractions could be grounded in tactile classifiers over GelSight-style height/shear maps, letting bi-level planners reason about contact states a camera cannot see. GelSight is the substrate the rest of the 2026-06-24 touch batch sits on: it is the sensor behind the touch–language datasets (TVL, Touch and Go), the tactile foundation models (Sparsh, T3), and the simulators that model it (TACTO, Taxim). Read it as foundational context for that whole line, not as a manipulation result in itself.
