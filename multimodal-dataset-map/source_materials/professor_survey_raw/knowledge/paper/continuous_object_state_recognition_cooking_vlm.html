Continuous Object State Recognition for Cooking Robots Using Pre-Trained Vision-Language Models and Black-box Optimization

Kento Kawaharazuka, Naoaki Kanazawa, Yoshiki Obinata, Kei Okada, Masayuki Inaba · The University of Tokyo (JSK) · 2024 · arXiv preprint · arXiv:2403.08239 · PDF

One-liner. Treats a cooking object's state change (water boiling, butter melting, egg cooking, onion stir-frying) not as a one-shot classification but as a continuous time-series signal — the cosine similarity between live images and a set of state-describing text prompts — and uses black-box optimization over per-prompt weights (fit to a sigmoid) to turn that noisy similarity trace into a sharp, threshold-able "the change just happened" detector, all from a frozen pre-trained VLM with zero task-specific training or image annotation.

Problem & motivation

Robot state recognition is usually framed as classification at a single time point (door open/closed, light on/off). Cooking breaks this: food state changes happen continuously over time, are complex enough to defy manual programming, and vary with the specific food, heat power, and recipe. Prior cooking-robot systems either time the heat application rather than sense the food state directly, or train per-state CNN classifiers on image datasets (sliced/chopped/etc.) that (a) only handle discrete predefined classes, (b) can't capture the degree or onset of change, and (c) require a separate dataset, model, and program per state — a code- and compute-management burden. The authors want a single frozen model that captures the continuous trajectory of a state change from language, with no annotation and no per-state training.

Method

The pipeline (Fig. 2) has three pieces: continuous similarity tracking, sigmoid fitting, and black-box weight optimization.

1. Continuous similarity from a frozen VLM. Among VLM task types only Image-to-Text Retrieval (ITR) yields a continuous scalar (a cosine similarity), so it is the operative primitive. Images Vt are acquired at 10 Hz; each is encoded to vt, each text prompt Qi to qi, and the similarity vtTqi is plotted over time. A rising trace (e.g. similarity to "boiled water") indicates the state change. Prompts come in positive sets Q1 (change occurred, e.g. "boiled water") and negative sets Q-1 (not occurred, e.g. "unboiled water"); a sign variable pi flips negatives.

2. Weighted aggregate + sigmoid fit. The aggregate signal is a weighted average awt = Σ piwivtTqi / Σ wi (Eq. 1), min-max scaled into [0,1] using a 3-second moving average (Eq. 3), then fit to a sigmoid f(t) = 1 / (1 + e-α(t-β)) (Eq. 2) by nonlinear least squares with α,β ≥ 0. The sigmoid is justified as covering the four qualitative shapes of state-change curves (Fig. 3) and giving a natural [0,1]-range threshold (e.g. detect at the 0.8 / 80%-change point).

3. Black-box optimization of prompt weights. The core trick: different prompts have wildly different recognition quality, and using all of them uniformly lets bad prompts drag down the signal. So they optimize the weight vector w (each wi ∈ [0,1]) to maximize an evaluation function E(w) = αβ/σ (Eq. 4) — reward a steep change (large α), occurring late in the process (large β), with small sigmoid fitting error σ. Optimization is done once on a calibration dataset Dopt via a genetic algorithm (DEAP: 300 individuals, 300 generations, blend crossover, Gaussian mutation, tournament selection); TPE and CMA-ES were tried with no significant difference. No image annotation is needed — only the captured time series and the text set.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected. Four state changes — water boiling, butter melting, egg cooking (fried egg), onion stir-frying. Each has an optimization set Dopt and an evaluation set Deval; the true change-end time tdata is annotated for evaluation only. Up to ~50 text prompts per state, generated by varying article ("a"/"the"/"this"/ "that"/none), state word (boiled/unboiled, melted/not-melted synonyms and antonyms), and phrasing form. No public benchmark.
  • Hardware / simulator: Real PR2 mobile robot observing a stove at 10 Hz; for onion, an image taken each time the PR2 stirs with a spatula. Closed-loop cooking demo: PR2 boils water, detects boiling (ImageBind+OPT), blanches broccoli, detects butter melt, then stir-fries.
  • Baselines: No external baseline. Three internal settings compared: OPT (proposed black-box-optimized weights), ONE (single best prompt, the one maximizing E), ALL (all prompts weighted equally). Two frozen VLMs compared: CLIP and ImageBind.
  • Compute: not reported (only GA hyperparameters given).

Results

Metrics: E (higher = sharper, better-fit change) and tdiff = |tdetected - tdata| (lower = more accurate onset, threshold Cthre=0.8).

  • OPT consistently wins. Water boiling, CLIP: ALL E=0.0 / tdiff=61.0 s → ONE E=44.4 / 26.6 s → OPT E=207.3 / 1.8 s (Fig. 6); ImageBind OPT reaches tdiff=1.1 s on Deval. Butter melting and onion stir-frying similarly recognized with high accuracy.
  • ONE > ALL usually, but not always — uniform use of all prompts often gives E≈0 and large tdiff because noisy prompts dominate.
  • Egg cooking is the failure case. The egg white turns white abruptly at the very start (curve shape (iii) in Fig. 3), then only subtle yolk change follows; neither CLIP nor ImageBind detects the later change well, so tdiff stays large (often >80 s).
  • ImageBind > CLIP on stability overall (less Dopt/Deval divergence), though CLIP sometimes wins; the authors suggest combining both.
  • Closed-loop cooking succeeded (Fig. 10): boiling- and melting-detection triggered the next manipulation step end to end.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Abrupt-then-subtle changes (egg cooking) are not captured — bottle- necked on the VLM's sensitivity to fine-grained change; expect future VLMs to help.
  • Recognition uses only image–text correspondence; video, audio, and heatmaps ("indispensable for cooking") are unused and could be integrated.
  • The text set Q is hand-authored; an LLM could auto-generate synonyms/antonyms for a more practical system.
  • Neither single model dominates; multi-model fusion is left to future work.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Recognition only, no control coupling beyond a trigger. The output is a scalar onset signal that flips one downstream step; there is no symbolic predicate, no precondition/effect structure, no replanning — just "fire when similarity crosses 0.8."
  • Calibration is supervised-by-the-back-door. "No image annotation" is true, but Dopt must be collected per state and E's design (reward late, steep change) bakes in the assumption that the change happens once and near the end — which is exactly why egg cooking (early change) breaks. The method is tuned to monotone end-loaded transitions.
  • The interesting move is hiding in plain sight: ImageBind can bind audio, depth, heatmaps, and IMU, yet only the image modality is used. The whole multimodal-sensing payoff is left on the table.
  • Per-state recalibration and threshold choice (0.8) are manual knobs; no transfer across foods or heat settings is shown.

Why I care

This is the closest pure change-tracking-for-robots work to my target idea: it explicitly reframes object state from a static classification to a continuous, temporally-monitored signal of transition ("boiling now", "melted now"), which is precisely the dynamic, change-oriented concept axis I want to ground. On that axis it is squarely on-theme — it monitors a process over time and even closes a coarse loop (boil-detected → add broccoli) in the cooking demo.

But it is on the wrong side of two of my axes. (1) Vision/VLM only: the entire signal is image–text cosine similarity; the non-visual modalities where many transitions actually announce themselves (the sound of a rolling boil, the thermal jump, the change in stirring force) are unused — ImageBind could bind them, yet doesn't. (2) Recognition, not closed-loop semantic correction: the output is a threshold-crossing trigger, not a learned predicate with preconditions/effects that a planner can reason over and replan against. So versus BLADE: BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers that are static snapshots (true/false at a frame) but embeds them in a symbolic precondition/effect structure with bi-level replanning; this paper learns a dynamic temporal signal but with no symbolic structure and only a trigger-level loop. The synthesis I want — dynamic transition concepts, grounded on non-visual sensing, expressed as replanning-ready predicates — sits exactly between them. This paper proves the continuous-temporal framing is tractable with frozen foundation models; my contribution is to push it onto multimodal sensing and into the symbolic/closed-loop layer it stops short of. It pairs naturally with its own follow-up Recognition of Heat-Induced Food State Changes (Kanazawa et al.) and contrasts with sound-centric monitors like HEAR.
