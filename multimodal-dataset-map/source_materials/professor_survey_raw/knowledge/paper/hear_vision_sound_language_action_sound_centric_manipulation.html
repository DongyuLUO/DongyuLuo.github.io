Towards the Vision-Sound-Language-Action Paradigm: The HEAR Framework for Sound-Centric Manipulation

Chang Nie, Tianchen Deng, Guangming Wang, Zhe Liu, Hesheng Wang · Shanghai Jiao Tong University / University of Cambridge · 2026 · arXiv preprint (SAGE journal format) · arXiv:2603.16086 · PDF

One-liner. HEAR formalizes Vision-Sound-Language-Action (VSLA) — VLA conditioned on continuous streaming audio under delayed, low-rate, open-loop decision loops — and instantiates it with a four-module architecture (streaming causal audio memory + omni-VLM reasoner + predictive audio world-model + flow-matching policy) so a robot can listen while acting and commit to actions only after a fleeting acoustic cue (a beep, a click, the onset of a rolling boil) has actually occurred.

Problem & motivation

Existing VLA models treat sound as a static pre-execution prompt (ASR transcripts appended to the language input) or as a waveform rendered into a 2D "camera-view" image, and they predominantly handle human speech. The authors argue this misses the regime where fleeting environmental acoustics provide the only reliable, low-level state-verification signal during execution — the sputtering of boiling liquid, a microwave "ding", an impact click. Two structural obstacles motivate the work. (1) The Blind Execution Interval (BEI): because large backbones run below the control rate, modern VLAs use action chunking — predict an H-step chunk and execute it open-loop — creating a gap during which the robot is oblivious to new observations. Combined with end-to-end latency, a transient cue (50–200 ms) can begin and end inside this gap and "vanish" before the next windowed audio observation, permanently destroying the evidence of a state transition. (2) Temporal Motion Collapse / temporal aliasing: sound-centric tasks have long monitoring phases where vision is quasi-static; snapshot-conditioned policies see nearly identical inputs across many decisions yet the correct behavior changes with elapsed time, so the robot drifts or freezes during the "waiting dilemma." Unlike vision (which is forgiving of delay because objects persist in view), audio's decision-critical information is concentrated in brief, non-repeatable events — so a delay that merely makes a visual frame slightly stale can cause catastrophic failure on an audio-gated task.

Method

The paper has two parts: a formalism (the VSLA paradigm) and an architecture (HEAR).

VSLA formalism. A robot runs a low-level controller at fc Hz but the policy is queried only at sparse decision times {tk} with an integer end-to-end latency τsys. The per-decision observation is otk = (multi-view RGB, a causal audio window Atk of the last Twin seconds, language l, proprioception). The paper makes the audio interface explicitly causal (the window depends only on samples that have arrived by the delayed boundary) and derives the condition (Eq. 7) under which a transient event terminates strictly before the next window begins — "evidence vanishing." Success is judged by a timing-sensitive criterion (Eq. 10): reaching the geometric goal before the required acoustic cue fires is counted as failure, even if the final state looks visually correct. This enforces sound-causality and rejects visual shortcuts.

HEAR architecture (Fig. 2), four modules decoupling high-frequency sensing from low-frequency decisions:

  • Historizer — a Streaming Stateful Transformer that maintains a compact causal audio memory htk updated independently of the policy query cadence. Incoming audio between decisions is partitioned into short causal packets (L = 640 samples / 40 ms each) and folded into a 16-token memory via a 4-layer transformer, so short-lived cues that arrive during the BEI persist into the next decision (resolves Challenge 1).
  • Envisioner — a coarse-to-fine hierarchical reasoner. A high-level omni-modal VLM (Qwen3-Omni) fuses multi-view RGB + language + proprioception + audio window + the Historizer memory, emitting (i) a semantic latent ztk, (ii) a key-value cache reused by a lightweight low-level LLM (Qwen3-0.6B) that produces a control feature utk, and (iii) a structured JSON stage description (e.g. {"stage":"wait","subgoal":"listen for beep"}) trained by token-level NLL (Ltext). The stage text forces the latent to separate phases that are visually quasi-static but acoustically distinct.
  • Advancer — a decoder-only audio world model (4 layers) that, from ztk, predicts the near-future discrete audio codes (Mimi neural tokenizer) for the interval up to the next decision, trained by cross-entropy on codebook indices (Ladv). This injects a predictive temporal-dynamics signal so the shared latent tracks the flow of time even when vision is static (resolves Challenge 2 / Temporal Motion Collapse). At deployment it need not actually generate audio — it only shapes the latent during training.
  • Realizer — a conditional flow-matching (CFM) policy conditioned on utk via FiLM-style adaptive normalization; generates a smooth H-step joint-position action chunk with a small fixed number of Euler ODE steps (NODE = 8), executed open-loop. Smooth chunks reduce mechanical ego-noise that would mask weak cues during listening.

Total loss Ltotal = Lflow + wadv Ladv + wtext Ltext, trained by imitation from expert action chunks (H = 32), with a two-stage recipe: pretrain on OpenX-Sound, then fine-tune per task.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: OpenX-Sound — ~120k Open X-Embodiment episodes retrospectively augmented with time-aligned synthesized audio tracks (16 kHz) via video-to-audio generation models; manual audit reported 98.7% synchronization within 100 ms tolerance. HEAR-Bench — the first sound-centric manipulation benchmark, built on a real-time audio-physics co-simulation engine over RoboTwin2; 7 tasks across 4 cue categories (event-triggered alarms: Alarm Clock, Microwave; human speech: Check Yes, Interrupt; continuous process: Pour Water, Boil Water; physical interaction feedback: Check Materials), 100 trials each, with strict timing-sensitive success rules.
  • Hardware / simulator: Simulation on dual-arm RoboTwin2 (3 RGB views + streaming mic). Real world on a single-arm Franka Panda with head + arm cameras and a standard workspace microphone (deliberately no contact mics or ear-in-hand arrays), 4 tasks (Moka Coffee, Answer Phone, Shake Bottle, Real Alarm Clock), 100 trials each. Control at fc = 30 Hz with a simple PD controller, no IK / motion planning / collision checking.
  • Baselines: VLA backbones OpenVLA and π0.5, each with two audio adapters — -Waveform (render 1D audio window as a 2D line-plot "camera") and -ASR (transcribe to text prompt); plus compact audio-native policies Play it by Ear and ManiWAV. All baselines share the identical two-stage protocol, visual inputs, causal audio segments, and qpos action chunks.
  • Compute: Ubuntu 22.04 workstation, 2× NVIDIA RTX 5090 GPUs, PyTorch/CUDA. AdamW, lr 1e-4, Twin = 4.0 s (64,000 samples), 200k pretrain steps + 50k (sim) / 30k (real) fine-tune steps per task; wadv = 0.1, wtext = 0.05.

HEAR wins every task (avg 0.81 vs 0.61 for the best baseline, π0.5-Waveform). Waveform rendering is the strongest baseline on loud spikes but is brittle to where the onset lands in the window; ASR helps only on explicit speech and discards non-speech timbre/prosody; vision-only backbones collapse (the early-press shortcut is rejected by the causal success rule).

Real robot (Table 4, 100 trials/task): HEAR 0.54 avg vs 0.39 (π0.5-Waveform), 0.34 (Play it by Ear / ManiWAV). Long-horizon real-world process monitoring is hard for everyone — Moka Coffee 0.18, Answer Phone 0.15 — but HEAR triples the best baseline on those; on Shake Bottle 0.88 and Real Alarm Clock 0.96 it is strongly robust.

Ablations (HEAR-Bench avg over 7 tasks, Table 5): removing OpenX-Sound pretraining 0.81→0.69; removing the Historizer is the most damaging, 0.81→0.57 (causal-GRU memory 0.67, EMA-pooling 0.62, all below the Streaming Stateful Transformer); removing the Advancer 0.81→0.73 (worst on continuous process tasks); removing stage text 0.77; replacing CFM Realizer with direct regression 0.70. Temporal Motion Collapse (Table 6, fraction of low-motion <2 mm steps): full HEAR 0.15 vs 0.33 (no Advancer) / 0.38 (regression Realizer) — the Advancer + CFM keep motion continuous during listening. Replanning frequency (Table 7): aggressive re-querying does not substitute for causal memory (execute-first-action-only drops to 0.71); open-loop chunk execution with the Historizer is best (0.81). Error decomposition (Table 8): HEAR cuts both false-trigger (act early) to 0.02 and miss-detection (fail to act after cue) to 0.04, vs e.g. 0.91/0.57 for OpenVLA.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • OpenX-Sound audio is synthesized from video; generated audio may omit subtle contact cues, alter event timing, or hallucinate sounds, capping the value of purely-offline pretraining — real fine-tuning remains necessary. They call for native datasets with synchronized mics + acoustic metadata.
  • Single workspace microphone — cannot localize sources or separate overlapping events (the cocktail-party problem); multi-mic arrays + beamforming are future work.
  • Trained entirely by offline imitation, so robustness is bounded by the noise/timing diversity in demos; real-world ego-noise and reverberation degrade perception. Online fine-tuning / RL is flagged.
  • Long-horizon unconstrained real-world cooking acoustics (Moka Coffee 0.18) remain an open challenge.

What I noticed reading it:

  • The "concepts" here are implicit and task-tied: the audio world model predicts raw future audio codes, and stage labels are free-text JSON ("wait", "listen for beep") baked into one network — there is no named, reusable, transferable audio predicate (no is_boiling(pot) classifier you could drop into a planner). State transitions are tracked in a latent, not exposed as precondition/effect symbols.
  • Success criteria are hand-authored per task in the simulator (timestamps of cue onset vs goal). The audio "concept" of completion is supplied by the benchmark's privileged trigger, not learned as a transferable recognizer — so generalization to a genuinely novel sound-defined state is untested.
  • Closed-loop here means re-querying after each open-loop chunk with a persistent memory; it is reactive timing, not semantic replanning over a symbolic state — there is no explicit failure-detection / recovery module, no planner.
  • arXiv ID 2603.16086 and the "Chi et al. 2025 / Intelligence et al. 2025" citations imply a future/placeholder dating; treat venue as preprint.

Why I care

This is the closest existing prior art on the AUDIO axis of the dynamic-state-change idea, and it both validates and partially threatens it. Mapped against BLADE and the "ground dynamic state-transitions on non-visual sensing, monitored over time, to drive closed-loop correction" thesis:

  • It covers the dynamic-over-time axis squarely. Pour Water and Boil Water are exactly the "coming to a boil" / "is full now" continuous process-monitoring cases — sound evolves while vision is quasi-static. Its audio world model (Advancer) explicitly learns temporal dynamics of the soundscape, and its timing-sensitive success rule operationalizes "act only after the state has actually changed." This is the strongest demonstration in the corpus that non-visual, time-evolving evidence can gate manipulation. BLADE's Boil Water demo is visual + endogenous; HEAR shows the acoustic, exogenous version of the same task.
  • It threatens the novelty on "monitor a state transition via audio." If the pitch is merely "robots can use boiling/pouring sound to know when to act," HEAR already does it end-to-end with a benchmark. The defensible delta must live elsewhere (below).
  • What it explicitly does NOT do — the open territory: (1) No open-vocabulary, learned, transferable concepts. The audio "state" is an implicit latent + per-task free-text stage label trained jointly; there is no reusable is_boiling/became_slippery recognizer transferable across tasks or composable. (2) No planner-level predicates. Nothing enters a symbolic state with preconditions/effects; there is no bi-level planning, no PDDL operator, no logical goal formula — exactly BLADE's contribution. (3) No semantic replanning / failure-correction loop. "Closed-loop" = re-query-with-memory, not detect-violation-and-replan as in REFLECT or Inner Monologue. So the live research wedge is: learn named, transferable, open-vocab audio/multimodal state-change predicates and lift them to a planner — combine HEAR's continuous audio world-model evidence with BLADE-style predicate grounding so a boil/pour cue becomes a planner-usable effect, not a latent that drives one open-loop policy.
  • Contrast on the modality axis: HEAR is the audio analogue of the tactile/force work (Octopi, Tactile-VLA) but goes further on dynamics (a predictive world model over future sound). It complements Audio-VLA / ManiWAV (which it beats as baselines) by adding causal memory + temporal grounding. The pouring-sound thread connects to Making Sense of Audio Vibration and the Sound of Water — but those infer physical properties, not closed-loop control.

  • Lipman et al. 2023 — Flow Matching & Chi et al. — Diffusion Policy — the action-generation backbone (Realizer); Diffusion Policy is also BLADE's low-level controller.

Related papers already in the corpus:

  • BLADE — the anchor: learns named predicates with preconditions/effects and bi-level-plans. HEAR is the audio-grounded, planner-free, implicit-latent counterpoint; the gap between them (transferable audio predicates → planner) is the research wedge.
  • Audio-VLA, ManiWAV, SonicSense — contact/in-the-wild audio in manipulation; ManiWAV is a HEAR baseline it outperforms. HEAR adds causal memory + a predictive audio world model.
  • Making Sense of Audio Vibration (pouring) and The Sound of Water — the pouring-acoustics thread; property inference vs HEAR's closed-loop control.
  • Octopi, Tactile-VLA, See, Hear, and Feel — the other non-visual modalities; HEAR is the audio cousin, distinguished by temporal-dynamics modeling.
  • REFLECT and Inner Monologue — multimodal failure explanation / closed-loop replanning; the semantic closed loop HEAR does not implement (it does reactive re-query-with-memory instead).
  • Kanazawa cooking robot — food-state→PDDL cooking; HEAR's Moka/Boil tasks are the sound-gated, non-symbolic version of the same cooking-state-change problem.
