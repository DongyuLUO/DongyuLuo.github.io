Making Sense of Vision and Touch: Learning Multimodal Representations for Contact-Rich Tasks

Michelle A. Lee, Yuke Zhu, Peter Zachares, Matthew Tan, Krishnan Srinivasan, Silvio Savarese, Li Fei-Fei, Animesh Garg, Jeannette Bohg · Stanford University · 2019 (ICRA; this extended version is the T-RO journal extension) · arXiv:1907.13098 · PDF

One-liner. Self-supervised prediction tasks (optical flow, next end-effector pose, will-it-contact, are-these-streams-time-aligned) fuse RGB, depth, force–torque, and proprioception into one compact latent that a sample-efficient RL policy uses to solve real-robot peg insertion — the canonical demonstration that force/touch is a first-class control modality, not a visual afterthought.

Problem & motivation

Contact-rich tasks (peg insertion, fastening, edge-following) need both vision and touch: vision gives geometry for reaching/pre-shaping, while haptic feedback gives the contact state needed for localization and control under occlusion. Hand-designing controllers that fuse modalities with very different dimensionality, sampling rate (force at 1 kHz vs. RGB at ~30 Hz), and value range is brittle and task-specific. End-to-end RL avoids hand-design but is too sample-inefficient to run on a real robot, and most learned manipulation policies close the loop around a single modality (usually RGB). The paper's bet: decouple representation learning from control — learn a compact multimodal state with self-supervision (no human labels), freeze it, then learn the policy on top with far fewer samples.

Method

The architecture (Fig 2) has four modality encoders feeding a multimodal fusion module, which feeds four self-supervised decoders; the fused latent z (default d=128) is the only thing handed to the policy.

Modality encoders. RGB (128×128×3) → six-layer FlowNet-style CNN; depth (128×128×1) → eighteen-layer VGG-like CNN; force — a 32×6 window of 6-axis F/T readings (a 32 ms window) → 5-layer causal convolutions; proprioception (EE position, orientation, linear + angular velocity) → 4-layer MLP. Each encoder, following a variational formulation, outputs a 2×d Gaussian (mean μ and variance σ²) rather than a point.

Variational fusion (product of experts). Each modality is treated as a conditionally-independent Gaussian observation of z; the modalities are fused by the normalized product of the per-modality Gaussians (Eqn 5), giving one multimodal Gaussian over the latent. Training minimizes the negative ELBO — reconstruction-style log-likelihood of the self-supervised targets plus a KL to an isotropic prior N(0,I), with the reparameterization trick for backprop. This variational design is the key delta from the authors' earlier deterministic ICRA-2019 conference version.

Self-supervised objectives (the supervision is generated automatically, no human labels). Given the next action (encoded by a 2-layer MLP and concatenated to z), four decoders predict: (i) action-conditional optical flow of the scene; (ii) the flow mask (segmentation of the robot in motion); (iii) contact — binary "will the EE make contact next control cycle?"; (iv) the next EE pose. A fifth objective is time-alignment / pairing: a 1-layer MLP classifies whether the sensor streams fed in were time-locked or a randomly shifted (mismatched) mix — forcing the latent to encode co-occurring contact events shared across modalities. Flow / contact ground truth is generated from known robot kinematics and thresholded F/T readings. Six loss terms total (five predictions + KL).

Policy + controller. The frozen representation (its mean is used at policy time) feeds a 2-layer MLP policy trained with TRPO (model-free) to output 3-DoF position displacement Δx + 1-DoF roll Δα at 20 Hz. Freezing the representation drops learnable policy params to ~1.5% of the model. A trajectory generator interpolates these to 500 Hz, an impedance PD controller tracks them, and an operational-space controller emits 1000 Hz torque commands (Fig 3) — compliant, reactive control so the peg can slide on the box surface. A staged reward (hole-search / alignment / insertion / depth) guides exploration.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: peg insertion, four 3D-printed peg shapes (square, triangular, semicircular, hexagonal), ~2 mm clearance. Representation trained on 150k real-robot states + 600k sim states; rollout policy + heuristic policy collect the self-supervised annotations offline.
  • Hardware / simulator: Kuka LBR IIWA (real) and Franka Panda (real, reproducing prior results); both 7-DoF torque-controlled. Kinect v2 RGB-D + wrist-mounted OptoForce 6-axis F/T (mounted between last joint and peg). Simulation uses SAI 2.0 for high-fidelity articulated rigid-body contact and CHAI3D for rendering.
  • Baselines: ablated modality sets (No RGB, No Depth, No Haptics); representation variants (Deterministic model [prior ICRA version], Reconstruction model [VAE reconstructing inputs], No Pairing [drops the time-alignment loss]); latent dimension sweep (d∈{16,64,128,256}).
  • Compute: representation trained 20 epochs on a single Quadro P5000 GPU; 100k real states ~= 90 min to collect; real-robot policy trained 450 episodes ≈ 7 h wall-clock.

Results

Headline: the Full Model completes simulated square-peg insertion 78% of the time; no modality-ablation or representation-ablation reaches it. On the real robot, learned policies hit a similar success regime and the multimodal representations transfer to unseen peg shapes (>70% on hexagonal and square pegs with no further policy training; a new policy on the same frozen representation pushes this to +8% / +10%). Policy recovers from occlusion and physical perturbation.

What the numbers say: (a) every modality matters — No Depth is worst, but No RGB and No Haptics both badly hurt, so vision and touch are complementary not redundant. (b) The time-alignment pairing loss is the single most important objective — dropping it collapses insertion to 22%, the largest drop among representation ablations. (c) The variational model beats the deterministic one by a wide margin (78% vs 24%), and Table I shows the deterministic model's contact/pairing test/train loss ratios blow up (312×, 2514×) — it overfits; the variational ELBO regularizes the latent. (d) Latent dimension is a sweet-spot: too small (d=16) underfits the state, too large (d=256) bloats the policy's state space; both hurt. The Reconstruction (VAE) baseline (36%) loses to action-conditional self-supervision, confirming that predicting dynamics/action-relevant targets beats reconstructing raw inputs for control.

Limitations & open questions

(a) Author-stated. Control is only 4-DoF (Δx + roll), not full 6-DoF pose; extending to 6-DoF position+orientation contact-rich control is future work. Real-robot training faces sensor-synchronization issues, variable sensing-to-control delays, and complex dynamics not present in sim. The latent-dimension trade-off is set empirically; a more principled way to size the representation is open. They flag richer modalities — sound, temperature, proximity — and new self-supervision sources as future directions.

(b) What I noticed reading it. Success is reported as percentage of staged completion over 50 eval rollouts per policy, with the "completed insertion" rate the headline — but there's no variance/CI across seeds on the bar charts (shaded RL curves aside), so the 78% vs 50% gaps are single-run point estimates. The task is a single family (peg-in-hole); generality to qualitatively different contact tasks (screwing, sliding, cable routing) is asserted via 4 peg shapes, not demonstrated. The self-supervised contact label is just a thresholded F/T magnitude — it's a crude binary "is in contact" signal, not a rich predicate (no notion of where or how hard). And the whole thing is on-policy RL (TRPO) per task; the "sample efficiency" win is relative to from-scratch RL, not relative to imitation or to modern offline/diffusion policies. No language anywhere — the task is fixed and reward-specified.

Why I care

This is a foundational pillar for the thesis that drives the 2026-06-24 batch: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — is_inserted, is_grasped, is_screwed_tight, surface_is_rough live in touch and force, not pixels. Lee et al. make exactly this argument empirically: a vision-only state can't solve tight-clearance insertion, and the force stream is what disambiguates the contact state during alignment and insertion. For BLADE, whose visual predicate classifiers gate symbolic preconditions/effects, this is both an opportunity and a warning: the predicates BLADE most wants (is_inserted) are precisely the ones a visual classifier will read unreliably. A natural extension is BLADE-style behavior abstractions whose precondition/effect classifiers ingest a Lee-style fused visuo-tactile latent rather than RGB alone — learning force-grounded predicates. The paper also seeds the key design lesson the batch repeats: action-conditional self-supervision and a cross-modal time-alignment objective are what make fusion work, a precursor to the contrastive/binding losses in UniTouch and ImageBind. It is squarely on-theme — the historical anchor (Cluster F, 2019 classic) for the force/proprioception-policy line; its main gap relative to Weiyu's agenda is the total absence of language and of compositional, long-horizon structure.
