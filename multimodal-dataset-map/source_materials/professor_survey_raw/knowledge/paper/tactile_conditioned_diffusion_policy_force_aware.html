Tactile-Conditioned Diffusion Policy for Force-Aware Robotic Manipulation

Erik Helmut, Niklas Funk, Tim Schneider, Cristiana de Farias, Jan Peters · TU Darmstadt / DFKI / Hessian.AI · 2025 (arXiv preprint, submitted to IEEE) · arXiv:2510.13324 · PDF · project page

One-liner. FARM puts grip force into both the observation and the action space of a diffusion policy — estimating a shear+normal force-distribution image from a GelSight Mini and predicting target grip force jointly with pose and grip width — so the policy can actively regulate contact force rather than letting it fall out as an accident of gripper position commands, all learned from robot-free handheld-UMI demos.

Problem & motivation

Contact-rich manipulation (handling an egg, a grape, a screw) hinges on applying the right grip force throughout the task. Most visuotactile imitation-learning work treats touch as just another observation modality — useful for resolving occlusion or detecting contact, but feeding the policy only through the observation embedding, so the applied force remains an uncontrolled consequence of gripper-width commands. What is missing, the authors argue, is a framework where tactile feedback is not only perceived but explicitly written into the action space, letting the policy target the contact interaction it wants to produce. FARM addresses this gap, and pairs it with a robot-free data-collection paradigm (handheld UMI gripper) to dodge the embodiment gap that teleoperation/kinesthetic methods incur.

Method

Force grounding via FEATS. A GelSight Mini (vision-based tactile sensor) sits on one fingertip; the opposite finger holds a passive gel-pad shell for symmetric contact. Raw GelSight images are passed through FEATS (Helmut et al. 2025, the authors' own finite-element-trained force estimator), which outputs spatial shear (x, y) and normal (z) force distributions. These are packed into a 3-channel 96×96×3 "force image" (one channel per force component, each independently normalized). The total normal force F̂z is also extracted as a scalar by integrating the normal-force field — this scalar is exactly what the deployment controller regulates.

FARM diffusion policy. Built on Chi et al.'s Diffusion Policy (1D temporal CNN with FiLM conditioning, LeRobot implementation). Observation at time t: in-hand RGB (96×96×3), grip width (ArUco-marker distance), the FEATS force image + scalar normal force, and 6-DoF pose (3D position + 6D rotation). Separate ResNet-18 encoders for RGB and the force image. The policy jointly predicts pose, target grip width wd, and target grip force Fdz over a 32-step prediction horizon / 16-step execution horizon, observation horizon 2. The key move: because force and grip width are both conditioning inputs and denoised outputs, the policy can anticipate and regulate the force needed at upcoming steps (Fig 3). Trained with MSE on the denoising noise; DDPM (100 steps) train, DDIM (10 steps) inference.

Dual-mode deployment controller. On the actuated UMI gripper, a controller switches between position control of grip width (free space / approach / release) and closed-loop force control (in contact). It checks both the predicted target force and the FEATS-estimated contact force against a -0.5 N threshold (set from FEATS noise characteristics); if both indicate contact, it runs a PID controller on the force error e = F̂z - Fdz (with anti-windup), converting the correction into a grip-width position command for the DYNAMIXEL motor. Force loop runs at 25 Hz (synced to GelSight); the diffusion policy runs at 7 Hz. Crucially, execution does not wait for force convergence before advancing — a contrast the authors draw against Feel-the-Force.

Hardware: matched handheld + actuated UMI grippers. Demos are collected robot-free with an adapted handheld UMI gripper (RealSense D405 swapped in for the GoPro, OptiTrack motion capture, rigid fingers, GelSight Mini). A purpose-built Actuated UMI gripper (single DYNAMIXEL XL430 motor, belt-driven, spring-return) mirrors the handheld geometry sensor-for-sensor, so policies transfer as a drop-in with no retargeting. Both open-sourced.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: No standard benchmark. 30 expert demos per task (same expert), collected via the handheld UMI gripper, on three custom real-world tasks: plant insertion (high static force), grape picking (low/delicate force), screw tightening (time-dependent, contact-state-detection-heavy).
  • Hardware / simulator: Real Franka Research 3 arm + Actuated UMI gripper, Cartesian impedance control (franky library). GelSight Mini tactile sensor (25 Hz reference clock), Intel RealSense D405, OptiTrack, ArUco markers. No simulator — all real-world.
  • Baselines: Three ablated variants, each itself a diffusion policy sharing RGB + pose + grip-width inputs — (1) Force-Aware: scalar normal force only as tactile input, with force in the action space (dual-mode control); (2) Tactile-Aware: raw GelSight image as input but no target force action (pose + grip width only); (3) Vision-Only: no tactile, binary open/close gripper command. (No external prior-method baseline.)
  • Compute: not reported (training: 60000 iterations per policy; no GPU/wall-clock given).

Results

Headline: success rate over 20 rollouts per task (Fig 5). FARM ties the Force-Aware baseline on the two static-force tasks and dominates on the time-dependent screw task where scalar force is insufficient.

MethodPlant InsertionGrape PickingScrew Tightening
FARM (force image)95%95%100%
Force-Aware (scalar force)95%85%10%
Tactile-Aware (raw image, no force action)65%60%40%
Vision-Only (binary gripper)85%0%0%
  • Force in the action space matters most when force must be delicate. Grape picking: vision-only crushes the grape every time (0%); only explicit force control avoids it.
  • Scalar force is not enough for contact-state detection. On screw tightening the scalar Force-Aware baseline collapses to 10% (loses Allen-key alignment, slips out of the head), while FARM's full force-image hits 100%. This is the paper's central argument for high-dimensional force.
  • Where FARM does not beat the simpler baseline: on plant insertion (95% vs 95%) and grape picking the scalar Force-Aware baseline matches FARM — the authors concede scalar force suffices for "consistent static force demands."
  • Force-domain fidelity (screw task, Fig 6). Wasserstein-1 distance between rollout and demo force distributions: FARM 0.7538 N < Force-Aware 1.6580 N < Tactile-Aware 4.3801 N (under-forces) < Vision-Only 5.0515 N (over-forces). FARM's applied forces track the demonstrations most closely.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Force estimates inherit FEATS model error — deviations possible from FE-model assumptions or cross-sensor generalization.
  • Future work: bimanual manipulation, anthropomorphic hands, and a flow-matching objective to improve policy reactivity.
  • Implicitly: scalar force already saturates the easy (static-force) tasks, so the force-image benefit is demonstrated on only one of three tasks.

What I noticed reading it:

  • N is small and uncontrolled. 20 rollouts/task, 30 demos, one expert, three bespoke tasks, no seeds, no confidence intervals. The 100%-vs-10% screw gap is striking but rests on 20 trials of a single hand-tuned setup; the statistical claim is count-of-success, not a rate with variance.
  • No external baseline. Every comparison is an internal ablation of FARM's own input/action design. There is no head-to-head against Reactive Diffusion Policy or Feel-the-Force, both discussed at length in related work — so "outperforms several baselines" means "outperforms its own degraded variants."
  • The force "action" is really a width correction. The PID on force error ultimately emits a grip-width position command to the DYNAMIXEL; force control is layered on a position actuator, not a true torque interface. Whether this is "force-based control space" or a clever width-modulation scheme is partly semantic.
  • Single-fingertip sensing. Only one finger has a real GelSight; the other is a passive gel shell. Force distribution is therefore one-sided, which may bias the FEATS estimate for asymmetric grasps.
  • Switching threshold is a magic number. The -0.5 N contact threshold is tuned to FEATS noise; robustness to a different sensor / gel wear is untested.

Why I care

This paper is a direct, concrete instance of the thesis underneath this whole 2026-06-24 batch: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — is_screwed_tight is the textbook example, and the paper says so outright ("vision alone cannot determine the screw's state"). Where BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers and stuffs all continuous parameters (grasp pose, force) inside the diffusion-policy body, FARM is exactly the complement: it keeps the policy flat but makes force a first-class, grounded, controllable quantity in both observation and action. The two are orthogonal and composable — FARM is roughly what BLADE's contact-primitive controllers should become if the abstraction layer ever needs to express force-modulated predicates like grip_is_firm or screw_is_tight. BLADE's own "Why I care" flags "force/continuous parameters" as a potential contribution area; FARM is a clean existence proof that force can be folded into a diffusion policy's action space without exotic machinery (just FEATS + a force-image channel + a dual-mode controller).

Most relevant connection: a tactile predicate like screw_is_tight is not just unobservable in RGB — it is a force-trajectory property (resistance rising over time), which is why FARM's scalar-force baseline fails where its force-distribution variant succeeds. That argues that force-aware predicate evaluation in a BLADE-style system needs more than a single scalar; it needs the spatial/temporal force field. A useful pointer for any future "force predicates for bi-level planning" direction.
