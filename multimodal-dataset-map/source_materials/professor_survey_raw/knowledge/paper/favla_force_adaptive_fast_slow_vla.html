FAVLA: A Force-Adaptive Fast-Slow VLA model for Contact-Rich Robotic Manipulation

Yao Li*, Peiyuan Tang*, Wuyang Zhang, Chengyang Zhu, Yifan Duan, Weikai Shi, Xiaodong Zhang, Zijiang Yang, Jianmin Ji, Yanyong Zhang · USTC / Xi'an Jiaotong / Central South University · 2026 (arXiv preprint, Mar 2026) · arXiv:2602.23648 · PDF

One-liner. FAVLA fixes the frequency mismatch in force-aware VLAs by running a slow VLM for semantics at low frequency while a fast flow-matching action expert re-executes at a force-adaptive high frequency — with raw force injected into AE layers via cross-attention and the AE's update rate scheduled by the VLM's own prediction of near-future force volatility — cutting peak contact forces roughly in half on contact-rich industrial tasks.

Problem & motivation

Force/torque feedback helps VLAs on contact-rich manipulation (insertion, tight-tolerance assembly), but most force-aware VLAs concatenate force tokens with visual tokens and run all modalities at a single operating frequency. This causes two failures the paper centers on. (1) Sampling-rate mismatch: cameras run at ~15 Hz while 6-axis force/torque sensors run at 100–1000 Hz; a unified-frequency pipeline downsamples the high-rate force to match the slowest modality, discarding exactly the reactive cues that signal impacts, stick–slip, and force spikes. (2) Open-loop chunk execution: mainstream VLM–action-expert (AE) pipelines run an expensive low-frequency VLM and then execute the predicted action chunk largely open-loop between VLM updates, so corrective responses to contact transitions arrive late — dangerous in high-precision industrial assembly where delayed adjustment causes excessive force and component damage. FAVLA argues semantic understanding is naturally "slow" while contact-corrective control should be "fast," and builds that asymmetry directly into the architecture.

Method

FAVLA is built on the π0 (Black et al. 2024) framework with a PaliGemma backbone: a larger slow VLM backbone plus a smaller fast action expert, shown in Fig 2. The key novelty is a force-injected fast–slow split plus a force-adaptive inference schedule.

Problem setup. At time t the observation is O_t = {I_t} ∪ {s_t} ∪ {f_{t-τ+1:t}, f'_{t-τ+1:t}}: RGB images (external + wrist), a 7-D proprioceptive TCP-pose+gripper state s_t, a history force window f of 6-axis measurements, and the latest force sequence f'. s_t and f' are updated at high frequency; images and history force at low frequency. The policy outputs an action chunk A_t = {a_t, …, a_{t+H-1}}.

Slow VLM backbone. SigLIP encodes the two camera views, Gemma embeds the instruction, and a lightweight Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN) tokenizer encodes the history force f into history force tokens z_f (a 1×1 temporal conv to project each per-step 6-D force vector, then causal dilated temporal blocks with residuals, downsampled to N_f tokens). Vision, language, and z_f are concatenated as prefix tokens for full self-attention; the backbone emits Vision-Language-Force (VLF) prefix tokens and, crucially, caches intermediate-layer key/value states {K, V} as a reusable context (a KV cache) for the fast module.

Fast force-injected action expert. A small transformer that refines action chunks via flow matching, conditioned on the latest force sequence f' and state s_t in addition to the slow VLM KV cache. Rather than feeding the force sequence into the AE's input token stream (which dilutes sparse force cues), FAVLA injects it through a force adapter: the same TCN tokenizer encodes f' into latest force tokens z'_f; then in each AE transformer layer, after self-attention with the cached K/V, the noisy action token z_a cross-attends to z'_f and is updated additively, z'_a = z_a + Attn(Q_a, K_f, V_f) (Eq 2), where K_f, V_f project z'_f and Q_a are the noisy action queries. This lets the AE adjust chunks directly on high-frequency contact signals.

Force variance head. An auxiliary head predicts future volatility of the contact force. From the future force sequence it computes a weighted variance ν_t = Σ_j w_j Var(f_{t:t+W-1}) (Eq 3), EMA-smooths it (Eq 4), and tanh-normalizes it; a VLF token is fed to an MLP head regressed to this normalized target. Training jointly optimizes the slow VLM, fast AE, and variance head with L = L_action + λ L_var (Eq 5, λ=0.1).

Force-adaptive fast–slow inference (Fig 3). Per cycle the VLM does one forward pass to build the KV cache; the AE then runs multiple times on the latest force to generate closed-loop actions. Two designs fix naive multi-run inference: (a) Adaptive inference frequency — using predicted force variance ν˜_t ∈ [0,1], schedule AE executions as n_t = max(1, ⌈ν˜_t · N_max⌉) (Eq 6): run the AE more when contact is imminent/ongoing, at 1× during free motion. (b) Consistent action ensemble — fix the sampled flow-matching noise ε across all AE calls within a visual cycle, and apply temporal ensembling (Zhao et al. 2023) across overlapping chunks to smooth jerky, inconsistent motion.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected real-world demonstrations on four contact-rich tasks — USB Insertion, Gear Assembly (both millimeter-precision), Box Flipping, Board Wiping (both dynamic-contact). 80 teleoperated trajectories per high-precision task, 50 per contact-rich task, collected via 3D SpaceMouse, synchronized and downsampled to a uniform 30 Hz frame rate. No public benchmark is used.
  • Hardware / simulator: "Monte" dual-arm whole-body robot with 7-DoF X-ARM arms; each arm has a wrist-mounted RGB-D camera and a end-effector-flange 6-axis force/torque sensor; one wrist camera + one external fixed camera. Real-robot only (no simulator).
  • Baselines: π0 (vision-only flow-matching VLA), π0+Force (force tokens concatenated to input), TA-VLA (auxiliary future-force-prediction objective), and ForceVLA (MoE force-image fusion). All fine-tuned on FAVLA's dataset via LoRA for fairness.
  • Compute: π0 weights initialized, then LoRA fine-tuning (VLM r=16, α=16; AE r=32, α=32); 30,000 iterations, batch size 8, lr 2.5e-5 cosine decay, single Nvidia A100 80 GB GPU.

Where it wins / ties / loses. Biggest gains on the force-sensitive Gear Assembly (93.3%) and Board Wiping (70.0%). On Box Flipping FAVLA ties TA-VLA and ForceVLA at 80.0% — no improvement there. On USB Insertion FAVLA (80.0%) only slightly edges π0+Force (76.0%).

Force regulation (Table 1). Average peak contact force is the headline safety metric: FAVLA hits 7.7 N on Gear Assembly and 9.9 N on Box Flipping vs ForceVLA's 10.9 N / 12.4 N and π0's 12.0 N / 12.2 N — roughly a 4–type N reduction (4.3 N and 2.3 N below vision-only). Notably π0+Force has higher peak force (13.0/13.8 N) than vision-only, suggesting naive force concatenation can worsen force control.

Ablations (Table 2, Fig 7). Stacking components on a vision-only baseline: Force-Injected AE 50→65% (Box Flip) and 10→60% (Board Wipe); +Force Variance Prediction →70%/60%; +Force-Adaptive Inference →80%/70%. On frequency ratio (Fig 7), fixed n=1,2,4 improves monotonically with frequency, but the adaptive schedule beats all fixed settings (Gear Assembly 93%, USB 80%) while saving compute during free motion. Fig 8 shows the predicted frequency ratio rising just before contact-onset windows.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • The model does not access future force data at inference; future force variance (Fig 8b) is used only as a post-hoc validation metric, so the variance head's reliability rests on training-time supervision generalizing.
  • Naive repeated AE inference produces jerky/unstable motion (the stated motivation for the consistency designs) — a fragility of the multi-run closed-loop scheme.
  • Scope is high-precision industrial contact tasks; no claim of generality beyond the four evaluated tasks.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Small-N success counts, no variance. Evaluation is 25/30/20/10 trials per task (USB/Gear/Box/Wipe) reported as single success rates with no seeds or error bars — Board Wiping's headline 70% is 7/10 trials. The +13.8-point average edge over ForceVLA is not accompanied by any significance test, and one of four tasks (Box Flipping) is a flat tie.
  • arXiv id looks anomalous. The ID 2602.23648 / "Feb 2026" with a "Preprint. March 2, 2026" footer is unusual; I recorded it verbatim as given but flag it for the metadata-verification step before any forward-citation use.
  • Force adapter vs token concat is asserted, not isolated. The claim that cross-attention injection beats concatenation ("dilutes sparse force cues") is plausible but there is no ablation swapping only the injection mechanism against π0+Force under identical other components — the comparison is confounded by the whole fast–slow stack.
  • Adaptive frequency depends on a learned scalar. The entire reactivity story hinges on one regressed variance scalar driving n_t; failure modes when the variance head is wrong (over-triggering AE, or missing a sudden impact) are not characterized.
  • No compute/latency numbers for the actual control loop — for an industrial "fast" reactive claim, achieved AE Hz and end-to-end latency would be the natural thing to report and are absent.

Why I care

FAVLA is squarely on the thesis that drives this batch: many manipulation predicates — is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, is_aligned, surface_is_rough — are not visually evaluable; they live in force/torque, and a vision-only policy is blind to the moment of contact transition. FAVLA's central insight is temporal: semantics is slow, contact correction is fast, and a unified-frequency stack literally throws away the signal. For my line of work this is a clean continuous-control counterpoint to BLADE's discrete abstraction layer. In BLADE I noted that continuous parameters (pour amount, grasp pose, force) sit entirely inside the diffusion policy and that the symbolic layer is purely categorical — a limitation for force-modulated tasks. FAVLA is what that "inside-the-controller" force handling looks like done seriously: force-conditioned flow matching with a contact-volatility-driven replan schedule. The architectural echo is striking: FAVLA's slow-VLM / fast-AE split with replan-on-contact mirrors BLADE's plan/controller split with replan-after-each-behavior, but FAVLA's trigger is a learned force signal rather than a symbolic precondition. A future BLADE extension could use a FAVLA-style force-variance predictor to decide when to re-evaluate a force-grounded precondition like is_inserted — marrying the symbolic body with continuous force-aware execution. This is on-theme, not tangential.
