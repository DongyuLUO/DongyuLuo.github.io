The Sound of Simulation: Learning Multimodal Sim-to-Real Robot Policies with Generative Audio

Renhao Wang, Haoran Geng, Tingle Li, Feishi Wang, Gopala Anumanchipalli, Trevor Darrell, Boyi Li, Pieter Abbeel, Jitendra Malik, Alexei A. Efros · UC Berkeley · CoRL 2025 · arXiv:2507.02864 · PDF · project page

One-liner. Instead of physically simulating sound (which is notoriously hard), MULTIGEN bolts a finetuned video-to-audio diffusion model onto a physics simulator so it can generate realistic pouring audio from simulated video, then trains an audio-visual diffusion policy entirely in sim that transfers zero-shot to real-world pouring — closing the multimodal sim-to-real gap for a non-visual modality without a single real robot trajectory.

Problem & motivation

Sim-to-real has worked mostly for vision-based tasks; multimodal transfer is "largely unrealized." Dynamic, contact-rich behaviors like pouring rely on auditory feedback (occlusion, transparency, and visual ambiguity make liquid flow and fill-level hard to read from pixels alone), but sound is "notoriously hard to model in simulation" — its propagation depends on complex wave dynamics and material interactions, making high-fidelity audio sim expensive and impractical at scale. The two intertwined challenges this paper attacks: (1) simulating a hard-to-model modality, and (2) transferring a multimodal policy sim-to-real. The bet is that a generative model conditioned on simulator state can synthesize the missing sensory stream cheaply and faithfully enough that a policy trained on it generalizes.

Method

MULTIGEN is a hybrid pipeline (Fig 1, Fig 2): a physics-based simulator runs in parallel with a generative model that conditions on simulator output to produce the modality the simulator can't — here, audio. Two main components, then a policy.

1. Generative audio model. Built on MMAudio, a pretrained video-to-audio diffusion transformer (cross-attention over visual / audio / text, trained with conditional flow-matching). Zero-shot MMAudio is inadequate for two reasons the authors identify: it was trained on noisy Foley sounds unlike liquids, and pouring is a long-tail event under-represented in its corpora (so it poorly captures pitch-rises-as-container-fills, viscosity dependence, and transient onset/offset impulses). They finetune on a curated 1031-video pouring dataset drawn from EPIC-Kitchens plus a YouTube crawl (various liquids/containers, 5–60 s clips). To bridge the real-vs-sim visual domain gap, they add a SAMv2 semantic segmentation conditioning pathway: extract per-frame masks over task-relevant classes (cup, mug, water, juice), propagate with SAMv2, and inject a lightweight-CNN segmentation embedding s_t into the transformer via a learned gate (alpha * seg_embed + (1 - alpha) * c_g), preserving the pretrained backbone.

2. Simulation framework. Built on RoboVerse for photorealistic, parallelized, asset-diverse physics sim. Pouring trajectories are motion-planned: each episode has a pouring container (pre-filled to 70%±5%) and a receiving container; sample a grasp within a 5% offset of the container center, move above the receiver, sample pour height, sample one of four discrete pour amounts (one-quarter / one-half / three-quarters / full), compute a deterministic tilt angle, then untilt. Structured domain randomization covers liquid properties (density, viscosity, color, transparency, friction), camera pose, lighting, and textures. Audio generation runs in a second thread off a dedicated frontal virtual camera on the receiving container — in-distribution with the real pouring videos used for finetuning, avoiding occluded/misaligned sim camera views.

3. Policy. A diffusion policy trained from scratch on MULTIGEN's simulated proprioception + vision + audio. RGB encoded by CLIP ViT-B/16; audio encoded by an Audio Spectrogram Transformer (AST) after downsampling 24-bit/192 kHz to 16-bit/16 kHz log-mel (window 400, hop 160, 64 mels). A transformer encoder fuses the streams with attention pooling and CLIP-text language commands ("Pour half the bottle"); the policy outputs 6-DoF Cartesian delta end-effector actions at 10 Hz, converted to 7-DoF via MoveIt IK. No real demonstrations or real robot data are used at any stage.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Finetuning audio dataset = 1031 in-the-wild pouring videos (EPIC-Kitchens + YouTube crawl). Policy eval benchmark = 8 real pouring conditions (4 opaque, 4 transparent containers; Table 1), each scored over 12 evaluations (4 language commands × 3 random locations). Ablation uses 10 real demos for an augmentation baseline and 100 sim trajectories for audio-quality analysis.
  • Hardware / simulator: RoboVerse simulator (with MuJoCo / ManiSkill backends shown). Real: Kinova Gen3 + Robotiq 2F-85 gripper, egocentric Logitech BRIO 4K webcam (monocular RGB), MAONO omnidirectional USB lapel mic on the end-effector (24-bit, 192 kHz).
  • Baselines: Vision-only Diffusion Policy (V) vs. vision+audio Diffusion Policy (V+A); plus a noise-augmentation audio baseline following ManiWAV's protocol (ESC-50 environment noise + robot motor sounds overlaid at 50% probability); plus a zero-shot-vs-finetuned MMAudio ablation.
  • Compute: Audio model finetuned 100K steps (~40 epochs), batch 32, AdamW lr 1e-5. Policy trained 500 epochs, batch 64, AdamW lr 1e-4. GPU type / count / wall-clock not reported.

Results

Headline: zero-shot sim-to-real pouring with average NMAE 0.46 (normalized mean absolute error of poured volume, lower better), and adding audio gives a 23.3% average NMAE reduction over vision-only. Audio helps most on opaque containers (29.4% reduction) vs. transparent (16.2%) — intuitive, since occluded visual feedback is where sound carries the fill-level signal.

Audio-quality ablations (Fig 4): MULTIGEN audio is both more diverse (higher Log-Spectral Distance spread) and more accurate (higher Signal-to-Distortion Ratio) than ManiWAV-style noise augmentation, and policies trained on it scale better with dataset size (lower policy error from 10→1000 trajectories). Zero-shot-vs-finetuned ablation (App B.2) shows finetuning is essential: SDR 7.8→11.3 dB (+45%), LSD 1.95→1.43 (−26.7%), diversity 0.67→1.02 (+52.2%), policy NMAE 0.50→0.37 (−26.0%).

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Still requires projecting real-world conditions into sim (approximate camera placement, correct embodiment) — loose but non-zero manual setup.
  • Limited generalization to extreme container geometries: significantly larger or irregular containers introduce unmodeled dynamics (unexpected sloshing) that degrade performance.
  • Policies don't account for collision avoidance / cluttered environments; real deployment would need obstacle-aware planning.

What I noticed reading it:

  • The whole pipeline is validated on exactly one task family (pouring). The framing ("inject entirely new sensory streams") is general, but every empirical claim is pouring-specific; nothing tests whether a generative audio model transfers to, e.g., insertion clicks or scraping.
  • The generated audio is never grounded against real fluid physics. Fidelity is measured as SDR/LSD against real microphone recordings of pouring, not against the simulator's actual (unsimulated) liquid state. So the policy could be learning to imitate a generative model's plausible sound rather than physically correct flow cues — a subtle distribution-matching vs. causal-correctness gap the paper doesn't probe.
  • NMAE on pour volume is a forgiving metric: a policy that always pours a "median" amount could score moderately without any audio reasoning. No success-rate or per-command breakdown showing the policy actually modulates flow differently across the four pour commands using audio.
  • The audio-quality ablation (Fig 4) is the strongest evidence, but the decisive policy-learning ablation there is on only a 4-condition subset; the main Table 1 has no audio-vs-no-audio paired statistical test (just overlapping std bars over 12 evals).
  • Finetuning data is YouTube + EPIC-Kitchens pouring — real human pouring sounds. So "no real robot data" is true, but the method is far from real-data-free; it leans heavily on a curated real audiovisual corpus.

Why I care

This sits squarely on the batch thesis that many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — is_full during an occluded pour lives in sound, exactly the case audio rescues here (opaque containers benefit 29.4% vs 16.2% transparent). Relative to BLADE: BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers and assumes the relevant state is readable from pixels; this paper is a concrete counterexample where the state estimator must be acoustic. A natural fusion direction is a BLADE-style is_full(container) or fill_level ≥ target predicate whose classifier reads an audio spectrogram, not RGB — predicate grounding in a non-visual modality.

The deeper relevance is methodological, and orthogonal to BLADE's symbolic line: it's a generative-sim-augmentation paper, not an abstraction-learning one. Its real contribution is the "generate the hard-to-simulate modality" recipe, which could in principle supply the training data for force/audio predicate classifiers that a long-horizon planner would compose. I'd file it under audio-as-state-sensing rather than as a planning paper; the bi-level / predicate-invention themes it does not touch.

Most directly comparable batch papers: ManiWAV (the augmentation baseline it beats), Making Sense of Audio Vibration (pouring) (same task, classical audio-for-fill-level), and the SoundSpaces-2 line of physics-based audio sim it argues against.
