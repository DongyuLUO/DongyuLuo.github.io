Touch and Go: Learning from Human-Collected Vision and Touch

Fengyu Yang*, Chenyang Ma*, Jiacheng Zhang, Jing Zhu, Wenzhen Yuan, Andrew Owens · University of Michigan / Carnegie Mellon University · NeurIPS 2022 (Datasets and Benchmarks) · arXiv:2211.12498 · PDF

One-liner. A human-collected, in-the-wild visuo-tactile dataset — people walk through indoor/outdoor scenes pressing a GelSight sensor on objects while recording egocentric video — that is large and diverse enough that self-supervised touch–sight contrastive features beat supervised ImageNet on material recognition and robotic grasping, escaping the narrow "robot-centric" data that prior visuo-tactile work was confined to.

Problem & motivation

Visuo-tactile learning has lagged other multimodal areas because good data is hard to get. The two existing sources are both narrow: (i) robot-collected data (Calandra et al. [8,9], VisGel [42]) is confined to a single lab, limited to small graspable objects, and suffers a chicken-and-egg problem (the robot must already be able to touch the objects it collects from); (ii) simulated data (ObjectFolder [21,22]) can't capture the squishing, deformation, micro-geometry, weaves, and tiny pores of real surfaces, and rigid sim objects don't deform when pressed. The authors argue many aspects of the visuo-tactile world are best learned by observing physical interactions performed by humans: humans reach a far wider range of spaces and objects (chairs, walls, trees, grass, sand, brick) than a robot can, so the captured signals match how objects are encountered in the wild.

Method

The contribution is primarily a dataset plus three demonstration applications; there is no new model architecture, the methods are adaptations of existing ones.

Data collection. Two data collectors (the authors) work in tandem: one presses a GelSight sensor onto objects, the other records an approximately egocentric RGB video of the hand, both feeding one laptop. They walk continuously object-to-object through indoor (classrooms, hallways, apartments) and outdoor (streets, playgrounds, hiking trails) scenes, pressing each object ~3 times, ~0.7 sec per press. GelSight is a vision-based tactile sensor (~1.5 cm, a camera watching a curved elastomer gel with embedded markers under colored lights); the gel deformation encodes surface normal, depth, and shear via photometric stereo. RGB and tactile videos both record at ~27 Hz. To support trimming, they train a binary ResNet-18 "press detector" on 10k hand-labeled GelSight frames (97% test accuracy, chance ~66%) to find frames in-contact, and hire workers (5 per image) to label material categories.

App 1 — self-supervised representation learning. Adapt Contrastive Multiview Coding (CMC, Tian et al. [63]): two encoders zI=fθI(xI) for vision and zT=fθT(xT) for tactile produce L2-normalized embeddings; an InfoNCE loss (Eq. 1, τ=0.07) pulls corresponding visuo-tactile pairs together against K−1 random tactile examples, symmetrized in both directions (Eq. 2).

App 2 — tactile-driven image stylization. A novel task: restyle an image so it "feels like" a given touch signal (e.g., add bumps to a smooth surface to match a rock's tactile reading). Built on contrastive unpaired translation (CUT [53]): an image-translation generator conditioned on a tactile example, trained with a visuo-tactile adversarial loss LVT (Eq. 3, a discriminator distinguishing real vs. shuffled visuo-tactile pairs) plus the structure-preserving patchwise contrastive loss LCUT (Eq. 4: LTDIS=LVT+LCUT).

App 3 — multimodal future touch prediction. Predict the next tactile frame x̂Tt+1 from a window of paired visuo-tactile frames, adapting the correspondence-wise video-prediction net of Geng et al. [23] (ResNet + 3D space-time convolutions, autoregressive multi-frame rollout, L1 + perceptual loss). Visual context acts as an implicit action-conditioning signal (it carries which action is being performed).

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: The Touch and Go dataset itself — ~13.9k detected touches, ~3971 object instances, ~246k visuo-tactile frame pairs, 20 material categories, balanced indoor (52.2%) / outdoor (47.8%). For self-supervised material probing: unlabeled 5172 touches (51.7%), labeled train 3921 (39.2%), labeled test 923 (9.1%), split by video to avoid leakage. Cross-dataset grasping uses the Feeling-of-Success / Calandra et al. [9] dataset (68 objects / 5921 touches train, 16/1204 val, 21/1204 test).
  • Hardware / simulator: GelSight (the [73] robotic-manipulation variant) as the tactile sensor; an ordinary webcam for RGB; one laptop. Real-world, no robot — data collected by humans walking through ~University-of-Michigan-area scenes.
  • Baselines: For representation learning — supervised ImageNet features [15], visuo-tactile CMC trained on ObjectFolder 2.0 [22] and on VisGel [42], and visual CMC (L/ab channels). For stylization — CycleGAN-based variant [78] and the un-stylized input ("baseline"). For future prediction — SVG [16] and Geng et al. [23], each touch-only vs. touch+vision.
  • Compute: CMC trained 240 epochs, batch 128 on 4× Nvidia 2080-Ti GPUs; stylization 100 epochs, batch 8, 4× 2080-Ti; future prediction 30 epochs, batch 8, 4× 2080-Ti.

Stylization (Table 3). Ours beats CycleGAN and the un-stylized baseline on both CMC consistency (0.197 vs 0.178 vs 0.165) and material-classifier agreement (0.142 vs 0.129 vs 0.107); qualitatively makes surfaces rougher/smoother and forms "hybrid" materials (e.g., adding grass) without ever seeing the tactile example's source image.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Data was mainly collected in one geographic location (near U-Michigan), so it may not generalize to other environments.
  • Humans introduce bias differing from robot/virtual data: they may probe unrepresentative object parts and don't press with consistent force.
  • The collector population may not represent the general population (e.g., skin tone), a fairness concern for the egocentric hand imagery.
  • No explicit force sensor — force is captured only implicitly through GelSight deformation, since "explicit force readings are not required for many visuo-tactile tasks."

What I noticed reading it:

  • Static, not contact-rich. Every datapoint is a brief ~0.7 s press onto a surface — there is no grasping, sliding, in-hand manipulation, or force modulation. The dataset captures material/texture perception, not the dynamics of contact-rich interaction, which is what manipulation predicates actually need.
  • No actions, no language. Despite being slotted into a "language + multimodal sensing" batch, the dataset has no language annotations beyond a flat material-category label, and no robot actions. It's an unsupervised representation resource, not a policy dataset.
  • Material labels are noisy by construction. Categories come from crowd workers labeling visual frames (5 per image), and "smooth/rough" labels are assigned from the visual image — so the tactile ground truth for the rough/smooth task is itself vision-derived, a subtle circularity.
  • Press detector quality gates everything. A 97%-accurate ResNet-18 press detector trims the videos; mis-detections silently inject not-in-contact frames into all downstream training, and that error rate isn't propagated into the app-level results.
  • The visuo-tactile gains are real but the linear-probe deltas over ImageNet on the binary hard/soft and rough/smooth tasks are modest (~1–5 pts) given chance is already ~56–66%.

Why I care

Relative to BLADE, this is an adjacent data/representation paper, not a manipulation or abstraction-learning paper — and I want to be honest about that. BLADE's predicates (turned-on(faucet), is_grasped) are learned from robot demos and currently evaluated visually; Touch and Go offers no actions, no language, and no contact-rich interaction, so it does not directly extend BLADE's pipeline.

Where it is relevant to my thesis — that many manipulation predicates (is_inserted, surface_is_rough, is_screwed_tight) live in touch/force rather than pixels — is as the canonical evidence that tactile signal carries material/surface information that vision alone misses and that this information is learnable self-supervised at scale. The surface_is_rough-style predicate is almost literally what the rough/smooth probe measures. So Touch and Go is best filed as the diverse, in-the-wild tactile data foundation that later touch-language and tactile-foundation-model work (TVL, UniTouch, Sparsh) builds on; it's a building block for grounding non-visual predicates, not a method I'd adopt. Its strongest transferable idea for my work is the human-collected, walk-the-world data-collection recipe — a way to get predicate-relevant tactile diversity that a single robot arm can't.
