Beyond Sight: Finetuning Generalist Robot Policies with Heterogeneous Sensors via Language Grounding

Joshua Jones*, Oier Mees*, Carmelo Sferrazza*, Kyle Stachowicz, Pieter Abbeel, Sergey Levine · UC Berkeley (BAIR) · 2025 (arXiv preprint, v3 14 Jan 2025) · arXiv:2501.04693 · PDF · project page

One-liner. FuSe finetunes an off-the-shelf generalist robot policy (Octo or a PaliGemma VLA) onto touch and audio — modalities with no large robot datasets — by adding two auxiliary losses (a CLIP-style cross-modal contrastive loss and a language-generation loss) that use natural language as a shared grounding space, unlocking zero-shot multimodal prompting, compositional cross-modal prompting, and verbal descriptions of touched objects, for +20% success over baselines.

Problem & motivation

Generalist robot policies (Octo, OpenVLA, RT-2, etc.) are trained almost exclusively on vision + proprioception, because that is what large robot datasets like Open X-Embodiment contain — almost none include touch or sound. Yet many manipulation tasks are partially observable from vision alone: reaching into a bag, pressing a button defined by the sound it makes, judging whether an object is squishy. Humans fill these gaps with touch and audio. The core question: how do you retain the internet-scale semantic knowledge of a pre-trained generalist policy while adding heterogeneous sensors for which you only have a small dataset? Naive MSE behavior-cloning finetuning on the extra channels fails — the model keeps relying on its pre-training modalities and ignores the new sensors. The paper's bet is that language is the common interface that can bind a weakly-represented new modality to the policy's existing semantic knowledge.

Method

FuSe tokenizes all heterogeneous modalities — workspace image, wrist image, touch (DIGIT tactile), audio (spectrogram), language — and passes them through a pre-trained transformer backbone with an action head (Fig 2). The recipe adds two auxiliary losses on top of the standard imitation loss; all three are summed during training.

Per-modality encoders. Because the finetuning dataset is small, the tactile channel reuses a pre-trained TVL encoder (itself trained via pairwise vision–language–touch contrastive learning) and finetunes it with the Octo backbone; both DIGIT sensors feed the same TVL encoder. Audio is converted to a spectrogram and treated as a regular image through a ResNet-26 encoder.

1. Multimodal contrastive loss (L_contrast). A CLIP-style contrastive loss that aligns language instructions with observations, built over all modality-based instruction combinations. An observation embedding is formed by re-passing all modalities through the transformer and combining via a multi-head attention layer; a CLIP-style loss is then computed for each instruction×modality combination and averaged. High-level goal: maximize mutual information between different modalities and the semantics of the same scene (e.g., pull “feels squishy” and “feels corded” tactile embeddings apart in the shared space).

2. Multimodal generative / language-generation loss (L_gen). A generative transformer head, added on top of the backbone, predicts the high-level language semantics of a given modality combination via cross-entropy against the appropriate instruction (e.g., observe touch+vision → generate “grab the object that feels soft and looks round”; observe audio → “push the button that plays piano”). A single shared transformer serves all modality combinations, with modality tokens distinguishing inputs.

The final objective is L = L_BC + β L_gen + λ L_contrast, with β = 1, λ = 1 in all experiments.

Language rephrasing. Trajectories are annotated after-the-fact with templated language built from multiple sensor inputs (“the object feels squishy and is red”). To support free-form test-time instructions, the templates are augmented by querying ChatGPT to rephrase while preserving semantics; the rephrasing buffer holds 20 variants per modality combination.

Backbone-agnostic. The same recipe is shown on two very different generalist policies: the transformer diffusion-based Octo (main experiments) and a 3B-parameter PaliGemma VLA. For the VLA, the generative language loss folds directly into the existing language-model head rather than needing a separate generative head.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: A newly collected real-world multi-task dataset of 27K (text says ~26,866) robot trajectories spanning vision, touch, audio, proprioception (9-DoF IMU), and language instructions across three tasks (tabletop grasping, shopping bag, button pressing). First robot dataset of its kind to include touch + audio with robot action data. All data, code, and models open-sourced.
  • Hardware / simulator: WidowX 250 6-DoF arm (real robot only, no sim); delta-EE-position control at 5 Hz. Third-person RGB (640×480), wrist RGB, two DIGIT tactile sensors on the gripper fingers (320×240, with a static background subtracted), one microphone (1 s of 44.1 kHz samples → spectrogram), 9-DoF IMU. Six sound-making buttons in the button task.
  • Baselines: Octo finetuned vision-only (Octo Vision FT); Octo trained from scratch on the multimodal data (Octo Scratch); ResNet-from-scratch with FiLM language conditioning (ResNet Scratch); plus ablations (no generative loss, no CLIP loss, no auxiliary losses). For the VLA: PaliGemma FuSe vs its Octo-FuSe counterpart.
  • Compute: All models trained 50,000 steps on a v5e-128 TPU pod, batch size 1024, cosine LR schedule with 2000 warmup steps, peak LR 3×10-4.

Results

Headline: FuSe increases success rates by over 20% versus all considered baselines, averaged across tasks (Fig 5). The largest gap is on the shopping bag task — the most visually partially observable setting (occlusions + poor wrist-camera lighting once the gripper enters the bag) — where vision-only finetuning is “considerably inferior” because visual features stop being discriminative. Octo-from-scratch and ResNet-from-scratch both fall well short, confirming the pre-trained-policy initialization is necessary given the small dataset.

Multimodal prompting (Table I). On objects that are ambiguous in one modality but unique in another, adding the complementary modality lifts success. On vision-ambiguous objects, Visual+Tactile beats Visual alone on average (Reach 0.53 vs 0.37, Grasp 0.37 vs 0.34); on touch-ambiguous objects, Visual+Tactile beats Tactile alone (Reach 0.45 vs 0.38, Grasp 0.35 vs 0.35).

Compositional cross-modal prompting (Fig 6). On a simple task (“grab the object the same color as the button that plays piano”) FuSe beats all baselines. On a multi-step task that chains two subtasks through the generative head (press a button visually → feed the resulting sound back → press the button that plays the same sound), FuSe beats Octo-with-all-sensors-and-losses-from-scratch, especially on full task completion; the from-scratch model shows poor language grounding and fails to fulfill the audio-based instruction.

Ablation (Fig 7). On the shopping-bag task, including both auxiliary losses outperforms either-one-alone or neither; performance deteriorates most on unseen test objects when the losses are dropped — the losses are key to actually exploiting the new sensors.

VLA generalization (Fig 8). The 3B PaliGemma FuSe VLA is competitive with the Octo-based FuSe and outperforms it on the hard shopping-bag task — claimed as the first open-source VLA finetuned on heterogeneous (non-visual) sensory inputs.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Training with additional modalities increases training resource cost, which currently caps the observation history at 0.4 s — too short to reason well about sparse signals like tactile data. More training efficiency / longer context is flagged as future work.
  • OXE pre-training generally improves low-level grasping vs original PaliGemma weights, but over-training on OXE during pre-training harms the policy's language understanding — a delicate pre-training balance.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Statistics are thin. Each scenario is run for only 5 rollouts; Table I numbers are averages over very small N (the per-cell values like 0.43, 0.5 imply ~7–ish trials). The headline “+20%” is an average over noisy small-sample tasks — no confidence intervals or seeds reported, unlike a cleaner success-rate-with-seeds protocol.
  • The tactile encoder is borrowed, not trained here. FuSe leans on the pre-trained TVL encoder for touch. So the “language grounds touch” claim partly rides on TVL's prior vision–language–touch alignment; how much of the gain is the FuSe losses vs the inherited TVL encoder is not cleanly disentangled.
  • Audio is shallow. 1 s spectrogram through a ResNet, used mostly to identify which button plays which sound — a classification-flavored use of audio, not contact-event / vibration reasoning. The harder “continuous acoustic state” question (pour fullness, scraping, screwing tight) is untouched.
  • No symbolic / predicate layer. Everything is implicit in the policy + language head; there is no explicit is_squishy(x) predicate the model can plan over. The cross-modal “reasoning” is end-to-end and one-shot, not a compositional planner.
  • Real-robot only, single embodiment (WidowX) — the cross-embodiment generalization that motivates generalist policies isn't tested for the new sensors.

Why I care

This paper is a direct, concrete instance of the batch's big thesis: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — feels_squishy, feels_corded, plays_piano (an acoustic property), is_grasped-but-occluded-in-bag all live in touch/sound, not pixels. FuSe shows that you can graft those non-visual senses onto a pre-trained generalist policy cheaply by using language as the binding glue. That is the mirror image of BLADE's program: BLADE learns explicit visual predicate classifiers and composes them with a symbolic planner; FuSe learns an implicit multimodal–language alignment and prompts it zero-shot. The interesting research gap sits exactly between them: BLADE's predicate-classifier layer is purely visual today (its own “Why I care” flags force/continuous parameters as a missing contribution area). FuSe's tactile/audio grounding is precisely the perceptual substrate a BLADE-style predicate like is_inserted or surface_is_rough would need — but FuSe stops short of giving it a symbolic, plannable form. A natural follow-up to my own line: learn tactile/acoustic predicate classifiers (FuSe-style language-grounded encoders) and feed them into BLADE-style abstract operators so the planner can reason over non-visual state. FuSe also strengthens the “language as the universal cross-modal interface” view that BLADE's LLM-translation step already relies on.

This is squarely on-theme (learning from language, sensory grounding, manipulation), with the caveat that it is a scale/implicit-policy paper, not a planning-abstractions paper — the complement to BLADE rather than a peer method.
