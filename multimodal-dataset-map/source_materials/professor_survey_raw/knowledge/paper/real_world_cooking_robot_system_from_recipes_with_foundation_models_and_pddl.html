Real-World Cooking Robot System from Recipes Based on Food State Recognition Using Foundation Models and PDDL

Naoaki Kanazawa, Kento Kawaharazuka, Yoshiki Obinata, Kei Okada, Masayuki Inaba · University of Tokyo (JSK Lab) · Advanced Robotics 2024 · arXiv:2410.02874 · PDF

One-liner. An end-to-end PR2 cooking system that takes a natural-language recipe, converts it to a sequence of robot-interpretable cooking functions via few-shot GPT-4 prompting, complements the omitted preparatory actions through classical PDDL planning over 17 hand-defined operators, and uses a CLIP-linear-probe food-state classifier trained from 3 demonstrations to time heat-driven state transitions — demonstrated on two arranged (unseen) recipes (butter sunny-side-up, boiled-and-sautéed broccoli) executed in a real kitchen with predefined motion trajectories.

Problem & motivation

Cooking is a canonical long-horizon manipulation domain that the field has chipped at for years (Beetz, Bollini, Paulius and collaborators are all cited). Two specific gaps motivate this work:

  • Real-world executable action planning. Recipes written for humans omit the preparatory steps people do unconsciously — holding the bowl before pouring, walking to the tap to fetch water, placing a pan on the stove before igniting it. A robot needs those steps filled in, conditioned on its current state and the kitchen layout.
  • Foodstuff state-change recognition. Recipes are riddled with state-conditioned instructions ("heat until it boils", "pour the eggs when the butter is melted"). Prior CNN-based state classifiers need large dedicated datasets and target identification after-the-fact; the authors want real-time recognition with very little training data per state.

The contribution is the integrated system: LLM (GPT-4) converts recipe → function sequence; PDDL planner fills gaps; CLIP linear-probe handles state timing; PR2 executes on real hardware with predefined motion trajectories.

Method

Cooking function vocabulary (Section 2). 10 typed cooking functions are defined as the LLM's target representation: pour, mix, turn-on-stove, set-stove, turn-off-stove, stir, heat, cook, boil, stir-fry. Arguments are typed (ingredient, vessel, tool, mixture, state).

Stage 1 — LLM recipe parsing (Section 3.1). GPT-4-0613 is few-shot prompted with three known recipe↔function-sequence pairs (sunny-side-up, poached egg, scrambled egg, all hand-annotated). It converts a new natural-language recipe into a cooking-function sequence. No fine-tuning; the prompt is the entire interface.

Stage 2 — PDDL planning to fill omitted actions (Section 3.2). The PDDL domain extends the 10 cooking functions with 7 basic actions for a total of 17 PDDL operators: hold, place, move-to, open-tap, close-tap, transfer, fetch-water. Types include object (ingredient/vessel/tool/mixture), spot (a location — "stove", "kitchen", "sink"), arm ("arm1"/"arm2", abstract rather than left/right). Each function is rule-based-translated into a target predicate state with default conditions (e.g., the robot's hands are empty before/after). Classical planning runs step-by-step across the sequence, with the final state of step k becoming the start state of step k+1, generating the fully-elaborated action procedure.

Stage 3 — Food state recognition via linear-probe CLIP (Section 4). For each heat-driven state transition (butter melting, water boiling, egg cooking), a human annotator marks the time of change in a short time-series; frames before the annotation are labeled 0 (pre-change), frames after are labeled 1 (post-change). A linear probe is trained on CLIP's image-encoder features. At inference time the model runs at 10 Hz on a cropped "gazing area" (only the pan/pot contents, not the whole pan) and the first frame labeled 1 marks the state change. Crucially: only 3 time-series per state are used for training.

Hardware. PR2 dual-arm wheeled robot, IH stove + water tap real kitchen. Motion execution is predefined trajectories created by direct teach (not learned). Cooking functions and basic actions are tied to hand-coded motion templates.

Setup

  • Known recipes (in-distribution): sunny-side-up, poached egg, scrambled egg — each hand-annotated with a target cooking-function sequence; used as the few-shot examples.
  • Unknown recipes (out-of-distribution test): butter-arranged sunny-side-up (uses only known ingredients but a new cooking flow), and boiled-and-sautéed broccoli (introduces a new ingredient, broccoli, while staying within the cooking-function vocabulary).
  • LLMs compared (Stage 1 ablation): GPT-4-0613, GPT-4o-2024-05-13, GPT-4o-mini-2024-07-18, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5 Flash. GPT-4-0613 is used for the main system.
  • State recognition data: 4 time-series per state (butter melting, water boiling, egg cooking), at 10 Hz; 3 for training, 1 for validation. Compared 1-data vs. 3-data training, and "gazing area = contents only" vs. "gazing area = entire pan".
  • Hardware / simulator: Real PR2 robot in a real kitchen environment with IH stove, water tap, frying pan, pot, small bowl, measuring cup, wooden spatula, spoon, whisk.
  • Compute: not reported.

Results

LLM recipe-to-function conversion (Fig. 7–8). All five LLMs produced the cooking-function sequence for boiled-and-sautéed broccoli; differences were in state-argument wording ("broccoli" vs. "broccoli-cooked") and minor missing/extra parts, not fatal planning failures. A separate ablation on prompt partitioning (Fig. 8) showed that fewer partitions of the recipe degrade LLM interpretation: with one fewer partition (b), the tool argument of the final stir-fry was wrong; with two fewer (c), pour(broccoli, pot) dropped entirely and the next function was misinterpreted as cook.

PDDL action procedure planning (Fig. 9–12). For all three known recipes, classical planning successfully complemented the basic actions (moving, holding/placing, fetching water, igniting and extinguishing stove) from a "no-ingredient-placed" start state. With ingredients pre-placed in the kitchen "spot" the plans were as expected, with appropriate extra movement steps. No quantitative success rate is reported — the evaluation is qualitative figure-by-figure inspection of the generated plans.

Real-robot cooking (Section 5.4, Figs. 19–22). PR2 autonomously executed both unknown recipes end-to-end: butter-arranged sunny-side-up (11 action steps shown in Fig. 19) and boiled-and-sautéed broccoli (20 action steps in Fig. 21). For sunny-side-up, the state classifier mis-detected the egg as cooked immediately because the pan was hot when the eggs were poured — but after the eggs were transferred to a plate the dish was visually well-cooked. For broccoli the butter melting recognition was a bit early ("butter was not yet completely melted when the robot judged"). The dishes were "tasty and human-eatable by eating them" (Fig. 22 shows the plated dishes). Success is reported as a qualitative N-of-1 demonstration per recipe, not as a multi-trial success rate.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Recipe coverage is limited by the 10-function vocabulary and 17 PDDL actions; more recipes require more functions, and they suggest using LLM error-feedback re-planning (citing LLM+P-style work) to scale.
  • Maintaining object-argument continuity across long recipe steps is fragile — they propose expressing arguments as indexed variables and graph-checking the function expression for validity.
  • State recognition is brittle when the same state has no training data; generalization-of-state-change research (their own follow-up Kawaharazuka et al. 2024; not yet ingested) is flagged as the fix.
  • Beyond visual state, future cooking robots will need tactile (texture, burning), olfactory (smell), and gustatory (taste) sensing.
  • Motion execution is predefined trajectories — they call out imitation learning (Diffusion Policy [34]) plus VLM-based perception as the next step toward adaptive motion.

What I noticed reading it:

  • No quantitative system-level success metric. The real-robot evaluation is two completed cooks, not a 20-trial success rate. Subsystem ablations (LLM, gazing area, training-data size) are quantified, but the integrated system is shown by figure-and-narrative. Honest for a systems paper, but a sharp contrast with BLADE's 20 initial states × 3 seeds protocol.
  • The state classifier had clear miscalls in the real run (egg "cooked" immediately, butter melted prematurely) but the dish was still edible — suggesting the recipe's downstream tolerance, not the classifier's accuracy, is what made it work. This is exactly the kind of system-level robustness that pure-benchmark numbers miss.
  • Vocabulary is closed and hand-engineered. The 10 cooking functions, 7 basic actions, type system, and PDDL operator schemas are all hand-written. The LLM only fills in the recipe parse — it doesn't propose new operators, unlike BLADE or InterPreT. The price of "applied and shipping" is "everything must be specified up front".
  • Motion is hand-taught. Direct-teach predefined trajectories per action; no learned low-level policies. The entire foundation-model layer sits above motion, not within it — an interesting decoupling.
  • Single-LLM choice for the system. GPT-4-0613 is used despite the prompt-only ablation showing other LLMs also work; no sensitivity analysis is run on the deployed system to LLM variance.

Why I care

This is the "what does the LLM+VLM+PDDL stack look like when it actually has to ship?" counterpoint to research-prototype work like BLADE. The contrast is illuminating in both directions:

  • What an applied system gives up. Closed vocabulary, hand-engineered PDDL operators, predefined motion, qualitative evaluation. No predicate invention, no operator learning, no learned low-level controllers. The cost of robustness is generality.
  • What an applied system buys. A real PR2 cooking real eggs and broccoli in a real kitchen from a recipe the system has never seen. Few-shot LLM prompting, 3-shot CLIP linear probe, hand-written PDDL — minimal data, maximal engineering, ships.
  • The eval-protocol gap is the most useful finding. BLADE evaluates a research prototype on 20 initial states × 3 seeds in CALVIN and reports mean ± std. This paper cooks two dishes once and shows the photos. Both are honest; they're measuring different things. The question is whether BLADE-style abstraction learning could survive the constraints (closed vocabulary, predefined motion, real kitchen) that this system accepts as given.
  • Bridge to predecessor work. Sits in the same lineage as Paulius et al.'s recipe-to-task-tree work but adds the LLM front-end and PDDL back-end — worth comparing on coverage vs. brittleness.
  • State recognition as the bottleneck. The real-run miscalls suggest the visual-only state classifier is the system's weakest link — an opportunity for tactile/olfactory multimodal state estimation, possibly with the kind of foundation-model-based perception that the BLADE line of work could extend into.

Related

  • From Cooking Recipes to Robot Task Trees — predecessor in the recipe-to-robot line; uses a Functional Object-Oriented Network rather than LLM+PDDL. This paper updates the front-end to a foundation model.
  • LLM+P — same LLM-translates-to-PDDL pattern but for general planning domains; the authors explicitly cite LLM+P-style error-feedback re-planning as their proposed fix for recipe-complexity scaling.
  • LLMs as Planning Formalizers (survey) — surveys the LLM-to-PDDL-translation landscape this paper sits in; useful for situating the design choice of "LLM parses recipe, classical planner does the planning".
  • BLADE — the research-prototype counterpoint: learns PDDL operators automatically from demos rather than hand-defining them, learns predicate classifiers and diffusion-policy controllers end-to-end, evaluates on 20 initial states × 3 seeds. Same overall stack (LLM + PDDL + VLM/classifier), opposite trade-off.
  • InterPreT — learns the predicates themselves from language feedback; addresses the "closed vocabulary" weakness this paper accepts.
