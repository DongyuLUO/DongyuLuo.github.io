That Sounds Right: Auditory Self-Supervision for Dynamic Robot Manipulation

Abitha Thankaraj, Lerrel Pinto (NYU) · 2022 · arXiv preprint · arXiv · PDF

One-liner. AURL shows that contact sound alone — recorded by cheap contact mics and compressed by a BYOL-style self-supervised encoder — is enough to predict the motion-primitive parameters of fast, contact-rich behaviors (swatting, striking, rattling), with self-supervision cutting prediction error far below supervised-from-scratch or pretrained-vision baselines in the low-data regime.

Problem & motivation

Most robot manipulation policies are built on visual observations, which carry rich spatial information but miss the high-frequency temporal signal that dynamic, contact-rich tasks (a tennis strike, cracking an egg, swatting a fly) produce. Tactile sensing captures contact but is too delicate for large-scale data collection. Sound is an "often ignored" third channel: a contact microphone gives a high-frequency, direct readout of what just happened at the moment of impact, and humans demonstrably use audition as a success signal in motor learning (the paper cites neuroscience on rowing/sonification). The question the paper poses: can audio be leveraged to learn dynamic manipulation skills on its own, without vision or multimodal fusion? The blocker to using raw audio directly is data hunger — reasoning over raw waveforms needs large datasets — so the paper attacks that with self-supervised representation learning.

Method

AURL frames each task as a dataset D = {o_k, a_k} of audio observations o_k paired with action parameters a_k, and learns a policy a_k = f(o_k) that predicts the action from sound. The pipeline (Fig 3) is three stages.

Audio preprocessing. Each 4-second clip is recorded at 44.1 kHz, padded/clipped to fixed length, downsampled to 11 kHz, then converted to a mel spectrogram (STFT window 400, hop 160, 16 mel banks; mel chosen over raw STFT / log-mel from initial experiments). Crucially, each contact microphone is treated as a separate channel, so a multi-mic recording becomes a multi-channel 2D "image" that a CNN can ingest. Tasks use 1 or 2 mics depending on whether spatial position of contact matters.

Self-supervised representation learning. A ResNet-18 encoder (input channels = number of mics) is trained with BYOL (Bootstrap Your Own Latent): online + target networks, target as slow EMA of online, objective = minimize distance between representations of two augmented views of the same audio. The latent z_k is fixed at length 512. The key augmentation finding: random resize+crop on the mel spectrogram works; Mixup (the standard BYOL-A audio augmentation) hurts, because mixing waveforms destroys the temporal structure that encodes the behavior. Trained 1000 epochs, LARS optimizer, lr 0.2, batch 1024.

Audio-to-behavior finetuning. Following the linear-probe convention from SSL vision, a linear model w_θ maps frozen z_k to action parameters by minimizing squared loss Σ ‖w_θᵀ z_k − a_k‖². High-capacity encoder for representations, low-capacity linear head to avoid overfitting on the modest dataset.

Self-supervision variants. Because they control data generation, the authors can build invariances into the data instead of only via augmentation: AURL+Act treats two audio clips from the same behavior as a positive pair (5 repeats per behavior were collected for exactly this); AURL+AA combines that with augmentations; AURL+All pretrains one encoder across all five tasks' audio jointly.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected audio-behavior dataset, 25,000 datapoints across five dynamic tasks — rattling a rattle, shaking a tambourine, swatting a fly swatter, striking a horizontal surface, striking a vertical surface. 1,000 unique behaviors × 5 repeats = 5,000 per task; per-task split 4,000 train / 1,000 test. Action parameters sampled uniformly from a parameterized primitive space; tasks are self-resetting for minimal supervision. RGB video also recorded (third-person) for the vision baseline.
  • Hardware / simulator: UR10 arm, joint-velocity control, dynamic behaviors via motion primitives (velocity/acceleration/oscillation-count, etc., per task). Commodity contact microphones (1–2 per task) on the tool or in the environment; audio at 44.1 kHz, 4 s clips. Deformable tools used for safety.
  • Baselines: Random (lower bound), Oracle (nearest-train-action by L2, rough upper bound), Supervised (ResNet+linear from scratch, MSE), Supervised+Aug, Pretrained-audio (data2vec features + linear probe), Pretrained-vision (5 frames through ImageNet ResNet + linear probe). AURL variants: AURL, AURL+Act, AURL+AA, AURL+All.
  • Compute: not reported (encoder: 1000 epochs, batch 1024, LARS; only that the vision baseline was capped at 5 frames because more frames were too compute-heavy).

Results

Two metrics: MSE on predicted behavior parameters (Table I, offline) and normalized DTW distance between desired vs. produced amplitude envelopes on real UR10 rollouts (Table II, online closed-loop "make-this-sound" evaluation). Headline: self-supervised pretraining gives 34.5% lower MSE than plain supervised learning and 54.3% lower than visual training, and online rollouts beat supervised by ~11.5% on audio similarity. Pretrained audio (data2vec) and pretrained vision both do poorly — speech/music pretraining is out-of-distribution for contact sound, and 5-frame vision lacks temporal resolution. Where it loses / only ties: on Striking-Horizontal and Shaking, AURL is only marginally better than supervised and well short of Oracle.

Notable findings from the ablations: (1) Supervised+Aug is often worse than plain supervised (e.g. 2.37 vs 0.25 on Rattling) — augmentations only help when the SSL objective extracts invariance from them, not when fed naively. (2) Scaling (Fig 5): SSL's advantage is largest in the low-data regime and shrinks as data grows — authors conjecture plain supervision may suffice at the data limit. (3) Task-specific > multi-task pretraining: AURL+All only beats single-task on 1/5 tasks, because striking tasks (sparse sharp spikes) and rattle/shake/swat (dense spikes) have very different spectrogram distributions. (4) Contact mics beat standard mics for ambient-noise rejection.

Limitations & open questions

(a) Author-stated. Only mild gains over supervised on Striking-Horizontal and Shaking, and far from Oracle on those — better SSL or large-scale audio pretraining needed. The framework learns only from a fixed labeled (self-collected) dataset, so it can only reproduce behaviors present in training; they suggest RL-based data collection to go task-directed.

(b) What I noticed reading it. (1) No success-rate or task-completion metric. Everything is MSE on action parameters or DTW envelope distance — proxies. There is no "did the swat actually land / did the strike succeed" number, and no human-rated audio similarity beyond DTW, so the 11.5% online improvement is on a hand-built metric, not task success. (2) The action space is the real target, and it's low-dimensional. AURL predicts a handful of primitive parameters (joint velocities, accelerations, oscillation counts), not closed-loop motor control — so "learning dynamic behavior from sound" is really "regressing primitive parameters from a single post-hoc sound clip." It is open-loop: sound is consumed after the action, used to map a desired sound to an action, not as feedback during execution. (3) Statistics are thin — single split per task, no seeds/variance/CIs reported on Tables I–II, and several AURL-vs-baseline gaps (Striking-H/V) are within plausible noise. (4) The DTW metric normalizes against same-action variance, which can flatter methods on noisy tasks; Shaking has high distances across the board (Oracle 7.61), suggesting the metric or task is poorly conditioned there. (5) Generalization to new objects/tasks/mic placements is untested — encoders are per-task and the input channel count is hard-wired to mic count.

Why I care

This is squarely on-theme for the thesis that many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — is_struck_hard_enough, contact_made, is_rattling live in the high-frequency acoustic signal, not in pixels. AURL is an early, clean existence proof that a contact-sound channel carries enough information to recover the parameters of a contact event, which is exactly the kind of signal a non-visual predicate classifier would need. For BLADE, where predicate classifiers are currently visual, this is suggestive of an acoustic predicate head: a self-supervised audio encoder feeding a light classifier could ground symbols like is_inserted or is_screwed_tight that vision cannot read. The honest caveat is altitude: AURL is a single-step, open-loop primitive-parameter regressor with no language, no planning, and no compositional structure — it sits far below the long-horizon / TAMP layer BLADE operates at. Its contribution to my line of work is the sensor-and-representation argument (sound + BYOL on mel spectrograms, task-specific, low-data), not a policy or planning architecture. Related directly to the audio-imitation work it cites (Play it by Ear) and the contact-audio sensing line.
