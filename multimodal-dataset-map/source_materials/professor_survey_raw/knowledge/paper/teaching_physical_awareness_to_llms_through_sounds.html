Teaching Physical Awareness to LLMs through Sounds

Weiguo Wang, Andy Nie, Wenrui Zhou, Yi Kai, Chengchen Hu · NIO / Peking University · ICML 2025 (PMLR 267) · arXiv:2506.08524 · PDF

One-liner. ACORN teaches an audio-LLM to reason about the physics of how sound propagated — Doppler shift, multipath/reverberation, direction-of-arrival, line-of-sight, range — rather than just what made the sound, by training a phase-aware audio encoder on a million-example QA dataset whose audio is synthesized by convolving real sound sources with a controllable physical-channel simulator.

Problem & motivation

Current audio-LLMs (Pengi, LTU, Qwen-Audio) recognize and describe sound content but lack physical awareness: they cannot tell from the raw signal whether a vehicle is approaching or receding (Doppler), whether the speaker is inside or outside an enclosure (line-of-sight / multipath), or where a sound came from (direction). The authors motivate this with a safety case — a voice-controlled car accepting Open the Window from outside the vehicle because it cannot discern the sound's physical origin. The core obstacle is data: large-scale physical-phenomenon audio is impractical to record across many environments and nearly impossible to annotate by hand (a human cannot label a Doppler shift or reverberation time without instruments). Their key insight: the received signal decomposes as y = h ⊗ s, a sound source s convolved with a physical-channel impulse response h (Eq. 1), so realistic physically-annotated audio can be synthesized by convolving existing source clips with simulated channels whose ground-truth physics is known by construction.

Method

Channel simulator (the data engine). A signal-processing channel simulator models five independently-controllable components of the channel impulse response (CIR): (1) LOS path + early reflections (delta functions at path delays, Eq. 4), (2) reverberation tail (frequency-subband Gaussian noise with per-band decay envelopes, Eq. 5), (3) Doppler effect (a time-varying delay τ(t) = (d0+v·t)/c, Eq. for h(t,τ)), (4) microphone-array reception (per-channel TDoA from direction θ, Eq. 6), and (5) parameter randomization of all non-target components. Sources are drawn from AudioSet (~2M 10-sec clips); each is convolved with a task-configured channel (Table 1 specifies, per task, which components are the controlled target T, randomized R, enabled, or disabled). This component-wise "simulate the whole life cycle of the sound wave" approach is contrasted with prior CIR work that reconstructs specific geometric environments.

AQA-PHY dataset. 1M <Audio, Question, Answer> tuples (Fig. 4 workflow). Closed-form QA (binary classification, multi-class, numeric estimation) from fixed templates; open-form QA (natural-language physics explanations) generated with ChatGPT-4. Per task: 200k closed-form + 10k open-form.

Phase-aware audio encoder (the architecture contribution). Unlike content-focused encoders that use magnitude only, ACORN's encoder ingests three STFT-derived channels: log-magnitude, sin(phase), and cos(phase) (sin/cos used to avoid the π→−π wraparound discontinuity, Eq. 3). The argument: physical effects live in subtle phase relationships magnitude-only encoders discard. Each of the three components passes a 3×3 1D conv (128→1280 channels) + GELU, the three are stacked (3840 channels), fused by two 3×3 convs to 1280, given sinusoidal positional embeddings, and processed by a 32-layer transformer to produce audio tokens. The magnitude subnetwork is initialized from Whisper-large-v2; the phase subnetwork is trained from scratch.

LLM coupling. Audio tokens are linearly projected to the word-embedding size, wrapped in <soa>/<eoa> tokens, concatenated with text tokens, and fed to the LLM (Llama3.1-8B or Qwen2-7B). Encoder + LLM trained jointly with next-token loss on the answer; LLM fine-tuned with LoRA.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: AQA-PHY (self-built, 1M tuples) across five tasks — LOS Detection, Doppler Estimation, DoA Estimation, Multipath Analysis, Range Estimation. Sound sources from AudioSet. Real-world eval: NIO ES6 vehicle cabin, LOS detection + DoA, zero-shot (trained on simulated only).
  • Hardware / simulator: custom DSP channel simulator (no robot; perception-only). Real-world rig: four omni-directional microphones in a vehicle cabin (two front, two back, left/right per pair), 16 kHz, data-acquisition board. Training on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, batch size 32, 7 epochs, ~61 hours total.
  • Baselines: Whisper-large-v2 encoder (magnitude-only, 0.63B params) vs. ACORN encoder (0.65B params), each paired with both Llama3.1-8B and Qwen2-7B; plus a task-specific random baseline. Comparison is encoder-vs-encoder under matched LLMs.
  • Compute: 4× A100, batch 32, 7 epochs, ~61 h training.

  • The phase-aware encoder beats the Whisper magnitude-only baseline on every metric: +5.3 pts BCA on LOS (Llama), 74.5% lower DoA MAE (1.423 vs 5.585, Llama), and a ~7× reduction in range REP (1.764 vs 12.572, Llama). The paper credits the phase channels for this.
  • Improvements hold across both Llama and Qwen, argued as evidence the encoder gain is model-agnostic.
  • Open-form QA (Qwen2-7B): the model gives natural-language physics explanations; for multi-step tasks it first extracts measurements then applies formulas (reported DoA error 6.505°, range error 0.472 m in the merged open-QA setting).
  • Ablations: LOS presence lowers multipath TCA (strong direct path masks subtle echoes); Doppler slightly lowers LOS BCA (signal distortion); range accuracy improves monotonically with SNR (MAE 5.64→0.91 samples from <10 dB to >40 dB, Llama). Degradations are small — argued as robustness for real environments where effects co-occur.
  • Real world (zero-shot, vehicle): LOS interior/exterior detection 0.845 (Llama) / 0.870 (Qwen); in-cabin left/right DoA 0.8975 (Llama) / 0.925 (Qwen). Degraded vs simulation but functional sim-to-real.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Single-turn only; multi-turn conversational reasoning is future work.
  • Model evaluates direct outputs; no "internal thought" / chain-of-thought-style internalization yet (they cite o1 / DeepSeek-R1 as inspiration for response conditioning).
  • Real-world experiments are limited in scope and scale; more extensive testing needed.

What I noticed reading it:

  • All "physical awareness" is passive perception of propagation physics, not of object state. The model infers properties of the channel (geometry, motion, reflectivity), not of the sound's source changing state. No notion of a process evolving over time toward a threshold ("coming to a boil").
  • Generalization rests entirely on the channel simulator's fidelity. Because both train and test audio are synthesized the same way, the strong table numbers may overstate real-world transfer; the much smaller, mostly-binary real-world results are the honest signal.
  • No closed loop: outputs are one-shot answers to questions; nothing acts on the inference or corrects behavior. The safety "reject the command" framing is illustrative, not an evaluated control loop.
  • The phase-encoder ablation is correlational at the system level (encoder swap), not a clean within-encoder magnitude-vs-phase ablation, so how much of the gain is phase vs full-spectral-resolution vs from-scratch training is not isolated.

Why I care

Adjacent / contrast paper. This is the concept-grounding-on-audio half of the dynamic-state-change idea — grounding language/physical-process understanding on raw sound in an LLM — but it lands on a different axis than what I need, and the gap is instructive:

  • Covers (the axis it shares): non-visual (audio) sensing fused into an LLM, with language-level physical reasoning over the signal. It is the cleanest existing demonstration that an LLM can be taught to read physics off audio rather than just labels — the perception substrate a dynamic-state-change predicate (is_boiling, became_slippery) would need.
  • Misses (the axes it doesn't touch): (1) Static channel physics, not dynamic source state-transitions. It reasons about how sound traveled (Doppler, multipath, DoA), not about an object's state changing over a manipulation. (2) Passive QA, not closed-loop correction. No monitoring over time, no replanning, no semantic correction — the exact opposite end from BLADE's replan-after-each-behavior loop. (3) Textual/neural answers, not symbolic predicates. There is no precondition/effect abstraction a planner could consume. (4) No manipulation, no robot, no contact audio. The sound is ambient/propagated, not the impact/scrape/pour audio of a gripper acting on objects.

So relative to BLADE: BLADE learns symbolic, dynamic, action-grounded predicates but on vision; ACORN learns neural, static, passively-sensed physical attributes but on audio. The interesting white space my idea sits in is the union neither occupies: dynamic source-state transitions, grounded on contact/process audio, expressed as monitorable predicates that drive a correction loop. ACORN is the strongest evidence that the audio-grounding leg is feasible; it is also a clean foil for what "physical awareness from sound" should additionally mean for manipulation — closer to HEAR and The Sound of Water (source-side process physics) than to ACORN's channel-side propagation physics.

Related papers already (or being) ingested in this knowledge base:

  • HEAR — sound-centric manipulation incl. process monitoring (boil/pour); the manipulation counterpart ACORN lacks — same audio-grounding leg, but closed-loop and on the robot.

  • Octopi — the tactile analogue: language-grounded reasoning over a non-visual modality but on static object properties, the same passive-recognition limitation as ACORN.
  • BLADE — the anchor: symbolic, dynamic, action-grounded predicates on vision; the structural opposite of ACORN's neural, static, passive audio attributes.
