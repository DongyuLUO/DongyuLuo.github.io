The Sound of Water: Inferring Physical Properties from Pouring Liquids

Piyush Bagad, Makarand Tapaswi, Cees G. M. Snoek, Andrew Zisserman · Oxford / IIIT Hyderabad / Amsterdam · 2025 · ICASSP 2025 (short version) · arXiv:2411.11222 · PDF

Adjacent / contrast paper. This is a vision-and-audio physical-reasoning paper with no robot and no control loop. It is the strongest existing demonstration of recovering a dynamic, time-varying process state (fill level, flow rate, time-to-fill) from audio alone — but it is perception-only, scalar-valued, has no named symbolic concept, and never closes a loop. See "Why I care" for exactly which axis of the dynamic-state-change idea it covers vs. misses.

One-liner. Using only the sound of liquid pouring into a vessel, this paper shows — first in theory, then empirically — that the pitch (fundamental frequency) of the pouring sound determines the air-column length, and hence physical properties like fill level, container size/shape, pour rate, and time-to-fill; it trains a wav2vec2-based pitch detector with no manual pitch labels by pre-training on physics-simulated audio and fine-tuning with a physics-derived visual co-supervision signal from video.

Problem & motivation

Humans can infer liquid level, time-to-fill, container size, and even temperature merely from the sound of pouring, and use that inference to adaptively control their action (e.g., stop pouring to avoid spillage) — an instance of Gibson's affordance theory. The paper asks whether machines can do the same from sound alone. The crux (after Berg & Stork): as liquid fills, a sound of increasing pitch with odd harmonics is heard, and that pitch is a deterministic function of physical properties. Four obstacles: (1) the task needs fine-grained, time-sensitive audio understanding, whereas contemporary audio models target coarse sound classification; (2) the pouring physics is not fully worked out for a general container/liquid setup; (3) no clean, controlled, large dataset existed; (4) supervision (pitch annotations or true physical properties) is hard to obtain at scale. The work targets cheap smartphone recordings — no bespoke force/IMU rigs — explicitly in contrast to prior multimodal liquid-state estimation (e.g., Wilson et al.) that fuses vision+audio+haptics with specialized equipment.

Method

Physics (the core insight). Modeling a cylindrical vessel as an organ pipe closed at one end, the axial resonance fundamental frequency is f(t) = c / (4 (l(t) + βR)), where l(t) is the air-column length, R the radius, c the speed of sound, and β≈0.62 an end-correction factor. Working in wavelength λ(t) = c/f(t) = 4(l(t)+βR) makes the relation linear in l(t), so "audio is effectively a metric ruler" for the video. From λ(t) plus boundary conditions (l(T)=0 at fill time T) the paper derives closed forms for: air-column length (Eq. 5), container height/radius (Eq. 6), volume flow rate (Eq. 7), and time-to-fill (Eq. 8). A subtler radial resonance (musical-glasses-like, frequency decreasing with level) is also characterized but not used for estimation.

Audio network. A wav2vec2 backbone (pre-trained on speech, which already has pitch dynamics) is repurposed for pitch detection: 1D-CNN encoder over 25ms windows (20ms hop), sinusoidal position embeddings, a 12-block Transformer, and a linear head producing a softmax distribution over K=64 wavelength bins (range [0,100], so ~1.56 cm/bin) per time step. Predicting wavelength (not frequency) bakes in linearity with air-column length.

Training without pitch labels (two stages). (1) Synthetic pre-training: a DDSP autoencoder (pitch/loudness/residual latents) is trained to reconstruct real pouring sounds, then driven with physics-derived pitch profiles (sample R~U[1,5]cm, H~U[5,25]cm, linear l(t), invert Eq. 3 for pitch) to generate 10,000 synthetic pouring clips with perfect ground-truth pitch; the network is trained with a KL loss against Gaussian-blurred target bins. (2) Physics-inspired visual co-supervision: to cross the sim2real gap without real pitch labels, a frozen-DINO + Transformer video network predicts air-column length lpx(t) and radius Rpx in pixels (pseudo-labels from temporal differencing + SAM container segmentation). A per-video scale factor α (encoding depth/intrinsics, estimated as an RMS-energy-weighted ratio of audio wavelength to pixel length) ties the two: α·(λ(t)/4) = lpx(t) + βRpx (Eq. 10). The audio net is fine-tuned with an MSE loss against this video-derived signal.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: New Sound of Water 50 dataset — 805 videos of a hand pouring liquid across 50 containers (4 shapes: cylindrical, semi-conical, bottleneck, hemispherical; 5 materials: glass, plastic, ceramic, steel, cardboard; 2 liquids: hot/normal water), smartphone-recorded, fixed camera, ~constant-but-randomized flow rate per video, durations 3.5–30.1s (mean 10.44s). Four splits (Table 1): Train (18 transparent cylinder-like containers, 195 videos), Test I (seen containers, distinct videos), Test II (unseen cylinder-like), Test III (434 videos, unseen, for shape classification). Also evaluated on the Wilson et al. weight dataset (136 sequences, 6 containers).
  • Hardware / simulator: No robot. Smartphone camera in a domestic setting; DDSP-based synthetic-audio generator (10,000 clips) for pre-training.
  • Baselines: Pitch detectors — Yin, CREPE, PESTO, and per-frame argmax on spectrogram. Mass estimation — k-NN, Linear SVM, SoundNet-5/8, TCN, and PSNN (Wilson et al.). Ablation: audio-only (synthetic-only) vs. co-supervised.
  • Compute: not reported (only optimizer/epochs given: pre-train 100 epochs, batch 32, Adam lr 1e−4; fine-tune 5 epochs, lr 1e−6).

Results

Air-column length (Table 2, MAE in cm). The co-supervised model reaches 0.60 cm (Test I) / 0.71 cm (Test II), vs. audio-only 0.78 / 0.82, and far below all pitch-detector baselines (best baseline argmax 4.60 / 5.11; CREPE 7.61 / 9.40; Yin 30.80 / 27.30) — an order of magnitude better.

Other physical properties (Table 3, MAE). Co-supervision helps most where the signal is weak (end of pour). Static: height 2.27 cm / radius 1.39 cm (Test I, co-sup vs. synthetic 2.23 / 1.62). Dynamic: flow rate 22.50 ml/s (vs. 25.20 audio-only); time-to-fill (given 25%/50%/75% partial audio) 4.16 / 1.49 / 1.07 s on Test I. Co-supervision notably improves radius and flow-rate (both depend on reliable late-audio λ and its slope).

Emergent shape & weight. A 3-way shape classifier on the co-supervised features (Test III, unseen containers) hits 90.91% sample / 92.47% mean-class accuracy (vs. 88.63 / 89.44 without co-supervision) — pitch features implicitly encode container shape. Linear-probing the features for liquid weight on the Wilson et al. dataset gives 1.20 oz mean MAE, beating all baselines and competitive with the strong supervised fine-tuned PSNN (1.35 oz), using no vision at test time. Generalizes qualitatively to unseen shapes (cups, teapots, freeform glasses), materials, in-the-wild YouTube videos, and even flute sounds (same physics).

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • The pitch detector struggles with hemispherical containers (too much room for air to escape → very weak resonance) and with bottleneck containers showing multiple frequency modes it can't disentangle (Fig. 15).
  • The physics derivation assumes cylindrical geometry; training is on cylinder-like transparent containers, with non-cylindrical generalization shown only qualitatively.
  • Time-to-fill assumes a constant flow rate; pausing mid-pour makes it ill-defined.
  • It generalizes to flute (same resonance principle) but not to other instruments / polyphonic music.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Everything is scalar regression — air-column length, radius, flow rate, time-to-fill. There is no named, discrete concept ("full", "about to overflow", "started boiling"); the "state" is a continuous physical quantity, not a predicate or symbol.
  • Strictly open-loop perception: the model estimates properties from a recorded clip but never feeds them to an actuator or replanner. The motivating affordance ("stop pouring to avoid spillage") is invoked rhetorically, never executed.
  • The closed-form physics is what makes audio a "metric ruler" — elegant, but it also means the method is tied to a specific, well-modeled process (pouring into an organ-pipe geometry). It would not transfer to processes without such a clean acoustic model (e.g., "became slippery after wetting").
  • Co-supervision is a clever label-free trick, but it requires a video of the same event with a segmentable container — so the "sound alone" claim holds only at test time, not at training time.

Why I care

For the dynamic-state-change-grounding idea, this is the strongest existing dynamic-process-from-audio perception result — and a precise illustration of where pure perception stops short of what I want.

  • Axis it covers (well): grounding a dynamic, time-varying process (fill level, pour rate, time-to-fill monitored over the whole pour) on a non-visual modality (audio), with a verified physical mechanism. This is exactly the "process state from sound, monitored over time" capability my idea needs as a perceptual front-end — e.g., a is_full(cup) or "about-to-overflow" precondition could in principle be read off this signal.
  • Axes it misses: (1) scalar, not symbolic — no named concept, no predicate, just a continuous estimate (contrast BLADE's discrete pre/effect predicates); (2) passive recognition, not closed-loop correction — no controller, no replanning, no "stop when full"; (3) state, not state-transition — it tracks a quantity continuously but never names the event of crossing a threshold ("became full", "started overflowing").
  • Relation to BLADE: BLADE grounds discrete predicates on vision and composes them via planning, but its preconditions/effects are categorical and visual. This paper is the missing perceptual primitive for a dynamic, audio-grounded predicate — if I want is_full or pour_rate_ok as a BLADE-style predicate driven by sound, this is the classifier-input front-end, and the gap to fill is: turn the scalar into a named predicate and wire it into a replanning loop.
  • Contrast with the static-property audio line (Octopi on tactile, SonicSense/See-Hear-Feel on acoustic material ID): those ground static properties; this is the cleanest dynamic counterpart. Pairs naturally with the pouring-process line (HEAR, Making Sense of Audio Vibration, ManiWAV) where the audio does enter a control/VLA loop — this paper is the deepest physics, those are the closest to my closed-loop target.

Related

Cited works plausibly worth ingesting (not in corpus; no local PDFs yet):

  • Wilson et al. — audio-visual CNN supervised to predict liquid mass from instantaneous video+audio; the closest prior and the mass-estimation evaluation set here. Methodological contrast (instantaneous, supervised, no physics).
  • Cabe & Pittenger — psychoacoustics of filling-level / time-to-fill perception; the human-capability anchor the paper measures against.
  • wav2vec2 (Baevski et al.) and CREPE / PESTO — the backbone and the pitch-detection baselines.
  • DDSP (Engel et al.) — the differentiable-DSP synthesizer used to generate the physics-conditioned training audio.

Related papers already in the corpus:

  • HEAR — sound-centric manipulation incl. pour/boil monitoring; the closed-loop, language-conditioned counterpart to this perception-only result.
  • Octopi — static tactile-property reasoning; the static-vs-dynamic contrast on a different non-visual modality.
  • BLADE — the predicate-grounding anchor; this paper supplies a candidate audio front-end for a dynamic predicate that BLADE currently lacks.
