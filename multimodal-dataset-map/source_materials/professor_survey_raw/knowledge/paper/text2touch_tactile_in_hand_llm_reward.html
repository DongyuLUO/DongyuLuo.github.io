Text2Touch: Tactile In-Hand Manipulation with LLM-Designed Reward Functions

Harrison Field, Max Yang, Yijiong Lin, Efi Psomopoulou, David Barton, Nathan F. Lepora · University of Bristol / Bristol Robotics Laboratory · 2025 · arXiv:2509.07445 · PDF

One-liner. Text2Touch is the first system to let an LLM auto-design the RL reward function for a tactile dexterous task — multi-axis in-hand object rotation on a real four-fingered Allegro hand with vision-based TacTip sensors — and the LLM-crafted reward, distilled sim-to-real via teacher–student, beats a carefully hand-tuned human baseline on rotation speed and stability while using ~1/10 the variables and ~1/4 the lines of code.

Problem & motivation

LLM-driven reward design (Eureka [5] and follow-ons) has been shown to match or beat hand-crafted rewards, but every prior demonstration used vision or proprioception only — never touch. Tactile sensing is known to be critical for human-like dexterity, yet it amplifies the difficulty of reward design: high-DoF fully-actuated hands plus rich, high-dimensional contact signals make the reward landscape hard to specify by hand. The authors ask a clean question: can an LLM autonomously write a good reward for a real-world, vision-based-tactile manipulation task? Their target is gravity-invariant multi-axis in-hand rotation (rotate an aloft object about each of 3 perpendicular axes, in both palm-up and palm-down hand orientations) — the hardest dexterity setting from AnyRotate [12], which itself showed this level of dexterity depends critically on tactile feedback.

Method

Text2Touch extends the Eureka iterative reward-design loop and bolts it onto a teacher–student sim-to-real pipeline (Fig 2). Three pieces.

1. Iterative LLM reward design (Eq 1). Given environment code/context M and a natural-language task description l, an LLM proposes a batch of reward-function candidates {R(k)}. Each candidate trains a PPO policy π(k); a fitness F (running count of sub-goal successes per rollout) scores them; the best Rbest(k) is fed back via reward reflection to seed the next iteration. The task description l was tuned to mirror the human baseline's task spec without injecting qualitative bias ("reposition and reorient objects… by regrasping or finger gaiting, where contacts must be detached and repositioned locally"). An environment-based subgoal curriculum updates the target orientation each time an incremental rotation is met, allowing multiple successes per episode.

2. Two novel prompting moves for high-complexity environments. The tactile environment exposes over 70 variables vs Eureka's ~10, which broke the original prompt. (a) Scaling bonuses and penalties: rather than Eureka's fixed scalar success bonus B added outside the LLM reward, both a success bonus B and an early-termination penalty P are exposed to the LLM as scalable variables (without revealing the success/failure conditions), so the LLM can size them relative to its own reward terms. The paper reports task success was empirically impossible without scalable B,P (Table 1). (b) Modified prompt structuring: supply an explicit reward-function signature Sdetailed listing all variable names/types, so Mmodified = {E, Sdetailed}. This sharply reduced syntactic/type errors and code failures at 70+ variables.

3. Reward-discovery training + sim-to-real distillation. Full 8-billion-step training per candidate is infeasible, so each iteration uses short 150M-step runs to rank candidates, then the single best reward is trained from scratch for 8B steps (5×). A Teacher policy is trained in sim with privileged observations (object pose/velocity, properties, gravity direction, auxiliary goal); a Student is then fit by regression (MSE to teacher actions) using only proprioception + tactile observations, and transfers to hardware with no further tuning. The Allegro hand wears TacTip vision-based tactile sensors and runs at 20 Hz.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: No external dataset — RL in simulation. Task = gravity-invariant multi-axis in-hand rotation following AnyRotate [12]. Real-world eval over ten household objects, 3 rotation axes (X/Y/Z) × palm-up/palm-down. OOD eval on heavier masses and novel shapes (Fig 4 top, Table 3).
  • Hardware / simulator: GPU-accelerated RL sim (Isaac-style; rigid-body tactile). Real hardware: fully-actuated four-fingered Allegro Hand with vision-based TacTip tactile sensors, 20 Hz, receiving joint positions, velocities, and tactile observations.
  • Baselines: Carefully hand-engineered human reward from AnyRotate [12] (stages 2–3 frozen as published, for fair comparison). Across the LLM-design study: 5 LLMs × 4 prompting strategies (GPT-4o, o3-mini, Gemini-1.5-Flash, Llama-3.1-405B, Deepseek-R1-671B; strategies = Bonus/Penalty+Mod, Bonus/Penalty, Mod template, Original).
  • Compute: Reward discovery uses 150M-step short runs; final policies trained 8B steps each, 5 repeats. Over 4 strategies × 5 LLMs × 5 experiments × 20 rewards = 2,000 generated reward functions in the prompting study. Wall-clock / GPU-hours not reported.

Results

Headline: the best LLM-designed reward beats the human-engineered baseline both in sim (with privileged obs) and, after distillation, in the real world.

Prompting ablation (Table 1, highest 360° rotations/episode and Solve Rate, frac. of episodes with >1 success). Only the Bonus/Penalty+Mod strategy yields non-trivial solve rates; removing scalable B,P or the detailed signature collapses to ~0% solve. Gemini-1.5-Flash reached the highest Solve Rate (31%); GPT-4o the highest best Rots/Ep among the top cells (5.46).

Sim, privileged teacher vs human baseline (Table 2). The baseline gets 4.92 best / 4.73 avg Rots/Ep; every LLM beats it on avg, and all use far less code:

Reward–baseline correlation is low (Corr 0.30–0.42), i.e. the LLM discovered genuinely different reward structures rather than re-deriving the human one, yet got ∼1/10 the variables, ∼1/4 the LoC, and ∼1/8 the Halstead Volume.

Real-world distilled students (Table 4, avg full Rotations and Time To Terminate over all objects). All three deployed LLM policies beat the baseline on average rotations; Deepseek-R1-671B gives a 38% increase in rotations and 25% longer episode duration over the baseline (Total Avg Rot 1.37 vs 0.99; TTT 25.1s vs 20.0s). Notably the direction flips from sim: the human baseline looked more stable in simulation, but the LLM policies are more stable on real hardware — the authors attribute this to LLM policies favouring faster, more reactive motions that exploit real tactile feedback for early instability detection and regrasping (rigid-body sim tactile being brittle vs smooth real TacTip signals). OOD (Table 3): students still beat the baseline on heavier masses and novel shapes.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Evaluated on a single in-hand rotation task; generalization to other tasks/sensor modalities and to multi-stage / long-horizon planning is untested. They flag long-horizon dependencies as future work.
  • Only reward design is automated; architecture, curriculum, and hyperparameters are held fixed — LLMs could also optimize those.
  • Fewer real-world hand orientations (palm up/down only) than AnyRotate's baseline study, for practical feasibility; real-world assessment still needs heavy human intervention (e.g. replacing a dropped object).
  • LLMs still frequently produce rewards that fail to train, giving low solve rates — reward discovery is compute-heavy and a bottleneck.
  • Sim-to-real gap for tactile is hard: rigid-body sim sensors vs soft real sensors; soft-body simulators flagged as future work.

What I noticed reading it:

  • The real-world headline (38% over baseline) rests on a single Deepseek-R1 student over ten objects with no reported per-object variance or seeds in Table 4 — it's an average over objects, not a multi-seed mean ± std like Table 1. The stability claim ("more stable in the real world") is partly qualitative (Duck-about-z anecdote).
  • The sim-vs-real reversal of stability is the most interesting finding but also the least controlled: faster motions being a sim liability yet a real asset is plausible but hinges on the (unmeasured) fidelity gap between rigid-body and TacTip tactile signals. No ablation isolates speed from reactivity.
  • The reward is "tactile" only in that tactile variables are available in the 70-var context; the paper doesn't quantify how much the LLM-chosen reward terms actually depend on tactile vs proprioceptive variables. A tactile-ablated reward-design run would sharpen the central claim.
  • Only 5 short-run iterations and 5 final repeats; with solve rates as low as ∼16–31%, the "best" reward per cell is selected from a noisy, small sample — selection bias likely inflates the best-cell numbers.

Why I care

This sits right on the thesis that motivates the whole 2026-06-24 batch: many manipulation predicates — is_grasped, is_rotating_stably, contact_is_slipping — are not visually evaluable; they live in touch and force. Text2Touch is a useful and slightly off-axis data point. It does not learn predicates or symbolic abstractions, and it has no language-grounded state at execution time — the "language" is confined to LLM-authored reward code, not to BLADE's goal-to-FOL translation or operator proposal. So relative to BLADE this is the low-level controller layer, not the abstraction layer: it's the tactile analogue of "train a good diffusion policy," except the policy is RL-trained and the LLM's job is to write its reward.

Where it connects: (1) It is direct evidence that an LLM can reason usefully over a tactile observation space (70+ contact/proprio variables) and produce concise, interpretable control objectives — encouraging for a future BLADE-style system whose contact-primitive bodies (e.g. rotate-in-hand, regrasp) are trained against force/tactile signals rather than vision. (2) The reversal "speed is a sim liability but a real asset via reactive tactile regrasping" is exactly the kind of force-modulated behavior BLADE's purely categorical abstraction layer pushes entirely into the controller — a reminder that the abstraction/controller split has to leave room for tactile reactivity. (3) The Eureka-style reward-reflection loop is an LLM-in-the-loop design tool that could, in principle, also propose predicates or termination conditions — the bridge to predicate invention, though this paper doesn't take it.
