DIGIT: A Novel Design for a Low-Cost Compact High-Resolution Tactile Sensor with Application to In-Hand Manipulation

Mike Lambeta*, Po-Wei Chou*, Stephen Tian*, Brian Yang*, Benjamin Maloon, Victoria Rose Most, Dave Stroud, Raymond Santos, Ahmad Byagowi, Gregg Kammerer, Dinesh Jayaraman, Roberto Calandra (Facebook AI Research) · 2020 · IEEE Robotics and Automation Letters (RA-L) · arXiv:2005.14679 · PDF

One-liner. DIGIT is a ~$15, fingertip-sized, high-resolution camera-based tactile sensor whose ruggedized gel and tool-less, off-the-shelf manufacturing finally make it practical to instrument every finger of a multi-fingered hand — turning vision-based touch from a benchtop curiosity into a mass-producible commodity for in-hand manipulation research.

Problem & motivation

General-purpose in-hand manipulation stays unsolved partly because robots cannot precisely sense contact forces, and touch is the most direct, low-noise way to measure them. Vision-based tactile sensors (GelSight, GelSlim, FingerVision, TacTip) give unmatched spatial resolution by imaging the deformation of a soft elastomer, but the authors identify three blockers to wide adoption: (i) bulky form factors that don't fit on multi-finger hands, (ii) soft contact surfaces that wear out fast, and (iii) largely manual, high-variability manufacturing that makes a damaged sensor hard to replace without re-calibration or retraining. A sensor that is simultaneously high-resolution, sensitive, reliable, easy to use, compact, and cheap did not exist. DIGIT is the engineering answer.

Sensor design / formalism

DIGIT is a vision-based tactile sensor: a camera images a soft reflective elastomer lit by colored LEDs, and contact geometry is read off as image deformations. The contribution is the concrete design that makes this compact, durable, and reproducible. Three design pillars:

Mechanical design (Fig 2). A 20×27×18 mm, ~20 g, three-piece plastic enclosure (3D-printable for prototyping, injection-moldable at scale). Camera and gel attach via press-fit connections so any component swaps out on breakage; the elastomer is replaceable with a single screw, and the housing can be swapped to change focal length. The modular body lets users drop in task-specific elastomers (Fig 4): purely reflective for texture, reflective-with-markers for optical-flow/force, or transparent-with-markers (FingerVision-style) to see objects during approach. This is a deliberate generalization of the GelSight idea into a configurable platform.

Electronic design. Custom electronics fit in ~7 cm² (barely larger than a human fingertip). Camera is an Omnivision OVM7692, a 60 fps color CMOS with a 1.15 mm focal-length microlens and 30 cm depth of field, at 640×480. A custom PCB routes to a SuperSpeed USB 3.0 hub so multiple DIGITs share one host USB port, and gives manual control of three RGB LEDs (max 4 lumens).

Elastomer design & durability. The headline robustness win. GelSight gels put an opaque image-transfer layer at the contact surface, which wears. DIGIT casts the gel in three stages: a silicone-based white pigment airbrushed into the mold and cured with a chemical kicker to make a uniform-thickness image-transfer layer; a Smooth-On Solaris base layer cured on a finger-shaped mold; then bonded to an acrylic window with Sil-Poxy optically clear adhesive, press-fit into the body. A thin transfer layer is sensitive but fragile; a thick one is rugged but blurs resolution — the authors hand-tune this thickness trade-off. The result is a far more abrasion-resistant gel (see Results).

Demonstration application — scaling tactile-MPC to multiple fingers. To validate the sensor they push tactile model-predictive control (tactile-MPC, ref. Tian et al.) from one sensor / 3-DOF to two DIGITs / 8-DOF. A keypoint autoencoder (tiny ResNet-18 backbone, "Structural VRNN" bottleneck) compresses each raw DIGIT image to a single (x, y, i) keypoint — the marble's position plus a press-depth intensity. State is s = [k_l, k_r, j] (left+right keypoints + 8 joint angles), only 14-D vs. the raw 64×64 images. A lightweight MLP dynamics model s' = f(s, a) predicts next state, and MPC with the cross-entropy method plans directly in the 14-D keypoint space (encoder called once per MPC step), which is what makes planning over two image streams and 8-DOF tractable (Fig 6).

Coverage

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected DIGIT marble-manipulation videos (4800 autonomous trials, 950 held out for validation, via a golf-tee-style auto-reset rig); BAIR robot pushing dataset used as a standard video-prediction benchmark. Abrasion-test bench comparing DIGIT gel vs. Yuan et al. [11] gel and a GelSight Inc. gel.
  • Hardware / simulator: DIGIT sensors mounted on an Allegro multi-finger hand (thumb + middle finger), on a Sawyer arm; the in-hand task is rolling a glass marble in a precision grip. No simulator — all real hardware.
  • Baselines: For the predictive model, CDNA (the video-prediction model used in prior tactile-MPC). For control, a hand-tuned linear proportional (P) controller in keypoint space. For the gel, two competing GelSight-style gels under abrasion.
  • Compute: not reported (no GPU/training-budget figures; only inference timings in Table III).

Results

Cost & form factor (Table I). DIGIT is 20×27×18 mm at 20 g and ~$15/sensor in components (batches of 1000: PCB $1.5, electronics $8, plastics $2, gel $3), vs. fingertip GelSight at 35×60×35 mm and ~$30, and GelSlim at 50×205×20 mm. Sensing field 19×16 mm, 640×480 @ 60 fps.

After just 5 passes the two competing gels were torn / rendered unusable; DIGIT's was nearly unaffected (Fig 5). Caveat the authors flag: DIGIT's gel is more translucent (transmits 676 of 1255 Lux vs. 17 and 16 for the others), but they argue the manipulation results show this doesn't hurt sensing.

So CDNA is ~5×–20× more accurate per-pixel, but Struct-NN is ~50× faster per MPC step (1.4 s vs. 69 s), which is what makes real-time multi-finger control feasible. A single MPC step runs 250 particles, horizon 10, ~120 CEM iterations (~0.3M dynamics forward passes).

In-hand control (Fig 8, 50 trials/condition). The learned-dynamics MPC steadily reduces Euclidean keypoint distance-to-goal over a rollout, while the hand-tuned P controller diverges. Drop rate: ~25% of trials drop the marble before reaching goal for the learned controller (attributed to planning inaccuracy and joint actuation noise); the P controller drops far more by ~6–8 actions in.

Limitations & open questions

Author-stated. ~25% of marble trials still end in a drop, blamed on low-level controller and actuation noise; they suggest a better low-level controller and more data. The image-transfer-layer thickness is a hand-tuned ruggedness/sensitivity trade-off rather than principled. Future work: further miniaturization and curved, omni-directional sensing fields.

What I noticed reading it. (a) The control results are single-task and single-object (one glass marble between two fingers) — the sensor's generality claim rests almost entirely on the design and the abrasion table, not on diverse manipulation. (b) Statistics are thin: 50 trials per condition with median/68th-percentile curves but no significance tests, and the headline "25% drop" is a count-style summary, not a per-condition rate with error bars across objects. (c) The Struct-NN keypoint trick collapsed to a single active keypoint (K=8 set, 7 went inactive) — convenient for a marble, but it's unclear this 1-keypoint representation generalizes to objects with richer contact geometry; the method effectively assumes one tracked blob. (d) Durability is the strongest empirical result, but it's one abrasion device, one weight (1.7 N), and three gels — no field-aging or thousands-of-grasp lifetime data despite the lifespan claims. (e) The "high-resolution" framing competes against a 640×480 sensor while the GelSight baseline in Table I is 1920×1080; DIGIT's edge is cost/size/durability, not raw pixels.

Why I care

This is a hardware enabler, not a planning or language paper, and I'll treat it as such — but it is foundational to a thesis running through my work. In BLADE I learn PDDL-style predicates (preconditions / effects) from language and ground them with visual classifiers. The uncomfortable truth that this batch keeps surfacing is that many manipulation predicates — is_grasped, is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, surface_is_rough, contact_is_stable — are not visually evaluable; they live in touch and force. DIGIT matters because it is one of the two canonical sensors (with GelSight) that made high-resolution touch cheap and dense enough to put on every finger, which is the precondition for any future predicate classifier that reads contact state from touch rather than RGB. Nearly every tactile-representation and touch-language model in this batch (Sparsh, UniTouch, TVL, Octopi, T3) trains on DIGIT/GelSight image streams — so DIGIT is upstream infrastructure for the whole tactile-predicate direction. Secondary relevance for my world-model interests: the paper's keypoint-space dynamics model + MPC is a clean, tiny instance of learning a latent forward model for contact-rich control, exactly the kind of compact abstraction a TAMP body could plan over.
