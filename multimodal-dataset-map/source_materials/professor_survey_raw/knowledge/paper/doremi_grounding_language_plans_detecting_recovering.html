DoReMi: Grounding Language Model by Detecting and Recovering from Plan-Execution Misalignment

Yanjiang Guo*, Yen-Jen Wang*, Lihan Zha, Jianyu Chen† · Tsinghua University (IIIS) / Shanghai Qi Zhi · 2024 (arXiv 2307.00329v4) · arXiv:2307.00329 · PDF

One-liner. DoReMi makes the LLM emit not just a high-level plan but also, for each skill, a set of natural-language constraints ("robot holds box", "red block on green block"); a VLM "constraint detector" then checks those constraints continuously during low-level execution (every 0.2 s), and the instant one is violated the skill is aborted and the LLM re-plans — closing the loop inside a skill rather than only between skills.

Problem & motivation

LLM task planners that decompose an instruction into a sequence of pre-trained low-level skills are typically open-loop: the skill is assumed to execute the plan step perfectly. In the real world it doesn't — environmental perturbations or imperfect controllers cause the low-level execution to drift from the high-level plan at any moment during a skill (the paper's running example: a humanoid carrying a box accidentally drops it while "go to the gray table"; continuing is futile). Prior feedback methods (ReAct, Reflexion, and especially Inner Monologue) inject feedback into the LLM prompt only after a skill finishes — delayed, low-frequency, step-level feedback. DoReMi's pitch is that misalignment must be detected during execution, continuously and at high frequency, with detectors cheap enough to run in the loop.

Method

Two-stage framework (Fig 2c, Algorithm 1):

1. LLM for plan + constraint generation. The LLM plays a dual role. Via few-shot in-context prompting it (i) selects the next skill πn from the skill set, and (ii) generates the constraints cn that must hold while that skill executes, drawing on its commonsense physical knowledge of the task. The prompt is constructed so that after the LLM picks the next step it continues generation starting from "Constraint:" to derive the constraints. Example: after "pick up box", the LLM emits the constraint "robot holds box"; violation signals a failed pick or a drop. Constraints are arbitrary text predicates ("red block on blue block", "no obstacles in front of the robot", "robot is holding an apple"). They run Vicuna-13B locally (pick next skill at max output probability) and also test GPT-4 (zero temperature) as the planner.

2. VLM as constraint detector. During the chosen skill's execution, a pre-trained VLM (BLIP-2) is queried every Δt = 0.2 s. Each LLM-generated constraint cj is auto-templated into a yes/no question "Question: Is the constraint cj satisfied? Answer:", and the VLM picks from {"Yes", "No"} — a tiny output (<0.1 s, <0.1 USD). If any constraint reads False, the skill is aborted, the violation text is appended to the LLM prompt, and re-planning is triggered for immediate recovery. Key design point (Fig 3): forcing the VLM into a focused binary query about an LLM-specified constraint is far less ambiguous than asking the VLM for an open-ended scene description (the failure mode they attribute to Inner Monologue's scene descriptors). For complex scenes (prepare-food) they LoRA-fine-tune BLIP-2 on a small dataset to sharpen the detector; the framework also notes detectors in other modalities could run in parallel at different frequencies, though only the vision VLM is implemented.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Robot-arm tasks adapted from Ravens / Transporter Nets (Pick-and-Place, Stack-blocks-in-order) with CLIPort-style pre-trained pick/place primitives; humanoid tasks (Obstacle-avoidance, Move-box, Prepare-food, plus real Prepare-food-real and Stack-real). A 128 image-text pair fine-tuning set (5 fruit demos) for the VLM-FT variant.
  • Hardware / simulator: Sim UR5e arm with suction gripper (third-view camera); a 20-DoF humanoid (6 DoF/leg, 4 DoF/arm) with a DeepMimic RL locomotion policy + model-based manipulation; real humanoid with dexterous/factory pick primitives, LLMs/VLMs on the cloud communicating at 1 Hz.
  • Baselines: SayCan (LLM step decomposition, open-loop); CLIPort (multi-task policy + VLM success detector, step-level repeat); Inner Monologue (IM) (VLM scene descriptors + success detector, re-plan on step completion); IM-Oracle (Inner Monologue with oracle feedback — an upper bound that doesn't exist in practice). DoReMi-FT denotes DoReMi with the fine-tuned VLM.
  • Compute: VLM LoRA fine-tuning on a single Nvidia A100; BLIP-2 detector query <0.1 s. Otherwise not reported.

Results

Across both robot-arm (Table I) and humanoid (Table II) tasks under injected disturbances (random box-drop probability p, placement noise n, obstacle density d, pick-failure), DoReMi reports higher success rates and shorter execution times than SayCan / CLIPort / Inner Monologue, trailing only the (impractical) IM-Oracle — and on some humanoid tasks DoReMi-FT even surpasses IM-Oracle on execution time because immediate detection avoids wasted motion.

  • SayCan fails consistently under disturbance (no detector, no re-planning).
  • On stack-blocks with collapse (Fig 5 left), CLIPort+success-detector keeps repeating the previous step and fails; DoReMi detects the misalignment and re-plans to fix the collapsed block first.
  • On the box-drop humanoid task (Fig 5 right), Inner Monologue (even oracle) only re-plans after the skill finishes — delayed recovery; DoReMi aborts mid-skill and recovers faster.
  • Humanoid Move-box with drop p=0.04: DoReMi 96±4 vs SayCan 42±9 / IM 46±9 success. Real humanoid: Prepare-food-real 90% and Stack-real 80% for DoReMi-FT vs 20% / 10–20% for IM.
  • Zero-shot VLM degrades as scene complexity rises (prepare-food); LoRA fine-tuning on 128 pairs lifts BLIP-2 detector accuracy markedly (Table III) and generalizes to unseen objects, backgrounds, and tasks (Fig 6). Ablation (Table IV): BLIP-2 ≈ Instruct-BLIP, all zero-shot VLMs weak on prepare-food.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Zero-shot VLM detector performance "diminishes as the scene complexity increases" — complex multi-object scenes need fine-tuning to be reliable.
  • Constraints are checked through a single vision VLM; detectors in other modalities are described as "compatible" but not implemented.

What I noticed reading it:

  • All constraints are vision-checkable boolean predicates. The whole pipeline assumes the relevant misalignment is visible to a camera ("box dropped", "block collapsed"). State changes that are invisible — "water is hot now", "grasp is slipping", "surface became slippery", "screw is tight" — have no detector here. This is the central gap relative to my direction.
  • Constraints are LLM-generated text, not grounded learned predicates. Their truth depends on the VLM correctly parsing free-form language into a yes/no judgement; there is no learned classifier per concept and no guarantee the VLM and LLM share a grounding of the phrase.
  • Constraints are largely static spatial/possession facts checked repeatedly, not temporal transitions. DoReMi monitors "is constraint X still true?" but does not reason about a process unfolding ("is it coming to a boil?", "has it become cooked?") — a transition would have to be encoded as a static end-state predicate.
  • Re-planning latency vs detection frequency tension: detector runs at 5 Hz but the cloud LLM/VLM loop on the real humanoid is 1 Hz; how detection frequency trades against re-plan cost in faster-dynamics tasks is untested.
  • No measure of false-positive aborts: an over-eager constraint detector that spuriously fires would abort good executions; the cost of detector precision isn't analyzed.

Why I care

DoReMi is a clean exemplar of the closed-loop replanning axis I want my work to live on: the LLM emits constraints, a perception model checks violations continuously, and recovery/replanning is triggered the moment a constraint breaks. That continuous-monitoring-plus-recovery loop is exactly the control structure I want for dynamic state-change concepts — it is the "detect misalignment during execution, not after" insight made concrete, and a sharper version of the step-level feedback in Inner Monologue and the post-hoc explanation in REFLECT.

But it covers only half the axes of my idea and misses the half I care about most. (1) Vision-only constraint checking: every detector is a VLM looking at a camera, so it can only catch visible misalignments. The dynamic concepts I target — "hot enough now", "became slippery after wetting", "coming to a boil", a slipping grasp — are exactly the ones a camera can't read; they need tactile/audio/thermal/force monitoring (the modality my line, with Octopi, Tactile-VLA, Audio-VLA etc., is built on). (2) Constraints are LLM-text, not grounded multimodal change-predicates: unlike BLADE's learned predicate classifiers with preconditions/effects, DoReMi's constraints are free-form phrases the VLM zero-shot-parses, with no learned grounding and no notion of effect-as-state-transition. (3) Static facts, not transitions: it monitors persistence of a static predicate, not the temporal evolution of a process. So DoReMi gives me the loop architecture I want but stops at vision + text + static facts; my contribution sits precisely in swapping its vision-VLM constraint detector for grounded, multimodal, time-series change-predicates that drive the same recovery loop. It pairs naturally with Code-as-Monitor (constraints as executable visual programs) as the two "constraint-as-monitor" points to position against.

We leverage LLMs to play a dual role, aiding not only in high-level planning but also generating constraints that can indicate misalignment during execution. Then vision language models (VLMs) are utilized to detect constraint violations continuously. — Abstract
