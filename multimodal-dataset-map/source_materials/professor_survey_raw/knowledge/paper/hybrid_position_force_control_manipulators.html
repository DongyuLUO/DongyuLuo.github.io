Hybrid Position/Force Control of Manipulators

M. H. Raibert, J. J. Craig · 1981 · ASME J. Dynamic Systems, Measurement, and Control, Vol. 102, pp. 126–133 · DOI · PDF

One-liner. The founding paper of force-aware manipulation: it partitions the task space into orthogonal position-controlled and force-controlled directions via a binary compliance selection vector, runs an independent servo loop in each, and sums their joint-torque contributions — so a robot can follow a position trajectory in one axis while regulating contact force in another (slide along a surface, press a peg into a hole) within one clean architecture.

Problem & motivation

Assembly and contact tasks demand positioning precision higher than imperfectly rigid, gear-driven arms can deliver from joint encoders alone. The authors' insight is that relative measurement via contact force is a cheap substitute for absolute precision: "small variations in relative position generate large contact forces when parts of moderate stiffness interact," so controlling force buys effective positional accuracy without the size/weight/cost of a stiffer arm. Prior compliant-control schemes (Paul & Shimano's "free-joint" method [6]) were approximate — they force-controlled only a subset of joints and patched the resulting position errors on later servo cycles. The goal is a conceptually simple, exact architecture for simultaneous position and force trajectory control using a wrist-mounted force sensor.

Formalism / framework

The method sits on Mason's compliance theory [4] and Paul's constraint frame. Every contact task is described in a task-related Cartesian constraint frame {C} with N degrees of freedom (here "position" means position+orientation, "force" means force+torque). The task geometry imposes natural constraints: along surface normals position is constrained (you can't move through a rigid surface); along surface tangents force is constrained (a frictionless surface can't sustain tangential force). These partition the DOFs into two orthogonal sets. The user then specifies the complementary artificial constraints — a desired force trajectory along normals, a desired position trajectory along tangents — which preserves consistency with the natural constraints (Fig. 1 works the crank and screwdriver examples).

The compliance selection vector. The core object is a binary N-tuple S: S_j = 1 marks DOF j as force-controlled, S_j = 0 as position-controlled. Its diagonal form is the selection matrix [S]. The torque applied by actuator i sums one contribution per DOF in {C} (Eq. 2): τ_i = Σ_j { Γ_ij[ s_j Δf_j ] + ψ_ij[ (1 - s_j) Δx_j ] }, where Γ and ψ are force and position compensators and Δf, Δx are force/position errors. Each DOF in {C} is governed by exactly one loop, but every joint contributes to both subsystems — this is the central idea, and what makes it exact where the free-joint method is approximate (no inter-cycle position correction needed).

Coordinate transforms (Fig. 2). Sensor signals live in their own frames — position in joint space {q}, force in hand frame {H} — and must be lifted into {C} before errors are computed: {}^C X = Λ(q) (forward kinematics) and {}^C F = [{}^C_H T] {}^H F (a 6×6 force transform built from the {H}→{C} rotation and the moment cross-product [V×]). Errors are formed (Eq. 4), masked by [S] / [I]−[S] into the two subspaces, mapped back to joint space through the Jacobian (q_e = [J]^{-1\,C}X_e for position, τ_e = [J]^T\,{}^C F_e for force), and the two torque vectors are summed: τ = τ_p + τ_f. The architecture is deliberately agnostic to the regulator: it "does not prescribe particular feedback control laws... it suggests a control architecture within which such laws can be applied." In the experiments the position loop is PID and the force loop is PI plus a saturation limiter and a quasi-static force feed-forward term (τ_ff = [J]^T\,{}^C F_d).

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: not reported — a 1981 control paper; evaluation is by step/ramp trajectory-tracking experiments, not a dataset. Tasks: force step/ramp tracking, position-disturbance rejection, simultaneous force-step + sinusoidal-position, and a peg-in-hole insertion (15.9 mm dia, 0.025 mm / 1 mil clearance, no chamfer).
  • Hardware / simulator: modified Scheinman Stanford arm (joints 1 & 3 for 2-DOF planar motion) with a "Maltese Cross" 16-strain-gauge force-sensing wrist; a numerically-controlled X-Y table supplies reaction forces and disturbance motions; control on a General Automation SPC-16/85 minicomputer. A two-axis dynamics model (Eqs. 5–11, with Coulomb+static friction and spring-modeled wrist/surface) was simulated alongside the physical rig. Servo rate 60–120 Hz.
  • Baselines: Paul & Shimano's "free-joint" compliant control [6] (discussed qualitatively, not run head-to-head); simulation vs. physical responses cross-checked against each other.
  • Compute: not reported in modern terms; the authors note the method is "not cheap in the computational sense" — requiring many coordinate transforms per cycle — which they argue newer microelectronics now justify.

Results

The controller achieves stable, accurate force and position tracking across all test conditions. Headline numbers:

TestResult
Force step (1, 5, 10 Nt)Rise time ~0.15 s, little/no overshoot; steady-state force error < 1.0 Nt
Position-disturbance rejection (table recedes at 0.65 cm/s while holding 5 Nt)Contact never lost; force error ≤ 1.75 Nt except at ramp ends (accelerations)
Simultaneous force step + sinusoidal position (Eq. 17)Force step produces no noticeable position disturbance; loops effectively decoupled
Servo rate 60→80→120 HzForce response improves (less overshoot, better stability); position accuracy unchanged
Peg-in-hole (1 mil clearance, no chamfer)Successful with the controller augmented to 3-DOF planar (X, Y, θ)

Simulated and physical step responses agree well. A persistent ~0.5 Nt, 286 Hz oscillation rides on every force record — the hand sits between the spring-like wrist sensor and the compliant surface, forming an oscillator; the model predicts f_n = (1/2π)√((K_r+K_w)/M_3) ≈ 280 Hz, matching observation. It is above controller bandwidth and so was low-pass filtered (12 Hz) out of the plots, not controlled away.

Limitations & open questions

Author-stated. (1) The method is computationally expensive — full coordinate transforms per servo cycle — which is why earlier workers preferred the cheaper free-joint approximation. (2) No correction was made for the manipulator's nonlinear time-varying dynamics or hand-mass acceleration forces (simulations suggested these were small here). (3) Choosing the constraint frame and natural constraints for a given task "awaits further research" — the work assumes a valid constraint set is given. (4) The high-frequency wrist-hand-surface oscillation is uncontrollable through the software loop. (5) All gains were tuned empirically with no optimality criterion.

What I noticed reading it. The validation is almost entirely 2-DOF planar; the headline "controls position and force simultaneously" is demonstrated on a single force axis + single position axis, and the only multi-DOF result (peg-in-hole) is reported as one successful run with no success-rate statistics. The decoupling claim ("force step produces no noticeable disturbance in position") is read off plots, not quantified. Stability depended critically on an ad-hoc saturation nonlinearity to tame the Coulomb-friction/integral-gain interaction — "without this term the system was stable only when heavily overdamped" — suggesting the clean linear architecture leans on hand-tuned hacks at contact. Most consequentially for downstream theory: the framework presupposes the task's contact mode (which DOFs are force- vs. position-constrained) is known and fixed; it has no machinery for detecting or switching contact state, which is exactly where modern learned predicates and tactile/force sensing re-enter.

Why I care

This is off the language/multimodal theme of the 2026-06-24 batch, but it is deliberately ingested as the root formalism from which all force-aware manipulation descends. The single move that matters for my thesis: partitioning task space into force-controlled and position-controlled directions via the binary compliance selection vector S. Every contact-rich policy in this batch — ForceVLA, Tactile-VLA, FoAR, Reactive Diffusion Policy — is, at bottom, learning a softer, data-driven version of this same position/force decomposition.

For BLADE specifically: BLADE composes long-horizon behavior by learning per-skill contact-primitive controllers with diffusion policies. Raibert & Craig is the classical controller those primitives generalize — a BLADE skill like insert or wipe implicitly enacts a hybrid position/force mode (press normal, move tangent) that this paper specifies in closed form. The big idea I keep returning to is that many manipulation predicates — is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, surface_is_rough, is_grasped — are not visually evaluable; they live in the force/touch channel. This 1981 paper is the first to make force a first-class, separately-controlled signal in the task frame, which is the control-side prerequisite for treating force/touch as a predicate-evaluation channel the way BLADE treats vision. Its key limitation — the contact mode must be known in advance — is precisely the gap that learned predicate invention and tactile sensing are meant to close: deciding which DOFs to force-control is itself a perception/abstraction problem.
