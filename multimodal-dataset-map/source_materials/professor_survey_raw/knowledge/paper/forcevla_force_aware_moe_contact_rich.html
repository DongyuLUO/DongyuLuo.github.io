ForceVLA: Enhancing VLA Models with a Force-aware MoE for Contact-rich Manipulation

Jiawen Yu*, Hairuo Liu*, Qiaojun Yu†, Jieji Ren, Ce Hao, Haitong Ding, Guangyu Huang, Guofan Huang, Yan Song, Panpan Cai, Wenqiang Zhang†, Cewu Lu · Fudan / SJTU / Shanghai Innovation Institute / Shanghai AI Lab / NUS / Shanghai Univ / XJTU / Noematrix · 2025 (arXiv, under review) · arXiv:2505.22159 · PDF

One-liner. ForceVLA bolts a 6-axis force/torque modality onto a π0-style flow-matching VLA by routing the force token through a small Mixture-of-Experts fusion block (FVLMoE) after the pretrained VLM, so the policy can phase-adaptively modulate contact-rich actions (insertion, wiping, peeling) that vision-only VLAs botch under occlusion or misalignment.

Problem & motivation

Vision-Language-Action models (OpenVLA, π0) inherit strong semantic grounding and spatial planning from pretrained VLMs, but contact-rich manipulation is "fundamentally driven by interaction forces," not just pixels. Under visual occlusion or pose error, vision-and-language cues alone leave the policy blind to the one signal that actually disambiguates success: the wrench at the end-effector (Fig 1 shows a VLA without force completing an insertion incorrectly while ForceVLA corrects). Furthermore, force requirements change across task phases — delicate grasp, controlled insertion, compliant surface contact each need different force modulation. The authors argue 6D external force should be a first-class modality, and the open question is where and how to inject it into a pretrained VLA without destroying the VLM's learned representations.

Method

ForceVLA is an end-to-end policy built on the π0 framework: a SigLIP-based, PaliGemma-backed VLM encodes multi-RGB views + instruction into fused features, and a conditional flow-matching action head denoises an Haction-length action chunk of target TCP pose + gripper width. The observation at step t is Ot = {Vtb, Vth, st, ft}: base + hand RGB, proprioceptive state st ∈ R7 (TCP pose as Cartesian (x,y,z) + Euler (α,β,γ) plus gripper width), and the estimated external wrench ft = {ftx, fty, ftz, mtx, mty, mtz} ∈ R6 in the world frame.

FVLMoE — the core fusion module (Fig 3). Rather than feed force into the VLM input, ForceVLA injects it after the VLM, positioned "akin to a higher-level cortical association area." The raw wrench fraw ∈ R6 is linearly projected by φF into a single force token EF = φF(fraw) ∈ RDmodel and appended to the VLM's visual-linguistic tokens: Ein = [EVL; EF] ∈ R(NVL+1)×Dmodel.

Routing and fusion. Ein first passes a shared encoder layer (LayerNorm + multi-head self-attention with Nheads heads + FFN) for holistic refinement, yielding Eenc. It then enters a sparse MoE layer of E = 4 expert MLPs with a gating network doing top-k = 1 routing per token (each token sent to its single most-appropriate expert). MoE output is added back via a residual connection (Efused), then linearly projected to the action-expert dimensionality.

Injection into the flow head. The final Haction tokens of EFVLMoE form a guidance sub-sequence GFVLMoE ∈ RHaction×Da, combined by element-wise addition with Ssuffix (from the VLM's processing of proprioceptive state st) and the noisy action trajectory atτ at denoising step τ. This additive injection lets the fused contact-aware features modulate the predicted action at every flow step. The key architectural claim, justified in the ablation (§5.4): force must enter post-VLM to preserve pretrained representations — early fusion corrupts them.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: ForceVLA-Data, a new self-collected dataset of synchronized vision + proprioception + 6-axis force/torque over 5 contact-rich tasks (bottle pumping, plug insertion, USB insertion, whiteboard wiping, cucumber peeling); 244 trajectories ≈ 140k synchronized timesteps, ~50 expert demos per task; images resized to 480×640 and normalized, actions as target TCP pose + gripper width. To be open-sourced.
  • Hardware / simulator: Flexiv Rizon 7-DoF arm with Dahuan adaptive gripper (wrench estimated on TCP); two RGB-D cameras — static third-person RealSense D435 (1280×720, 30 FPS) + wrist-mounted D415 (640×480, 30 FPS); teleoperation via Quest3 VR. Real-world only, no simulator.
  • Baselines: π0-base w/o F and w/ F (force concatenated to state input), π0-fast w/o F and w/ F. The "w/ F" variants isolate the value of the FVLMoE fusion vs. naive force concatenation.
  • Compute: not reported.

Results

Headline: ForceVLA reaches 60.5% average success across the five tasks vs. 37.3% for the strong π0-base w/ F baseline — a +23.2% absolute improvement, with up to 80% success on individual tasks (e.g., plug/USB insertion). Limitations & open questions

Author-stated:

  • Uses estimated external wrench (from the Flexiv arm), not a dedicated high-fidelity F/T sensor — may miss precision in extreme-haptic-sensitivity scenarios.
  • Validation is predominantly on an integrated, high-cost force-torque platform; broader accessibility on cheaper external/retrofitted sensors is "actively being assessed," not demonstrated.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Tiny-N statistics. The ablation table reports success as blunt percentages over very small trial counts (10–20 trials/task; the ablation appears to be on a single task), so a "45% vs 55%" gap is ≈1–2 successes — no variance or seeds reported anywhere, unlike BLADE's mean±std over 3 seeds. The 0% MoE-before-VLM result is striking but also rests on this thin base.
  • Force as a single token. The entire 6D wrench at one timestep is compressed into one token via a linear projection — no force history / temporal window, despite the paper's own framing that contact dynamics evolve over a phase. The "phase-aware" claim is carried by the MoE routing, not by any explicit temporal force encoding.
  • No simulation, no public benchmark. All five tasks are self-collected on one robot; there is no comparison on any external contact-rich benchmark, so cross-lab reproducibility and the 23.2% headline are hard to situate against other force-aware VLAs.
  • Generalization is mixed. ForceVLA actually loses on Unstable Socket and only ties on two of five settings; the averaged "+11pt over best baseline" is driven mostly by Visual Occlusion (90%), which is exactly the case force should help — consistent, but the win is narrower than the abstract implies.
  • Why top-k=1 with only 4 experts? No analysis of whether the gating actually specializes experts per task phase (the motivating claim) vs. per task or arbitrarily — the routing interpretability that would validate "phase-aware" is asserted, not shown.

Why I care

This is squarely on the thesis that many manipulation predicates — is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, surface_is_clean, is_seated — are not visually evaluable and live in force/touch. ForceVLA is direct evidence: vision-only VLAs fail insertion under occlusion precisely because the success signal is a wrench, not a pixel, and adding force recovers it (Visual Occlusion 30/60% → 90%). For BLADE, this connects to a limitation I flagged in my own summary: BLADE's abstraction layer is "purely categorical" and "continuous parameters (pour amount, grasp pose, force) sit entirely inside the diffusion policy." ForceVLA shows the opposite design point — force is a first-class input to an end-to-end flow policy with no symbolic structure at all. The interesting synthesis BLADE could pursue: keep the symbolic plan, but let a force-aware controller (or a force-grounded predicate classifier) decide is_inserted as a precondition/effect, rather than leaving force implicit in the controller. ForceVLA's empirical message — force must enter after the pretrained VLM, not before — is also a useful prior for anyone retrofitting force into a foundation-model backbone.

It is the structure-vs-scale debate again: ForceVLA is firmly on the "scale + end-to-end + a smart fusion module" side, a clean foil to BLADE's structured-abstraction stance. Tracking it is about understanding what end-to-end force fusion buys before deciding what a symbolic force-predicate layer must beat.

Quotable

ForceVLA introduces FVLMoE, a force-aware Mixture-of-Experts fusion module that dynamically integrates pretrained visual-language embeddings with real-time 6-axis force feedback during action decoding. — Abstract
6D external force sensed at the robot's end-effector should be treated as a first-class modality and formally integrated into the action expert module to enable phase-aware action generation. — §1, Introduction / p.2
Notably, the MoE-based early fusion failed entirely (0% success rate), highlighting that altering the input representations of a pretrained VLM disrupts its learned feature distributions and undermines its capacity to process visual-linguistic signals effectively. — §5.4, Ablation Studies / p.8
