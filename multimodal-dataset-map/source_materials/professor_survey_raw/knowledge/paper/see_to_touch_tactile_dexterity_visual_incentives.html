See to Touch: Learning Tactile Dexterity through Visual Incentives

Irmak Guzey, Yinlong Dai, Ben Evans, Soumith Chintala, Lerrel Pinto · New York University / Meta AI Research · 2023 (arXiv:2309.12300; ICRA 2024) · arXiv:2309.12300 · PDF

One-liner. TAVI learns contact-rich, multi-fingered dexterous policies via online RL where the action uses both tactile and image input but the reward comes purely from a learned visual optimal-transport match to a single human demonstration — sidestepping the fact that raw tactile signals are too ambiguous to reward task completion on their own.

Problem & motivation

Multi-fingered hands need tactile sensing for precise, contact-rich fingertip manipulation, but tactile-only control is a dead end for reward: the touch signal from picking up a slender object looks almost identical to a pinch grasp, so it "lack[s] the necessary cues to reason about objects' spatial location." Pure offline imitation (T-DEX) needs large demonstration sets, requires densely covering object configurations, and cannot recover from execution errors. TAVI's insight: keep tactile in the policy input for fine control, but compute the reward from a learned visual representation, so the agent gets a strong, spatially-grounded incentive while still adapting tactile behavior online from a single demonstration.

Method

TAVI = Tactile Adaptation from Visual Incentives. Three pieces, illustrated in Fig 1: a learned visual+tactile representation, an optimal-transport (OT) reward over the visual features, and online RL that adapts a residual policy.

1. Representation learning (vision + tactile). Both encoders map high-dimensional observations to a low-dimensional latent (mitigating the need for explicit state estimation). The image encoder is trained on task demonstrations with two losses: an InfoNCE time-contrastive loss (LNCE, following Time-Contrastive Networks — nearby observations are positives) plus a change-prediction loss Δ̂(zt, zt+k) regressing the multi-step change in robot proprioceptive state between latents (LΔ = ||st+k - st - Δ̂||, with k=5). Final loss L = LNCE + λ LΔ. The tactile encoder is the pretrained T-DEX encoder (2.5 hours of self-supervised tactile play data), used off-the-shelf.

2. OT reward from visual features only. Building on FISH, given an expert trajectory and a robot trajectory, encode both to latents and solve an optimal-transport problem with a pairwise cost matrix C; the transport plan μ* yields a time-invariant reward rOT = -∑ Ct,t' μ*t,t' (Eq 2). Crucially the OT reward uses only the visual latent; the authors found that including tactile in the reward "results in convergence to sub-optimal solutions" (the agent pinches fingers together to game the touch signal). A second design choice (differing from FISH): match the last 10 frames of the robot rollout to the last frame of the expert — a sparse final-frame match that avoids the degenerate start-to-end matching that all-frames OT produces.

3. Online residual RL. They use FISH-style imitation: the base policy is an open-loop replay of the single expert demonstration; a learned residual policy π(ot, τt) (image + tactile in) adds corrective offsets, trained with DrQv2/DDPG to maximize rOT. Observations are augmented with random resized crops. Exploration is restricted to task-relevant subsets of the action space per task (e.g., only base joints of fingers for peg insertion), with additive OU noise to prevent motor jitter. Long-horizon tasks (Fig 4) are handled by training separate sub-policies and concatenating them zero-shot.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: No public benchmark; six self-collected contact-rich dexterous tasks — Peg Insertion, Sponge Flipping, Eraser Turning, Bowl Unstacking, Plier Picking, Mint Opening (Fig 2). One human demonstration per task collected via the HOLO-DEX VR framework; data subsampled to 10Hz.
  • Hardware / simulator: Real robot only — 6-DOF Jaco arm + 16-DOF AllegroHand (4 fingers), fitted with 15 XELA uSkin tactile sensors (each 4×4 tri-axial = 240 force readings total), plus a single RGB camera. Online RL trained directly on hardware (~30 min / task).
  • Baselines: T-DEX (offline NN imitation w/ tactile+image encoders); BC-BeT (Behavior Transformers); Tactile-Only (tactile features in the OT reward); Image+Tactile Reward (both in reward); AVI (FISH with no tactile in the policy at all). Visual-representation ablations: Contrastive-Only, Joint-Difference-Only, BYOL, end-to-end BC, ImageNet-Pretrained ResNet-18.
  • Compute: not reported (RL agent DrQv2; Adam, lr 1e-4, batch 256, replay buffer 150k, 1000 exploration steps — Table V).

Results

Headline: TAVI averages 73% success across the six tasks, vs 16% for T-DEX, 0% for BC-BeT, 38% Tactile-Only, 35% Image+Tactile-Reward, 31% AVI — a 108% relative gain over policies using tactile+visual rewards, and 135% over policies without tactile observational input. Where it wins and loses:

  • Tactile in the policy matters most on precision tasks: AVI (no tactile) nearly matches TAVI on peg insertion (6 vs 8) and plier picking (6 vs 7) where force feedback is less critical, but collapses to 0 on eraser turning and 1 on mint opening — tasks needing care not to over-exert force.
  • Tactile in the reward hurts: Tactile-Only and Image+Tactile-Reward each roughly halve success vs visual-only OT on several tasks, confirming the central thesis that touch is a poor reward signal even when it is a good observation.
  • Visual representation ablation (Table II, 4 tasks): the full TAVI encoder averages 7.75/10 vs 6/10 for BYOL (best baseline) and 4–4.5/10 for Contrastive-Only / Pretrained ResNet — ~56% improvement in four of the tasks over prior representation methods.
  • Generalization (Fig 3): on unseen objects, bowl unstacking holds at 50% and peg insertion at 57% — partial transfer, degrading when shape/mass change substantially.
  • Camera sensitivity (Table IV): bowl unstacking drops 9→6→2→1 /10 as the camera moves None→2cm→2-10cm→12cm+orientation, because the visual encoder is not multi-view consistent.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • The observation representation lacks historical context; a transformer could help but needs solving training with limited demonstration data.
  • Performance is "very dependant on the camera view" — reward depends on the match between expert and robot trajectory; large camera shifts break it (Table IV).
  • The exploration mechanism requires manually knowing which action-space dimensions to enable per task; automating this would cut domain expertise.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Tiny statistics. Every success number is a count out of 10 rollouts with no seeds/std reported — a much weaker statistical claim than the headline percentages suggest. The 73% vs 35% "108% improvement" rests on 6×10 trials.
  • Single demonstration, but heavy priors. "Only one demonstration" is true for the OT target, but the method also leans on a T-DEX tactile encoder pretrained on 2.5h of play and hand-specified per-task action subspaces — the one-demo framing understates the upfront task engineering.
  • Reward never sees touch, so force-sensitive success is indirect. Tasks like eraser turning succeed because the visual end state correlates with the right force application; there is no explicit force/contact term in the objective. For predicates like is_screwed_tight where the visual end-state is ambiguous, this visual-reward trick would not transfer.
  • Long-horizon claim is thin. Sub-policy concatenation (Fig 4) is shown qualitatively on one Sponge→Plier→Mint sequence, with no success-rate table.
  • The XELA tactile is used raw through a frozen encoder — no finetuning on these six tasks; how much headroom a task-adapted tactile encoder leaves is untested.

Why I care

This is squarely on-theme for the batch thesis that many manipulation predicates live in touch/force, not vision. But See to Touch is a sharp, almost contrarian data point on that thesis: its core empirical finding is that touch is an excellent controller input yet a poor reward / success signal, because tactile readings don't disambiguate object spatial state. That is exactly the boundary I care about for BLADE's predicate-grounding question: a predicate like is_grasped may need tactile to achieve reliably but is most cheaply evaluated from vision — whereas is_screwed_tight or surface_is_rough have no clean visual end-state and would break TAVI's visual-reward recipe. TAVI sits at the low-level-control layer (diffusion-policy/RL controllers below an abstraction), the same slot BLADE's diffusion policies occupy; one could imagine BLADE's contact-primitive controllers trained TAVI-style with visual OT incentives instead of pure BC. The reliance on hand-picked per-task action subspaces is a nice contrast to BLADE's learned contact-primitive segmentation — TAVI bakes in the structure BLADE recovers automatically. Mostly relevant as a controller/ representation building block, not as a planning or predicate-learning paper.
