FACTR: Force-Attending Curriculum Training for Contact-Rich Policy Learning

Jason Jingzhou Liu*, Yulong Li*, Kenneth Shaw, Tony Tao, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Deepak Pathak · Carnegie Mellon University · 2025 (arXiv preprint, 24 Apr 2025) · arXiv:2502.17432 · PDF

One-liner. FACTR stops a behavior-cloning policy from ignoring its force channel by blurring away the vision channel early in training and gradually un-blurring it — a curriculum that forces the network to learn from external joint torques first, yielding +40% success on unseen objects in contact-rich tasks; it ships alongside a low-cost actuated leader-follower teleoperation rig that relays follower-arm torques back to the operator's hands.

Problem & motivation

Contact-rich tasks (box lifting, dough rolling, prehensile pivoting) hinge on force feedback, yet two gaps keep force underused. (1) Data side: popular low-cost leader-follower teleoperation rigs (GELLO, ALOHA) are passive and unilateral — the operator never feels the follower arm's contact forces, so they can't teleoperate delicate contact tasks well. (2) Learning side: even when force is recorded and fed as policy input, the policy tends to overfit to vision and ignore force. The paper's diagnosis is that contact-force signals are weakly discriminative — they sit near zero for long stretches when the arm isn't touching anything — so gradient descent latches onto the always-informative visual modality and the force encoder never learns. This caps a vision-only policy's ability to generalize to objects whose appearance/geometry change but whose force signature is the discriminating cue (e.g., lifting boxes of varied color but similar mechanics). FACTR attacks both gaps: a force-feedback teleop system and a force-attending training curriculum.

Method

Bilateral force-feedback teleoperation (Sec. III). A low-cost actuated leader arm (off-the-shelf servos, 3D-printed, kinematically equivalent to the follower) relays the follower's external joint torques back as motor torques the operator feels. The leader control law (Eq. 5) sums five terms: τ_feedback (relays follower external torque τ_ext, scaled by a mediating constant μ_f, with PD damping — Eq. 1), τ_null (null-space projection (I − J†J) to a user-set resting posture for customizable redundancy resolution — Eq. 3, Fig. 3), τ_grav (gravity compensation via RNEA inverse dynamics — Eq. 4), τ_friction, and τ_limit (artificial potential keeping the leader inside the follower's joint limits). Gripper force feedback uses the follower gripper's servo current as a proxy (Eq. 2), EMA-smoothed. Crucially, only external torques are relayed, so the operator doesn't feel the follower's own inertia/friction during free motion.

Base policy (Sec. IV-A). An encoder-decoder action-chunking transformer (ACT-style). A pretrained ViT f_φ tokenizes image I_t into vision tokens z_t^V; an MLP force encoder g_ψ maps the external joint torque τ_t to a single force token z_t^F. Concatenated tokens go through a transformer encoder, then k action tokens cross-attend to vision+force in the decoder, projected to future joint-position targets q̂_{t:t+k}. Trained by BC with MSE on joint targets (Eq. 6).

Force-Attending Curriculum (Sec. IV-B, Algorithm 1, Fig. 4). The core idea. A blurring operator with scale σ_n, large at the start and decayed to ~0 over N steps, corrupts the visual branch so the force branch dominates early gradients. Two operator families: Gaussian blur and downsampling (MaxPool+nearest interp). Two spaces: pixel-space β_P(I, σ) blurs the raw image, or latent-space β_L(z^V, σ) blurs the vision tokens. As σ → ∞, all visual inputs collapse toward the same tensor, so the only way to reduce loss is to learn from force — then vision is gradually unveiled. Five schedulers for σ_n (constant / linear / cosine / exponential / step), with an optional warm-up that fixes σ_0 for some steps to let the randomly-initialized force encoder catch up. Appendix VII grounds the intuition in Neural Tangent Kernels: a Gaussian-blurred input acts as a low-pass filter; in the σ → ∞ limit the NTK matrix becomes rank-1 (all-ones), so the network can only fit a single global visual output, redirecting gradient capacity to the force encoder.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected real-robot demos, 50 teleoperated demonstrations per task across four contact-rich tasks — Box Lift (bimanual, lift+balance a box ≥2s), Non-Prehensile Pivot (flip an item against a fixture corner by 90°), Fruit Pick and Place (grasp soft/delicate fruit, place in bowl), Rolling Dough (roll dough into a cylinder ≥8s). Train vs. test object splits evaluate generalization (Fig. 5); 5–10 trials per object.
  • Hardware / simulator: Real-world only. Franka Panda arm(s) with OpenManipulator-X grippers; external joint torque from the Panda's built-in torque sensing. Front ZED2 camera + wrist cameras (RGB). Low-cost actuated leader arm (servos + 3D-printed parts); teleop on the KUKA LBR iiwa also mentioned as a compatible torque-sensing arm.
  • Baselines: ACT (Vision-Only) [32]; ACT (Vision+Force) [13,14] — same force input as FACTR but no curriculum; FACTR (Ours) — ACT + force-attending curriculum (default: latent-space, Gaussian blur, linear scheduler). Teleop user study compares against an un-actuated leader-follower with mechanical joint regulation (GELLO-like).
  • Compute: not reported.

Results

Headline: FACTR improves unseen-object success by an average of +40.0% over the same policy with force-but-no-curriculum, and the abstract states +43% generalization improvement over baseline approaches without a curriculum. On test objects, vision-only averages 21.3% success, naive vision+force 61.2%, and FACTR 87.5% (Sec. V-C).

On train objects most policies tie near-ceiling, except vision-only fails Rolling Dough completely (0/10) — it "smashes the dough without any rolling actions" because the oscillatory rolling motion is visually near-indistinguishable while the torque norm has a distinctive oscillatory pattern (Fig. 8). Recovery (Table I): after a lifted box is knocked down, force-attending policies recover on test objects 27/30 (FACTR) vs. 16/30 (V+F) vs. 4/30 (vision-only); vision-only policies "remain static after the box is knocked down," whereas force policies detect the torque drop and retry. Mode switching (Fig. 9): attention visualization shows FACTR's action tokens shift attention toward force exactly at contact onset. Teleop user study (Fig. 7): the force-feedback rig gives +64.7% task completion rate, −37.4% completion time, +83.3% subjective ease of use over the un-actuated baseline. Where it loses / is flat: the curriculum-parameter ablation (Table II, pivoting task, 5 test objects) shows no uniform winner across the 2 spaces × 2 operators × 5 schedulers grid (scores cluster 15–21/25); the authors conclude FACTR is "relatively robust" to these choices, i.e., the specific operator/scheduler doesn't matter much — only that some decaying curriculum beats a fixed scale.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors (Sec. VI):

  • External joint-torque sensing on the follower arm has limited precision; too noisy for subtle fine-grained force adjustments. They suggest high-resolution tactile sensors / haptic gloves to improve feedback fidelity.
  • The method assumes an arm with external joint-torque sensing; adapting to an end-effector force/torque sensor is future work.
  • Many hyperparameters (blur operator, scheduler) require per-application tuning; adaptive/self-tuning curricula are flagged as future work.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Small-N statistics. Headline test-object counts are out-of-N successes (e.g., 14/15, 8/10), not rates with seeds/CIs, and the central ablation (Table II) is a single hard task (pivoting), 5 objects × 5 trials = 25 attempts per cell. The "robust to parameters" claim rests on differences of 2–5 successes out of 25, well within noise.
  • The curriculum is a regularizer, not force-specific. Blurring vision to force attention onto a weak modality is a general co-training trick (cf. their cited [28] on multimodal training); nothing ties it to force per se — it should equally rebalance audio, tactile, or any under-attended channel. That's a feature, but the paper doesn't test it, so the generality is asserted, not shown.
  • No latent-space "what does vision still contribute" probe. If the final policy must see unblurred vision (their own ablation says a fully-blurred final policy underperforms), how much of the +40% is force vs. just better-regularized vision? A force-input-zeroed ablation at test time would isolate that; it's absent.
  • Force here is joint torque (proprioceptive), not tactile/contact sensing — coarse, and as the authors admit, near-zero off-contact. The discriminative signal is essentially "am I touching / how hard," which is exactly the binary is_grasped/in_contact predicate flavor (see below), not fine texture/material.

Why I care

FACTR is squarely on the batch thesis: many manipulation predicates live in force, not pixels. Its cleanest result — vision-only policies cannot roll dough or detect a dropped box because the visual observation is near-constant while the torque norm carries the signal (Figs. 8, 9) — is direct evidence that mode-switch predicates like in_contact, is_grasped, and is_lifted are force-evaluable, not visually evaluable. That's the exact gap I flagged in BLADE: BLADE's contact-primitive segmentation already keys off gripper state, and its "Limitations" notes caging grasps / contact-rich tasks need richer contact detection, and that continuous force parameters sit opaquely inside the diffusion policy. FACTR is the low-level, force-channel counterpart — it shows the policy can learn to attend to force, but as an entangled BC representation, not a symbolic predicate. The open question this sharpens for my line: could a FACTR-style force encoder feed a learned classifier for force-grounded predicates (is_grasped(x), surface_is_rough, is_screwed_tight) that the planner reasons over, rather than leaving force implicit in the controller? That would let BLADE's predicate layer cover the non-visual half of the state.

FACTR's attention-rebalancing curriculum is also a generic tool for any multimodal policy that under-uses a weak modality — relevant to the audio/tactile VLA papers in this batch where the same overfit-to-vision failure recurs.
