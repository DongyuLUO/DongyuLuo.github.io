VLA-Touch: Enhancing Vision-Language-Action Models with Dual-Level Tactile Feedback

Jianxin Bi*, Kevin Yuchen Ma, Ce Hao, Mike Zheng Shou, Harold Soh* · NUS (Dept. of Computer Science / Smart Systems Institute) · 2025 · arXiv:2507.17294 · PDF

One-liner. VLA-Touch bolts tactile sensing onto a frozen VLA (RDT-1B) at two levels without any VLA fine-tuning: a pretrained tactile-language model (Octopi) turns GelSight signals into words for a GPT-4o planner, and a BRIDGeR interpolant diffusion controller refines the VLA's action chunk using low-dimensional tactile force signals — getting touch into both the planning and control loops of a generalist policy that was never trained on touch.

Problem & motivation

State-of-the-art VLA models are trained almost entirely on vision, language, and action data, with no intrinsic mechanism to process tactile signals. This limits them on contact-rich tasks where object properties (hardness, surface roughness, whether a sealed cup is full) and contact dynamics (friction, contact events) are visually ambiguous — they live in touch, not pixels. The naive fix — fine-tune the VLA on tactile data — is blocked by the absence of large multi-modal (vision + language + action + touch) datasets. The authors ask: how can an agent acquire and plan with tactile information when most pretrained models have never seen touch, and how do you inject touch into a VLA whose backbone has no tactile input modality? Their answer is a deliberately modular, loosely-coupled scaffold that probes the utility of touch in both planning and control without touching the base VLA.

Method

A dual-level framework (Fig 1), explicitly analogized to the human neural system: a VLM task planner (prefrontal cortex), a tactile-language model (secondary somatosensory cortex), and a tactile-augmented controller (posterior parietal cortex). Base VLA is Robot Diffusion Transformer (RDT-1B); the method is claimed to generalize across VLA architectures.

1. Tactile-assisted task planning (high level). A language-conditioned VLM planner (GPT-4o) is given task goal g, scene image, and a system prompt declaring the robot's capabilities (one arm + gripper + a tactile sensor that can classify hardness, roughness, surface patterns), a one-sentence-per-step response format, and an interaction protocol promising feedback after each action. It emits one elemental manipulation instruction I_k at a time. When an instruction completes (or max steps exceeded), tactile signals o^m_{t-n:t} from the GelSight are converted by Octopi [35] — a pretrained tactile-language model for GelSight, processing a 6-frame tactile sequence — into a linguistic description L^m_t of contacted-object properties (roughness, hardness). That text is fed back to GPT-4o to generate the next instruction. This interleaved plan/execute loop (Algorithm 1) repeats until the goal is met. For force specifically (Octopi is not pretrained for force), a marker-tracking algorithm produces estimated force vectors plus baseline reference values handed to GPT-4o as calibration points.

2. Tactile policy refinement (low level). Because RDT does not natively ingest tactile signals, VLA-Touch wraps an interpolant-based diffusion controller (BRIDGeR [44]) around the VLA's output. Unlike standard diffusion that denoises from Gaussian noise, BRIDGeR uses a stochastic interpolant to diffuse from an informative source distribution — here the VLA-generated action chunk — toward the expert distribution, which gives strong refinement in few steps. The controller π_I(â_{t:t+T_i} | a_{t:t+T_i}, s_t, m_t) conditions on the source action chunk, current state s_t (RGB + proprioception), and a tactile signal m_t. The tactile force m_t = (X, Y, M) is computed from a 7×9 GelSight marker array: per-marker displacement → per-marker force vectors → aggregated into a summed force vector + magnitude (following [9]'s aggregation, for noise reduction and compactness). RGB is embedded with a frozen DinoV2. The controller is trained by supervised MSE (Eq. 3) on paired (VLA-generated source action, expert action) data: states sampled from expert demos, source actions generated by the VLA. At inference a sliding window refines non-overlapping segments sequentially — the first 48 steps of each 64-step VLA chunk are refined and executed, then the VLA regenerates from the endpoint. Refined actions execute at ≤8 Hz via a Cartesian PD controller (impedance controller for the Mango Peeling task).

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: No public benchmark. Three self-collected real-world contact-rich tasks — Cup (pick a lidded cup, sense via touch whether it holds water, place at the matching location without spilling), Wipe (touch-select the smoother of two sponges, then wipe ink off a plate), Peel (touch-identify the softer/harder of two mangoes, peel the selected one). Interpolant-controller training data collected via kinesthetic teaching: Cup 100 episodes (~8 min), Wipe 120 episodes (~15 min), Peel 180 episodes (~30 min); sensor streams (2 cameras, GelSight, proprioception) logged at 10 Hz; each episode labeled with a linguistic instruction.
  • Hardware / simulator: Real-world only. Franka Emika Panda + Robotiq 2F-140 gripper. One GelSight Mini tactile sensor on one finger. Two RealSense cameras (fixed overhead scene cam + wrist-mounted). Base VLA: RDT-1B. Tactile-language model: Octopi. Planner: GPT-4o.
  • Baselines: Planning — GPT-4o (scene image only, no tactile), GPT-4o + raw tactile images, GPT-4o + Octopi (ours). Control — vanilla RDT, RDT + RNN/LSTM residual controller, RDT + Interpolant controller (ours). System ablations — w/o Planning (GPT-4o plan only), w/o Control (RDT manipulation only), plus interpolant-controller modality ablations w/o Touch and w/o Vision.
  • Compute: Single RTX 4090 (inference workstation, and fine-tuning). RDT first fine-tuned on unrelated Franka data (100k steps, ~80 hrs) to align action spaces, then per-task fine-tuned (~20k steps, ~16 hrs each) without tactile modality before the interpolant controller is added.

Results

All numbers are real-world, reported as count-of-success out of 20 trials. Headline claims: linguistic tactile feedback improves planning efficiency by up to 40%; the tactile-incorporated controller achieves up to 35% higher manipulation success vs vision-only VLA baselines; the full dual-level system gives up to 35% higher task success than tactile-in-planning-only or tactile-in-control-only.

Planning (Fig 3C, 20 trials, success rate). Vision-only GPT-4o is non-committal and near random on tactile properties. Raw tactile images let GPT-4o nail roughness (100%) but it struggles with force (50%) and hardness (60%). Octopi linguistic feedback: force 90%, roughness 100%, hardness 75% — structured language beats raw tactile images for the off-the-shelf VLM on force and hardness, though raw images edge it on hardness in their own appendix study.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Gripper control setup differs from Octopi's pretraining dataset, creating contact-measurement discrepancies that compromise tactile interpretation — observed especially for object hardness.
  • Generalization is studied only across object positioning / target locations (cup heights, ink patterns, mango specimens), not across tasks; cross-task generalization would need substantially more training.
  • The interpolant controller runs at 8 Hz and does not exploit high-frequency tactile signals (≥25 Hz) that could help dynamic interactions.
  • Future work flagged: active grasping via combined visual-tactile feedback; event-triggered inference to cut latency during stable contact periods; task-agnostic policy refinement that scales to more tasks.
  • In the appendix they concede natural-language tactile descriptions can underperform the raw continuous classifier output on subtle hardness differences (mango: language 60% vs continuous 75%) — language is a lossy bottleneck for fine physical properties.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Statistics are thin. Every headline is count-out-of-20 on three bespoke tasks, no seeds or variance, no public benchmark. "Up to 35%" aggregates the most favorable task. Hard to compare against anything.
  • Two heavyweight dependencies, frozen. The planning level is entirely GPT-4o + Octopi; failure modes (Octopi mis-classifying hardness because of the gripper mismatch) cascade silently into planning. The "modular scaffold" framing is honest but it means the contribution is more integration-engineering than a new learned representation.
  • The force path is a hack. Octopi can't do force, so they bolt on a marker-tracking estimator and hand GPT-4o reference magnitudes (empty cup ~0.55 N, full cup ~1.1 N) as in-context calibration. Clever, but those reference values look task-specific and hand-tuned, not learned.
  • Tactile is aggregated to (X, Y, M). The whole 7×9 marker field collapses to a 3-D force at the controller — deliberately compact, but throws away spatial/shear structure that contact-rich tasks like peeling presumably care about. An untested assumption that the summary force suffices.
  • Residual controller occasionally underperforms vanilla RDT (Cup pick 7 vs 9), which undercuts "any tactile controller helps" — the win is specifically the interpolant formulation, not tactile conditioning per se.

Why I care

This is squarely on-thesis for the line I keep flagging from BLADE: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — is_full(cup), surface_is_rough(sponge), is_soft(mango) live in touch and force, not pixels. VLA-Touch is a clean existence proof that these touch-resident predicates can be lifted into a symbolic / linguistic planning loop: Octopi converts contact into words, GPT-4o plans over those words. That is exactly the predicate-grounding-in-touch move BLADE's visual classifiers can't make. Where BLADE auto-labels visual predicate classifiers from every frame and composes diffusion-policy bodies via bi-level planning, VLA-Touch instead interleaves an LLM planner with a frozen VLA + an interpolant refiner — no learned symbolic operators, no preconditions/effects, replanning driven by linguistic tactile feedback rather than a fast-forward heuristic over learned predicates. The interesting gap: their "tactile property" feedback (hard/soft/rough) is essentially an ad hoc predicate read out of a frozen tactile-language model, never inducted as a reusable symbol with pre/eff semantics. A BLADE-style pipeline that invents tactile predicates (is_full, is_screwed_tight) from touch-language data and grounds them as classifiers for bi-level planning would strictly subsume this — that is a concrete project. Their controller layer (BRIDGeR interpolant refining a frozen VLA chunk) is also a direct foil to BLADE's per-behavior diffusion policies: refinement-of-a-foundation-policy vs train-a-controller-per-behavior. Solidly relevant; not a competitor, a complementary touch-grounding instance.

Quotable

We hypothesize that tactile feedback benefits contact-rich manipulation at two levels: 1) at the planning level, tactile feedback enables reasoning about object properties such as hardness and surface roughness that are visually ambiguous; and 2) at the control level, tactile sensing informs contact dynamics including surface friction and contact event detection for precise manipulation. — §1 / p.1
Current VLA models are predominantly trained on visual, language, and action data, with no intrinsic mechanisms to process or reason over tactile inputs; this fundamentally limits their ability to leverage tactile feedback. — §1 / p.1
