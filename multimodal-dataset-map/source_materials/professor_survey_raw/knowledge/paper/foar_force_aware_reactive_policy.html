FoAR: Force-Aware Reactive Policy for Contact-Rich Robotic Manipulation

Zihao He*, Hongjie Fang*, Jingjing Chen, Hao-Shu Fang†, Cewu Lu† · Shanghai Jiao Tong University · 2025 (arXiv:2411.15753v2; ICRA 2025) · arXiv:2411.15753 · PDF

One-liner. FoAR bolts high-frequency (100 Hz) force/torque sensing onto the RISE point-cloud diffusion policy and lets a learned future-contact predictor gate when the force channel is allowed to influence actions — so noisy F/T data helps during the wipe/peel/chop and stops poisoning the non-contact grasp/move phases — while a reactive controller nudges the end-effector toward more contact when forces read too low, all under plain position control.

Problem & motivation

Vision-only policies can't see contact: RGB and point clouds look nearly identical just before and just after the eraser touches the whiteboard (Fig 1 left), so the policy can't tell whether it is actually pressing. Force/torque is the obvious complementary signal — contact produces force, directly and in real time. But prior force-augmented policies ([21] ForceMimic, [31] TacDiffusion-style hybrid force/position, [46] impedance-feedforward) fuse F/T with vision throughout the whole trajectory, ignoring that force is sparsely activated: in a wiping task only the wipe phase is contact-rich, and during the pick-up/place phases the raw sensor noise actively degrades the policy. Many of these methods also sidestep the problem by assuming the object is already grasped or rigidly fixed to the arm. FoAR's premise: the policy should learn to modulate force usage by predicted contact, not fuse it uniformly.

Method

FoAR extends RISE [44], a real-world imitation policy that maps a single-view RGB-D point cloud pt to an action chunk at:t+Ta via a sparse-3D (Minkowski) encoder + transformer + diffusion head. FoAR adds a force/torque stream and a contact-gated fusion (Fig 2).

1. Two encoders. The point cloud is encoded to a scene feature hst ∈ R512 (RISE backbone). The force/torque history ft−To:t ∈ RTo×6 — sampled at ~100 Hz over To=200 steps (~2 s) — goes through a 3-layer MLP per timestep, then a transformer with sinusoidal temporal positional encodings, producing a force feature hft ∈ R512. Note the two streams run at different rates: F/T history at 100 Hz, action prediction at ~10 Hz.

2. Contact-gated feature fusion (the core idea). A future contact predictor outputs φ(t) ∈ [0,1], the probability that contact will occur in the near future. Fusion is:

ht = [ hst ; φ(t)·hft + (1−φ(t))·h* ]

where h* is a learnable neutral embedding. When contact is likely (φ→1) the real force feature is concatenated to the scene feature; when not (φ→0) the force slot is replaced by the neutral embedding, blocking sensor noise from the non-contact phases. The fused ht conditions the diffusion action head.

3. The contact predictor is deliberately a separate, lightweight head. It takes the current RGB image It (ResNet18 encoder) plus the F/T history (MLP), concatenates, and a linear layer outputs φ. Two design notes from the paper: (i) RGB is used instead of the point cloud because it performs comparably for contact-state classification but is lighter; (ii) crucially, the predictor must not share the policy's sparse-3D encoder — the ablation FoAR (3D-cls) that shares it collapses below RISE, because the predictor wants coarse "is the tool over the board" features while the policy wants precise object/end-effector geometry, and shared attention conflicts.

4. Supervision. Action loss Laction is standard L2 in the diffusion process. The ground-truth future contact label is auto-extracted from demos: within a window [t−2s, t+2s], contact is true if F/T magnitude exceeds a per-task threshold δdemo (read off the demonstrations manually). It supervises φ via BCE Lpredictor. Total loss L = Laction + αLpredictor, α=0.1.

5. Reactive control at deployment (Alg 1). No compliance/impedance/hybrid control — plain end-effector position control. At each inference step the predictor's φ is thresholded by δφ=0.9: if below, the action goes to a non-contact temporal-ensemble buffer; if above (contact imminent), the controller checks current force/torque, and if it reads insufficient (force < δf=8N or torque < δt=5N·m) it nudges the predicted action positions by a small step ε=0.006m along the predicted future direction d (toward more contact). Separate ensemble buffers for contact vs non-contact phases prevent the two regimes from blurring each other's trajectories.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected real-world demos, no public benchmark. Three contact-rich tasks (Fig 3) in two categories — surface force control (Wiping, Wiping General, Peeling) and instantaneous force impact (Chopping). 50 teleoperated demos each for Wiping/Peeling, 40 for Chopping, collected via haptic teleoperation (RH20T-style [14]).
  • Hardware / simulator: Real robot only — Flexiv Rizon arm, Dahuan AG-95 gripper, OptoForce 6-axis F/T sensor mounted between flange and gripper, single Intel RealSense D435 front camera, 45×60×40 cm workspace. Training on 2×A100; data collection/eval on i9-10900K + RTX 3090.
  • Baselines: Vision-only ACT [52], Diffusion Policy [10], RISE [44]; plus three ablation variants — RISE (force-token): F/T as extra transformer tokens; RISE (force-concat): F/T feature concatenated to vision feature; FoAR (3D-cls): predictor shares the policy's 3D encoder. Oracle = the demonstrations themselves (Chopping).
  • Compute: Training on 2×NVIDIA A100, batch size 240, lr 3×10-4, cosine schedule, 2000 warmup steps. Diffusion head: 100 train / 20 inference (DDIM) steps, Ta=20.

Key findings:

  • Naive fusion barely helps and sometimes hurts. RISE (force-token) and (force-concat) only modestly beat vision-only RISE, and force-token actually lowers grasp ASR (non-contact phase) because of noise — direct evidence for the sparse-activation thesis.
  • The fusion gate is the load-bearing idea, not just adding force. FoAR (3D-cls), which adds force but shares the encoder, collapses below RISE (0.175 vs 0.500 on Wiping) — showing the separation of predictor and policy is essential.
  • Ablations (Table III, Wiping): removing reactive control drops Score 0.875→0.650; future-prediction beats contact-detection (detection makes contact late/misses); 100 Hz F/T > 10 Hz (0.875 vs 0.800) > 2 Hz (0.625).
  • Robustness (Table IV): under Rewrite / Move / Rewrite+Move disturbances to Wiping (General), FoAR holds 0.800–0.850 while RISE (force-token) degrades to ~0.45–0.60.
  • Encoder ablation (Table V): a transformer F/T encoder beats a plain MLP (Peeling 0.588 vs 0.426), though even the MLP version beats vision-only RISE (0.293).

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Static, manually-set force/torque thresholds (δf, δt, δdemo) — effective here but expected to struggle in more complex environments.
  • Reactive control is just simple end-effector position control with a fixed nudge; future work wants compliance / hybrid force-position control.
  • Demonstrated only on a single arm; extending to dual-arm, humanoids, and dexterous hands is flagged future work.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Tiny-N statistics. 20 trials per method for surface tasks, but only 10 trials for Chopping — and Chopping is evaluated only for FoAR vs RISE, dropping the four other baselines "to conserve resources." Scores like 0.875 then rest on 20 trials with no confidence intervals on the Score metric (only #Segments has ±).
  • The reactive nudge can launder credit. Reactive control actively pushes the end-effector toward more contact when force is low. That means part of FoAR's contact-consistency win comes from a hand-tuned deployment-time controller (ε=0.006m, Tf=5), not the learned policy alone — the Table III ablation shows ~0.225 of the 0.875 is reactive control. Fair, but it muddies "the policy learned contact."
  • Contact-label leakage risk. The future-contact ground-truth is derived by thresholding the same F/T signal the policy consumes, within a ±2s window. The predictor is essentially learning a smoothed version of the live force reading; under distribution shift (a stiffer object, a different tool) the threshold-defined label may no longer correspond to "useful contact."
  • No language, no composition. Tasks are single-skill (one wipe, one peel); the multi-phase structure is handled implicitly by φ rather than any explicit phase/skill abstraction. There is no long-horizon chaining and no symbolic state.

Why I care

FoAR is a clean, narrow instantiation of a thesis that sits underneath BLADE: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable. Whether the eraser is actually pressing the board, whether the peeler is engaged with the skin — these are is_in_contact-style predicates that live in force, and FoAR's Fig 1 makes the point starkly: RGB before/after contact is nearly identical. BLADE's own limitations section explicitly flags that continuous force parameters sit invisibly inside the diffusion policy and that gripper-state segmentation can't handle contact-rich tasks; FoAR is a concrete demonstration of why that gap matters and one mechanism (a learned contact probability φ) to fill it.

The most BLADE-relevant idea is that φ(t) is a learned, self-supervised contact predicate grounded in force — auto-labeled from demonstrations, exactly the spirit of BLADE's every-frame predicate auto-labeling, but for a predicate vision can't ground. A natural research move: lift FoAR's binary contact gate into BLADE's symbolic layer, so that is_contacting(tool, surface) becomes a force-grounded predicate with preconditions/effects, rather than a continuous knob hidden in the controller. That marries "structured abstractions over imitation-learned skills" with non-visual sensing — the through-line of this whole 2026-06-24 batch.

FoAR is squarely on-theme as a force-grounded skill controller, but note what it is not: no language, no planning, no composition, no predicate invention. It is the low-level layer, comparable in role to the diffusion policies BLADE composes — an upgraded contact-aware version of them.

Related

Papers cited that should likely be ingested next:

  • [44] RISE (Wang et al. 2024) — the point-cloud diffusion-policy backbone FoAR extends; the direct vision-only comparison and ancestor.
  • [10] Diffusion Policy (Chi et al. RSS 2023) — the action head and the canonical IL baseline; same dependency as BLADE.
  • [52] ACT (Zhao et al. RSS 2023) — action-chunking transformer; baseline and the temporal-ensemble source.
  • [31] ForceMimic (Liu et al. 2024) and [46] TacDiffusion (Wu et al. 2024) — force-domain diffusion policies for hybrid/admittance control; the "fuse force throughout" methods FoAR argues against.
  • [21] Adaptive Compliance Policy (Hou et al. 2024) and [53] Admittance Visuomotor Policy (Zhou et al. 2024) — diffusion-guided compliance / admittance control; the control-side alternatives FoAR sidesteps with plain position control.
  • [41] Suomalainen et al. 2022 — A Survey of Robot Manipulation in Contact (RAS) — the framing survey for contact-rich manipulation.

Newly ingested in 2026-06-24 batch — directly relevant:

  • Reactive Diffusion Policy — nearest sibling: a slow-fast visuo-tactile diffusion policy with a reactive fast loop for contact-rich tasks; same "reactive control over a diffusion policy" pattern with tactile instead of F/T.
  • Tactile-Conditioned Diffusion Policy — the force-aware diffusion-policy cousin; direct method comparison on how the contact modality conditions the action head.
  • ForceVLA — the VLA-scale analogue: a force-aware mixture-of-experts for contact-rich tasks; FoAR's contact-gated fusion vs ForceVLA's MoE routing are two answers to "when should force matter."
  • Towards Forceful Robotic Foundation Models (survey) — situates FoAR in the broader push to bring force into foundation-scale policies.
  • Making Sense of Vision and Touch — foundational multimodal (vision+force+proprioception) representation for contact-rich tasks; the lineage FoAR's fusion sits in.
  • Hybrid Position/Force Control (Raibert & Craig 1981) — the classical control method FoAR explicitly avoids (using position control + reactive nudge instead); the control-theory baseline.
  • FACTR and KineDex — other force/tactile policy-learning approaches in cluster F; adjacent points of comparison on how proprioceptive/force signals enter the policy.
