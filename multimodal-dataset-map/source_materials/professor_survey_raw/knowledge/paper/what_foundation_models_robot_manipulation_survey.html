What Foundation Models can Bring for Robot Learning in Manipulation: A Survey

Dingzhe Li, Yixiang Jin, YuHao Sun, Yong A, Hongze Yu, Jun Shi, Xiaoshuai Hao, Peng Hao, Huaping Liu, Xiang Li, Xinde Li, Fuchun Sun, Jianwei Zhang, Bin Fang · 2025 · arXiv preprint (SAGE journal format) · arXiv:2404.18201 · PDF

One-liner. A 47-page survey that argues a single end-to-end robotic foundation model won't get you to general manipulation; instead it proposes an "autonomous-driving-style" modular framework — eight functional modules (Interaction, Pre-/Post-conditions, Hierarchy of Skills, State, Policy, Transition, Data Generation) — and catalogues, module by module, which class of foundation model (LLM / VFM / VLM / LMM / VGM / RFM) plugs in where and what challenges remain.

Problem & motivation

The stated goal is the "universal robot" that manipulates arbitrary objects in unstructured open-world scenes. The authors fix a concrete bar: human manipulation success rates are ~99%, and a robot that can't clear 99% can't replace humans (Kumar 2023). They observe that current Robotic Foundation Models (RFMs, e.g. RT-X) are single-model end-to-end approaches that struggle to hit that bar and demand enormous task-level data. Their thesis: general manipulation needs an overarching framework akin to autonomous driving, where heterogeneous foundation models assume distinct roles across functional modules, rather than one monolithic policy. The survey is positioned against Di Palo et al. (2023), Firoozi et al. (2023), and Xiao et al. (2023) — prior FM-for-robotics surveys that the authors say lack a unifying framework tying modules together.

Survey scope / taxonomy

Two orthogonal taxonomies organize the paper.

(A) Six foundation-model types (their input/role categorization, after Firoozi et al.):

  • LLMs (BERT, GPT-3) — chain-of-thought reasoning; generate policy code / action sequences, drive interaction.
  • VFMs (Visual Foundation Models; SAM) — open-world perception / segmentation.
  • VLMs (GPT-4V, CLIP) — the "cornerstone" of vision–language alignment; treated as a separate category from LMMs.
  • LMMs (Large Multimodal Models; ULIP) — expand beyond vision+language to 3D point cloud and haptic data.
  • VGMs (Visual Generative Models; DALL-E, Sora) — generate 2D images / 3D meshes for scene generation.
  • RFMs (Robotic-specific FMs; RT-X) — end-to-end observation→action policies.

(B) The eight-module general-manipulation framework (Fig 2, the paper's central contribution). The flow: a human/agent issues an instruction → Interaction (I) disambiguates it → Pre-conditions Detection (P) perceives objects + affordances → Hierarchy of Skills (H) decomposes into subgoals → the Skill Execution loop runs State (S) → Policy (Π) → Transition (T) → Post-conditions Detection (P); on failure a Post-hoc Correction module self-corrects, and a Manipulation Data Generation module supplies training data throughout. Each section (3–8) maps one module to its FM contributions.

The "Level 0 (L0)" framing. A recurring conceptual move: they designate the achievable near-term target as L0 — improving existing skills and manipulating rigid objects in static scenes for short-horizon goals with low precision on contact points and forces/torques. Current research "has a high probability of achieving L0," but safety and accuracy remain open. Everything beyond L0 (deformables, dynamic scenes, long-horizon, force-precise contact-rich tasks) is future work.

Policy sub-taxonomy (Sec 7, the part most relevant to VLA work): following RT-2's "Vision-Language-Action" naming, they split policies into VLAC (Vision-Language-Action-Code; LLM emits executable robot code, e.g. Code-as-Policy, VoxPoser), VLAKP (Vision-Language-Action-Key-Pose; outputs discrete key poses for motion planning, e.g. Instruct2Act, ReKep, PerAct, 3D Diffuser Actor), and VLADP (Vision-Language-Action-Dense-Pose; outputs continuous dense trajectories end-to-end, e.g. RT-1, Gato, RT-2, OpenVLA). Coverage

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Surveys RFM benchmarks (Table 3): RLBench, Behavior-1K, VirtualHome, RoboCasa, LIBERO, Robosuite, Sapien, Maniskill2, CALVIN — flagged as non-standardized (different simulators, robots, sensors). Datasets for video learning: Ego-4D, Ego-Exo-4D, RoboVQA, Epic-Kitchens-100, HowTo100M, InternVid. Open X-Embodiment (22 robots, 160k+ tasks), DROID (76k trajectories) for RFM training.
  • Hardware / simulator: Simulator survey — PyBullet, MuJoCo, CoppeliaSim, NVIDIA Omniverse (best rendering), Unity, plus learning-based simulators (Sora, UniSim). Low-cost teleop hardware survey (Table 2): ALOHA, GELLO, HumanPlus, Open-TeleVision, AirExo, UMI, DexCap.
  • Baselines: not applicable (survey). Comparative tables instead: Table 4 (representative policy works with success rates), Table 5 (2D vs 3D methods, Appendix B), Table 6 (hierarchy-of-skills methods, Appendix C).
  • Compute: not reported as a survey-level number; notes RFMs "necessitate extensive data" and "significant computational resources" as a barrier to democratization.

Key insights / classifications

Headline thesis. No single foundation model achieves general manipulation; a modular framework with role-specialized FMs is the proposed path. Per-module FM contributions (Figs 3–8 each give a per-module taxonomy):

Module (Sec)FM types that helpWhat they bring
Interaction (3)LLM, VLM, LMMNatural-language disambiguation, corrective-instruction comprehension; CoT beats fixed question templates (SeeAsk, KNOWNO, Matcha)
Pre/Post-conditions (4)LLM, VFM, VLM, LMMOpen-set affordance + object recognition; task-oriented grasp (GraspGPT, 3DAP); failure analysis (AHA, RobotGPT)
Hierarchy of skills (5)LLM, VFM, VLMLLM-to-PDDL subgoal decomposition, skill-library scoring (SayCan, Text2Motion, LLM+P, GD); video-instruction understanding
State (6)VLM, VFM, LMM3D reconstruction (NeRF/3DGS + CLIP relevancy maps), open-set 6D pose (FoundationPose, FreeZe)
Policy (7)LLM, VLM, VFM, RFMVLAC / VLAKP / VLADP families; RFMs (RT-2, OpenVLA, RDT-1B, π0); FM-assisted RL reward design (Eureka)
Data Generation (8)VLM, VGM, LLMReal-to-Sim + automated scene gen (RoboGen, GenSim, PHYSCENE), sim-to-real, data augmentation (GenAug, ROSIE)

The nine open questions (Sec 9) the authors flag as their own concerns: (1) what is the framework for general manipulation; (2) what learning capability is needed (interactive / few-shot / continual / online); (3) what FMs bring per module; (4) how to use internet-scale video for RFMs (they enumerate six information types extractable from video: pose, affordance, motion, environment-transition dynamics, semantic, spatial/texture); (5) post-conditions detection & post-hoc correction; (6) end-effector design (incl. LLM-designed end-effectors, Stella et al.); (7) dexterous manipulation; (8) whole-body control (force-position hybrid control flagged as "often overlooked"); (9) how to establish a standardized benchmark.

Surprises worth noting. (i) They explicitly invoke force/torque precision as the axis separating L0 from general manipulation — "tasks may necessitate different degrees of precision with respect to contact points and applied forces/torques" — yet the force/tactile content is thin relative to perception/policy. (ii) Appendix D gives a genuinely detailed model-based (non-FM) treatment of contact-rich dexterous manipulation (contact modes, non-smooth dynamics, Anitescu relaxation, contact-explicit vs contact-implicit), conceding that learning-based FMs don't yet handle this. (iii) They argue model-based methods still beat learning-based ones on success rate / precision / safety for specific tasks (Pang et al. 2023) and call integrating the two an important research direction.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors (Sec 9.1.3, Sec 10):

  • The proposed framework is sequential, contrasting with the parallel execution of human manipulation.
  • Both the framework and the surveyed literature are learning-based; model-based methods generalize less but outperform on success/precision/safety for specific tasks — integration is unaddressed.
  • The framework is grounded in a learning-based + general-manipulation definition; brain-like cognitive frameworks "should also be explored."
  • Per-module residual challenges (Sec 10): interaction needs multimodal (gesture/action) input; hierarchy-of-skills has many unconsidered factors; pre/post-condition detection happens only after execution (unacceptable delay); state needs touch+hearing integration; policy RFMs can't self-explore and lack a unified benchmark; data generation accuracy from LLMs/VGMs is "insufficient."

What I noticed reading it:

  • Survey is a snapshot, not a synthesis. The dominant mode is "method X does A, method Y does B" enumeration. Cross-method contradictions are rarely surfaced; e.g. the 2D-vs-3D-observation debate (Appendix B) is left as "still unclear whether 2D or 3D is more suitable for the scaling law" rather than adjudicated.
  • The L0 claim is unfalsifiable as stated. "Current research has a high probability of achieving L0" is asserted without a measured aggregate — no meta-analysis of success rates across the surveyed RFMs backs the 99%-vs-current-gap framing quantitatively.
  • Force/tactile/audio are under-weighted relative to the thesis. The framework foregrounds contact points and forces/torques as the L0→general axis, but the State module (Sec 6) is almost entirely 3D-reconstruction + pose; tactile sensing gets passing mention (MOSAIC haptic encoder, optical tactile in sim) and there is no dedicated tactile/force-representation section. For Weiyu's thesis this is the most important gap (see "Why I care").
  • Author list / affiliations. Heavily Chinese-institution (Samsung R&D China, BUPT, Tsinghua, Southeast Univ., Univ. Hamburg); citation coverage skews toward 2023–2024 arXiv. The v7 stamp is Nov 2025, so the snapshot is reasonably current through ~mid-2025 (cites OpenVLA-OFT, Groot N1, Helix, FAST, RDT-1B, π0).
  • No quantitative headline (it's a survey), but Tables 4–6 report per-method success rates with mixed Seen/Unseen splits and inconsistent benchmarks — the authors themselves flag this as "solely estimating the success rate is meaningless."

Why I care

This is one of the four Cluster-J survey anchors for the 2026-06-24 batch, and it's the broadest map of the "foundation models for manipulation" terrain — useful as a related-work scaffold and a citation quarry, less so as a deep read. Relevance to my work is real but partial, so let me be precise rather than manufacture it.

  • Where it lands squarely on my thesis. The paper's central L0→general axis is force/torque and contact-point precision — exactly the "many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable (is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, surface_is_rough)" argument I care about. They name it ("different degrees of precision with respect to contact points and applied forces/torques") and flag whole-body force-position hybrid control as "often overlooked." So the survey endorses the gap my batch is built around — but does not fill it. It surveys the perception/policy side richly and leaves tactile/force representation thin, which is precisely the opening the touch-language and force-VLA papers in this batch exploit.
  • Where it complements BLADE. Their Hierarchy-of-Skills module (Sec 5) is the LLM-to-PDDL subgoal-decomposition layer BLADE lives in; they survey SayCan, Text2Motion, LLM+P as the same landscape BLADE's baselines occupy. BLADE is a concrete instance of their "LLM-assisted classical TAMP" branch — but BLADE's distinctive move (auto-recovering preconditions/effects from demos, not assuming them given) is exactly the kind of thing this survey's taxonomy doesn't have a box for. Their framing treats pre/post-conditions as a perception module (detect object affordances), whereas BLADE treats predicates as learned, planner-facing abstractions. Good foil for situating predicate learning against the mainstream "FM-as-perception" view.
  • Useful structure to borrow. The VLAC/VLAKP/VLADP policy taxonomy is a clean way to organize the VLA papers in this batch (FuSe, Tactile-VLA, OmniVTLA, ForceVLA, etc. are mostly VLADP-style end-to-end dense-pose with extra sensor streams). Worth reusing in a topic page.
  • Off-theme parts to skip. Sec 6 (3D reconstruction/NeRF/pose), Sec 8 (scene generation / sim-to-real), Appendix D (model-based contact dynamics) are tangential to my long-horizon/predicate-learning line, though Appendix D is a surprisingly good primer if I ever need the model-based contact-rich baseline.
