Recognition of Heat-Induced Food State Changes by Time-Series Use of Vision-Language Model for Cooking Robot

Naoaki Kanazawa, Kento Kawaharazuka, Yoshiki Obinata, Kei Okada, Masayuki Inaba · The University of Tokyo · 2023 · arXiv preprint · arXiv:2309.01528 · PDF

One-liner. Run CLIP over a cooking-robot camera video and track, frame by frame, the classification probability of a positive vs. negative natural-language description of a heat-induced food change (e.g. "boiling and bubbling water" vs. "not boiling liquid water"); the time-series of that probability, smoothed and thresholded, becomes a recognizer for when the state change occurred — a purely visual, recognition-only, language-grounded detector for dynamic thermal cooking transitions.

Problem & motivation

Cooking is defined by large, special state changes of the foodstuff (vaporization, melting, browning, coagulation) that don't occur in most manipulation tasks, and applying heat on a stove produces many such changes depending on the recipe. Prior cooking-with-heat robots (pancake robot, omelette-quality batch-Bayesian-optimization robot) adjusted by heating time rather than recognizing the state change itself — brittle to ingredient variation, randomness, and unknown recipes. Prior food-state recognition trained CNNs on bespoke datasets and only classified the final post-cooking state; none addressed detecting the change point of a real-time transition during cooking. Collecting labeled data for every possible ingredient state change is infeasible, which motivates using an open-vocabulary VLM (CLIP) as a language-driven feature calculator so the state to recognize is specified by a textual description instead of a trained class.

Method

A unified, training-free pipeline built on frozen CLIP (ingest candidate; not yet in corpus):

  • Two-prompt degree-of-change signal. For each target transition, prepare a positive and a negative linguistic description (e.g. positive "Boiling water" / negative "Not boiling water"). At every video frame, CLIP computes the classification probability of the image against the two prompts; the positive probability is read as the instantaneous "degree of state change."
  • Four prompt styles compared. (a) simple state-change phrase ("Boiling water"); (b) phrase with extra description of the change ("Boiling and bubbling water"); (c) ingredient-word-first ("Water that is boiling"); (d) ingredient-first plus extra change description ("Water that is boiling and bubbling"). Hypothesis: more detailed change descriptions are more sensitive to the transition.
  • Prompt selection by slope. The per-frame degree-of-change time series is fit with a linear approximation; the prompt whose line has the largest slope (LA Slope) over the cooking session is chosen as the best recognizer — i.e. the prompt under which the signal rises most monotonically across the change.
  • Change-point recognition. A human annotates the time they perceive the change. The smoothed signal (simple moving average, window 10) at that annotated time is taken as a threshold; on unseen videos, the first time the smoothed signal crosses that threshold is the recognized change point. (For the discrete Maillard data, raw values are thresholded since there are too few samples to smooth.)
  • Gaze region. Two image crops are compared: the whole pot/pan ("entire") vs. only the contents inside it ("contents").

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected. Robot-camera videos of four canonical heat-induced changes — vaporization (water brought to a boil), melting (butter in a frying pan), thermal denaturation of proteins (egg cooked sunny-side-up), and Maillard reaction (onions stir-fried to a candy color). No public benchmark; no train/test split in the usual sense — "known" data tunes prompt + threshold, "unknown" data (same and different heat power) is evaluated. Number of trials per condition not reported.
  • Hardware / simulator: Cart-mobile robot PR2, recording with its onboard camera. For the Maillard experiment the robot direct-teach stir-fried onions with a spatula and periodically paused stirring to capture clean (arm-/spatula-free) images, yielding discrete rather than continuous samples.
  • Baselines: No external method baseline. Internal comparisons only: 4 prompt styles × 2 gaze regions, and same- vs. different-heat-power generalization. Recognized change time is compared against a single human annotation per video.
  • Compute: not reported.

Results

Prompt-style finding (Tables 1, 3, 5, 7): the ingredient-word-ending prompt that also describes the change — style (b), e.g. "Boiling and bubbling water" — gave the largest LA Slope and best recognition in almost all conditions; the lone exception was vaporization with the "contents" gaze, where the simple prompt (a) won. Including a description of the change beat a bare state phrase, confirming the sensitivity hypothesis.

Gaze finding (counter to the authors' hypothesis): gazing at the entire pot/pan generally beat gazing only at the contents, despite the change happening in the contents. Conjectured causes: CLIP's training images of whole pots, or whole-pot images being easier to read as "food being cooked."

Vaporization and melting were recognized well (errors of seconds with the entire-pot gaze). Maillard matched the human annotation exactly at the entire-pan gaze — but the authors attribute the perfect match to the data being discrete (few candidate times to choose among), tempering the claim. Thermal denaturation of proteins was the failure case: even with matched prompts/gaze the per-trial signal plots differ in shape, and recognition only worked for the entire-pot / different-power condition (Table 6, Fig 9–10). The authors suspect either that the four prompt styles don't capture the egg transition, or that protein denaturation simply has larger visual variance across trials than the other changes.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • The method recognizes vaporization, melting, and Maillard, but fails on thermal denaturation of proteins; a more robust recognizer needs an automated way to search for suitable prompts and better time-series processing.
  • To cover more state-change types, the authors say it "may be necessary to integrate them into a multimodal recognition method" — i.e. vision alone is insufficient.
  • Threshold is set from a single human annotation per change; no discussion of inter-annotator variance or threshold robustness.

What I noticed reading it:

  • The "recognizer" is just a thresholded scalar CLIP-probability curve. It detects a monotone visual drift labeled by language, not a grounded predicate over the physical process — there is no notion of "boiling" as a precondition/effect that a planner could consume, and no closed loop back to action.
  • Evaluation is thin: a handful of self-collected videos, one human annotation each, no success-rate statistics, no external baseline. The "perfect" Maillard number is an artifact of discrete sampling, as the authors concede.
  • Prompt selection requires the change to have already happened in a known video (to fit the slope and read the threshold), so the method is not truly open-recipe at deployment — it needs a labeled exemplar of each transition first.
  • The thermal axis is entirely visual: "heat-induced" describes the cause, but the only sensing is RGB. Vaporization/melting are visually obvious (bubbles, liquefaction); the failure on eggs is exactly where the visual signature is weak and where a thermal or other non-visual sensor would carry the signal.

Why I care

This is the closest existing work to the "dynamic thermal state grounded in language" corner of the idea — and it cleanly exposes the gap. It does three things the idea wants: (1) grounds a dynamic, change-oriented concept ("becoming boiling", "coming to a melt") rather than a static property; (2) binds that concept to natural language via an open-vocabulary VLM, so the target is specified textually instead of as a trained class; (3) monitors over time, producing a temporal signal and an explicit change point.

But it misses every other axis. It is vision-only — "heat-induced" is the cause, yet the only modality is RGB, with no thermal/tactile/audio sensing; the authors themselves end by saying multimodal integration is needed, which is precisely the thesis. It is recognition-only — a passive thresholded curve, with no loop back into action, no replanning, no semantic correction. And it is sub-symbolic — the output is a scalar probability, not a predicate with preconditions/effects that BLADE-style bi-level planning could consume. Versus BLADE, whose visual predicate classifiers label static relations from single frames and feed a symbolic planner, this paper is the temporal/dynamic complement on the perception side but stops short of both the symbolic abstraction and the control loop. It is the cleanest evidence for the central bet: the state changes that matter (and the one that fails here — egg coagulation) are exactly where vision is weakest and where thermal/non-visual sensing should ground the "hot enough now" predicate. Same authors / lab as the Kanazawa cooking robot (food-state → PDDL) and the VQA-based continuous object-state recognition line — this is the time-series-VLM predecessor that those build on.
