Bi-LAT: Bilateral Control-Based Imitation Learning via Natural Language and Action Chunking with Transformers

Takumi Kobayashi*, Masato Kobayashi*†, Thanpimon Buamanee, Yuki Uranishi · Univ. of Osaka / Kobe University · 2025 · arXiv:2504.01301 · PDF · project page

One-liner. Bi-LAT bolts a language encoder onto a bilateral-control imitation-learning policy (Bi-ACT) so that everyday force adverbs — "softly grasp the cup" vs. "strongly grasp the cup" — map directly to graded grasp/twist force at execution, demonstrating that natural-language can modulate torque without any numeric threshold, with SigLIP the standout text encoder.

Problem & motivation

Most imitation-learning teleoperation is unilateral: the demonstrator sends only position commands and never feels contact force, so the data poorly captures the fine force interactions needed for fragile or deformable objects. Bilateral control fixes the data side — leader and follower exchange both position and force in real time, so the operator feels contact and the demos encode force. The gap Bi-LAT targets is the command side: prior force-conditioned policies still rely on numerically specified torque/position values, not on human-readable instructions. The paper asks how much language can substitute for explicit force thresholds. It builds directly on Bi-ACT [13] (bilateral control + Action Chunking with Transformers + vision), adding a language encoder so the same skill (grasp a cup, twist a sponge) can be executed at different force levels purely from a verbal cue.

Method

Data collection — four-channel bilateral control (Fig 2). A human teleoperates a leader robot; a follower mirrors it in the environment. The control law couples them so joint angles match and torques oppose: θl − θf = 0 (Eq. 1) and τl + τf = 0 (Eq. 2). Torque is estimated sensorlessly via a disturbance observer (DOB [20]) and reaction-force observer (RFOB [21]) — no force/torque sensor needed. The 1000 Hz control loop logs joint angle, velocity, and torque for both robots; RGB images are logged at 100 Hz. Crucially, each demonstration is paired with a natural-language instruction describing the desired force ("softly grasp the cup" / "strongly grasp the cup").

Learning model — language-conditioned CVAE (Fig 3). The backbone is a Transformer Conditional Variational Autoencoder (the ACT recipe). The CVAE encoder takes a [CLS] token plus the follower joints (angle/velocity/torque) and the action sequence to produce a style variable z. The decoder Transformer fuses four streams: (i) z, (ii) follower joint angle/velocity/torque, (iii) ResNet-18 features from RGB images, and (iv) a fixed-length embedding of the language instruction from a dedicated language encoder. The Transformer decoder then predicts a chunk of future leader actions — sequences of joint angle, velocity, and torque. Training minimizes the error between predicted leader joint data and the bilateral-control ground truth. Predicted actions are converted to per-joint current by the 1000 Hz position-and-force controller (Fig 5).

Language encoder sweep (Fig 4). The central experimental variable. Four encoders are compared: DistilBERT [25] and ModernBERT [26] (BERT family, strong on language understanding) versus CLIP [22] and SigLIP [24] (CLIP family, strong at fusing visual + textual cues). CLIP-style prompt engineering ("a photo of a…") is applied during data collection to sharpen encoder responses to short/ambiguous instructions. All Bi-LAT models use 4 encoder layers and 7 decoder layers.

DABI augmentation [15]. High-frequency (1000 Hz) robot data and low-frequency (100 Hz) image data are jointly downsampled, expanding the original 6 demonstrations into 60 (a 10× increase) to feed the data-hungry Transformer.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: No public benchmark — two custom real-world tasks. Unimanual cup-stacking (Pick → Move → Place with deformable paper cups) and bimanual sponge-twisting (Grab → Lift → Twist a deformable sponge). Each task has two force conditions paired with language ("softly" / "strongly"); 6 demos per task expanded to 60 via DABI [15].
  • Hardware / simulator: Real robots only. Unimanual: two OpenManipulator-X (ROBOTIS) arms (4 arm joints + gripper = 5 joints), leader/follower pair, 3 ELP USB RGB cameras (640×360: top, side, gripper). Bimanual: ALPHA-α platform [9] — 4 robots total (2 leaders + 2 followers), 6-DoF + gripper = 7 motors each, 4 RGB cameras. 1000 Hz bilateral control loop, 100 Hz cameras. Sensorless torque via DOB/RFOB.
  • Baselines: Bi-ACT [13] with no language ("Bi-ACT (None)") — the direct ablation showing what language buys. Within Bi-LAT, the four language encoders (DistilBERT, ModernBERT, CLIP, SigLIP) are compared against each other.
  • Compute: not reported.

Results

Task success is saturated; force accuracy is the real metric. Every method completes the cup-stacking task at 5/5 (100%) (Table I). The discriminating column is "Force Accuracy" — whether the policy actually distinguishes "softly" from "strongly" (judged by how closely inference joint-angle/torque histograms match the two training distributions, Fig 10).

Key findings:

  • Language is necessary for force modulation. Bi-ACT with no language merges "softly" and "strongly" data and infers a profile biased toward strong force (joint angle ~2.75 rad, torque 0.10–0.20 Nm), unable to differentiate — motivating the whole approach.
  • SigLIP wins. Fig 10(e): SigLIP inference data for each instruction most closely tracks its training distribution. DistilBERT shows some separation but with inflated angles/torques; ModernBERT and CLIP show only weak max-value differences and don't cleanly separate the two conditions.
  • Bimanual generalization (Table II). Sponge-twisting with SigLIP: "softly" hits 100% across Pick/Lift/Twist (overall 5/5). "Strongly" is 100% on Pick and Lift but Twist drops to 4/5 (80%) — the sponge's higher internal resistance under a strong grip caused one loss of secure grip during twisting (overall 4/5, 80%). Torque profiles for soft vs. strong runs diverge sharply during grasp/twist while spatial trajectories stay similar (Fig 15).

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Evaluated only on deformable objects (paper cups, sponge); future work targets more diverse object properties including rigid and deformable.
  • Force levels limited to a binary "softly" / "strongly"; varying task difficulty and a richer continuum of force are flagged as future work.
  • Real-time language interaction needs refinement; larger-scale datasets are needed for generalization.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Tiny-N statistics. Everything is 5/5 or 4/5 successes — counts, not rates over seeds. The headline "100% success" is over 5 trials of a 2-condition task; the SigLIP-vs-others ranking rests on qualitative histogram-overlap judgments (×/△/◯), not a quantified force-accuracy metric. No numeric force error, no statistical test, no confidence intervals.
  • Only 6 real demos per task (60 after DABI). The encoder comparison is drawn from an extremely small data regime; whether SigLIP's edge holds with more data or more force levels is untested.
  • "Force accuracy" is never operationalized. It is inferred from whether inference histograms visually match training histograms — which conflates "matches the demo distribution" with "applied the correct force." A policy could match the distribution without the force being task-appropriate.
  • The language space is degenerate. Two instructions differing in one adverb. This is closer to a learned 1-bit force selector conditioned on text than to open-vocabulary force grounding; the claim of "everyday human instructions" is broader than what is tested.
  • Why SigLIP > CLIP > BERT is not mechanistically explained. The paper observes the ranking but offers no ablation isolating why a vision-language sigmoid-loss encoder beats a text-only one on a force-from-text task where no image disambiguation of the adverb is obviously needed.

Why I care

This paper is a clean, small-scale instantiation of the batch's central thesis: many manipulation predicates live in force, not pixels. "Softly grasped" vs. "strongly grasped" is precisely a predicate that is not visually evaluable — the cup looks the same; only the torque profile differs. Bi-LAT shows that such a force-level distinction can be (a) captured in demos via bilateral control's sensorless torque, and (b) commanded in language. Relative to BLADE, this is the missing continuous-force layer: BLADE's abstraction is purely categorical, with all continuous parameters (including force) buried inside the diffusion policy and no language handle on them. Bi-LAT is the inverse — no symbolic abstraction, but a direct language→force mapping at the controller. A predicate like is_grasped_firmly(cup) would need exactly Bi-LAT's force signal to ground its truth value; the natural synthesis is BLADE-style operators whose bodies carry a language-modulated force argument rather than a single diffusion policy. The bilateral-control data-collection trick (force without an F/T sensor) is also directly relevant to anyone wanting force-labeled demos cheaply for predicate learning.
