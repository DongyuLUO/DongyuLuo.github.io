Multimodal Detection and Identification of Robot Manipulation Failures

Arda Inceoglu, Eren Erdal Aksoy, Sanem Sariel · 2023 · arXiv (journal-style extension of the IROS 2021 FINO-Net paper) · arXiv:2305.04639 · PDF

One-liner. FINO-Net is a deep sensor-fusion network that fuses egocentric RGB, depth, and audio over the course of a tabletop manipulation action to detect whether the execution failed and classify which failure type occurred (collision / miss / overflow / spill / overturn) end-to-end from raw signals, with no hand-crafted features and no symbolic state.

Problem & motivation

Service robots in unstructured environments cannot perfectly predict the outcomes of manipulation actions: misaligned grasps, underestimated container contents, or wrong self-model assumptions lead to accidents (knocked-over objects, spilled liquids). For situation awareness — maintaining integrity, recovering short-term, and learning to avoid failures long-term — executions must be continuously monitored and failures detected and identified. The authors stress two phases (Fig. 1): the manipulation phase (end-effector in contact, where proprioception helps) and the post-manipulation phase (contact has ended, no proprioception available, so exteroceptive environmental monitoring is required to catch e.g. a secondary object overturning after a push). Prior failure detection was mostly addressed at the symbolic level, on single actions (usually grasping), and on robot–primary-object interactions only; there was no public multimodal robot-failure dataset. This work targets data-driven monitoring across a set of primitive actions, covering both primary and secondary objects.

Method

Failure detection and classification are cast as supervised classification over multimodal observation sequences. For detection, target y ∈ {success, fail}; for classification, y ∈ {success, collision, miss, overflow, spill, overturn}. A recording is the set of synchronized modality streams over one action; the goal is a function Φ(·) mapping the per-modality feature extractors' outputs to a label.

The FINO-Net architecture (Fig. 3) has two branches with a fusion head:

  • Vision branch (φI). RGB and depth are early-fused by stacking (xI = xC ∪ xD). Preprocessing samples 8 RGB/depth frame pairs spanning the execution: self-occluded frames are removed by depth thresholding, the sequence is roughly segmented into approach, manipulate, retreat phases, and 4 frames are drawn from approach and retreat. Three blocks, each = two 3×3 conv layers + a convolutional-LSTM (convLSTM) layer (LSTM with convolution operators replacing the fully connected gates) to capture spatio-temporal structure, with max pooling, batch norm, and dropout.
  • Audio branch (φA). Single-channel 16 kHz audio is windowed into 32 ms frames, STFT → Mel filterbank → 20 MFCCs; window count fixed by padding/clipping. Two conv layers (64 filters, size 32) + max pooling. (Earlier work by the authors classified MFCCs into symbolic states drop/collision/idle/ego-noise via SVMs; FINO-Net instead maps MFCCs directly to execution states.)
  • Late fusion (Φ). The two unimodal latent vectors are concatenated and passed to two fully connected layers: y = Φ(φI(·) ⊕ φA(·)). So RGB+D fuse early; vision+audio fuse late.

Two deployment modes for classification are studied: stand-alone (one 6-way head jointly detects and classifies, including the success class) and cascaded (a 5-way failure classifier triggered only after the detector flags a failure; trained on failed samples only).

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: The authors' open-source FAILURE dataset, here extended with 99 new multimodal recordings and annotated with failure types. Total 324 recordings across five actions (push, pick&place, pour, put-in-container, stack-a-tower): 152 successes, 172 failures spread over collision (77), miss (22), overflow (25), spill (20), overturn (28) (Tables I–II). Split 70% train / 10% val / 20% test. Data augmentation: per-sequence color jitter (p=0.2) and vertical flip (p=0.5).
  • Hardware / simulator: Real Baxter humanoid with a parallel gripper, head-mounted Asus Xtion Pro RGB-D camera, and a PSEye microphone on the lower torso (Fig. 2). Egocentric, onboard, real-time sensing — no external instrumentation.
  • Baselines: Internal modality ablations — FINO-Net-RGB, -D, -RGB-D, -A, -RGB-D-A (the full model). No external method baseline is reported; comparison is against the authors' own earlier hand-crafted-feature / symbolic-MFCC pipelines in prose.
  • Compute: 250 epochs, Adam, learning rate 1e−5, early stopping on validation. Wall-clock / GPU not reported.

Results

Full multimodal fusion clearly wins (Table III, test set):

ModelDetection F1Classification F1
FINO-Net-RGB0.7620.655
FINO-Net-D0.6830.687
FINO-Net-RGB-D0.7690.680
FINO-Net-A0.6640.679
FINO-Net-RGB-D-A0.8660.796

Adding audio to RGB-D lifts detection F1 from 0.769 to 0.866 and classification F1 from 0.680 to 0.796 — the headline evidence that the modalities are complementary (audio catches drops/collisions that vision misses under occlusion). Confusion matrices (Figs. 4–5) show the main errors come from post-manipulation scene similarity: overflow vs spill both leave particles on the table; collision vs overturn both displace the primary object. A real-time analysis (Fig. 6) shows predictions become reliable in the second half of execution as failure indicators appear, yet the model can still predict on partial sequences. A frame-sampling sensitivity study (Table IV, averages of 50 samplings) confirms RGB-D-A > RGB-D for both tasks. A qualitative compound scenario (Fig. 7: successful pour → successful put-in-container → failed push) exposes the audio failure mode — a benign container-collision sound during put-in-container fools the RGB-D-A model into predicting a collision failure.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Audio is hard: environmental and robot ego-noise degrade performance, and nearly every object interaction emits a sound regardless of success, so sound presence alone is a weak failure cue (the Fig. 7 false positive).
  • Post-manipulation scenes for distinct failure types look alike, capping classification accuracy.
  • Detection is reliable mostly toward the end of the action — little early warning.
  • Future work named: anticipating failures before they occur to minimize damage.

What I noticed reading it:

  • The label space is a flat, action-outcome taxonomy (collision/miss/overflow/spill/overturn), not a representation of object state or state transition. It answers "did this action succeed?" not "is the water boiling now?" — no notion of a continuous or evolving physical property.
  • It is a passive classifier, not a controller: it emits a failure label but has no recovery, replanning, or correction loop. The "closed-loop" value is latent (situation awareness as a prerequisite), never realized in this paper.
  • The temporal modeling (convLSTM over 8 sampled frames + MFCC windows) is monitoring-as-sequence-classification, with no symbolic precondition/effect structure — failures are detected, not explained in terms of which expected effect did not hold.
  • No tactile/force or thermal sensing despite "multimodal"; the non-visual modality is purely contact-audio. So it covers the audio axis of the state-change idea but not touch/force/thermal.

Why I care

For the idea of grounding dynamic, change-oriented manipulation concepts on multimodal non-visual sensing, monitored over time, to drive closed-loop semantic correction, FINO-Net is the closest existing "multimodal temporal monitor" anchor — it does exactly the fuse-RGB-D-+-audio-over-time-to-judge-execution part, and demonstrates concretely that audio carries failure information vision lacks. But it stops well short of the idea on three axes:

  • Flat outcomes, not state-change predicates. Its targets are action-level failure classes, not object-state transitions ("became slippery", "coming to a boil"). There is no symbolic predicate whose precondition/effect could feed a planner — the exact opposite of BLADE's learned precondition/effect predicates over which bi-level planning runs.
  • Detection, not correction. BLADE's replan-after-each-behavior loop is the closed-loop mechanism FINO-Net only motivates. To serve the idea, a FINO-Net-style monitor would need its output wired to a replanner — the role REFLECT (multimodal failure explanation + LLM-driven correction) and DoReMi (detect & recover from plan–execution misalignment) play, and that FINO-Net does not.
  • Audio-only non-visual sensing. It validates the audio axis but ignores tactile/force/thermal, where many of the dynamic predicates I care about actually live.

Net: FINO-Net is the right architectural skeleton (multimodal convLSTM temporal monitor on a real robot, with a real open dataset) but the wrong output abstraction (flat failure label, no symbolic change-predicate, no precondition/effect tie-in, no replanning). It is the strongest "monitor as classifier" foil to argue against — the gap I'd want to close is turning its temporal multimodal evidence into grounded state-transition predicates that replanning can consume.
