Towards Forceful Robotic Foundation Models: a Literature Survey

William Xie, Nikolaus Correll · University of Colorado at Boulder · arXiv preprint, Apr 2025 · arXiv:2504.11827 · PDF

One-liner. A comparative survey of 25 transformer-/diffusion-based end-to-end tactile manipulation policies, organized around three axes — how force/touch data is collected, how force-conditioned actions are expressed, and how force data is represented — arguing that the next generation of robot foundation models will need force and torque as first-class input and output, not just position and vision.

Problem & motivation

Current "robot foundation models" (RT-2, Octo, OpenVLA, Gemini Robotics, π0) condition almost exclusively on visual input and output position control. But force is the second derivative of position (F = m·&xuml;), a richer, higher-frequency representation of motion: small position errors cause large force errors in stiff systems, so position-only control is neither sample-efficient nor sufficient for contact-rich tasks like pouring, peg-in-hole insertion, and handling delicate objects. The survey asks when and why forces are needed and how to fold them into generalist policies. A recurring tension it sets up: force and touch are "abstract quantities that can be inferred through a wide range of modalities and are often measured and controlled implicitly" — e.g., impedance control bakes compliance in without ever sensing force — so it is genuinely unclear whether explicit force representations are needed at all. The survey scopes itself to transformer [10] and diffusion [11] end-to-end methods precisely because their scaling properties make them candidates for foundation models.

Survey scope / taxonomy

The survey first lays a background (Sec. 2) covering: human tactile sensing (FA-I/FA-II/SA-I/SA-II mechanoreceptors, 20–1500 Hz bandwidths) and proprioception (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs, joint receptors); robotic force control — the Jacobian relations &xdot; = J&qdot; and τ = JTF, impedance control as a virtual mass-spring-damper F = M&xuml; + D&xdot; + K(x − xd), compliance control (M=0,D=0), admittance control, and hybrid position/force control; and policy learning (imitation learning vs RL, behavior cloning, Decision/Trajectory Transformer, diffusion policy as a special case of flow-matching, VLAs).

It then reviews 25 works implementing tactile robot policies, which collectively yield 64 manipulation experiments / 59 distinct policies / 53 unique tasks. The core analytic device (Figs 3–4) plots each task on log-axes of force magnitude (0.1–10N+) vs task length / "makespan" (0.1–20s), binning force into delicate (0.1–1N), typical (1–10N), and high (>10N). The survey body is then organized into three lenses, each its own section:

  • Data collection (Sec. 4) — five methods: human video demonstration, kinesthetic teaching, reinforcement learning, augmented/bilateral teleoperation (haptics, handheld grippers, force-matching leader-follower), and plain teleoperation.
  • Action space (Sec. 5) — Cartesian position control vs joint-space control vs explicit force control (impedance/admittance/compliance, wrench targets).
  • Representation learning (Sec. 6) — behavior-cloning architecture choice, plus visuotactile vs force representation learning, and "scaling multimodal reasoning."

Coverage

  • Datasets / benchmarks: 25 reviewed papers, 64 tasks (53 unique). No consistent benchmark across papers — "peg-in-hole" is the only task with as many as five papers providing benchmarks. Tasks span door open/close, peg/plug/USB/key insertion, pouring, wiping/erasing, nut threading, grasping (fragile/delicate/deformable), zucchini peeling, dough rolling, chip/card swiping, book tipping, etc. A full per-paper/per-task reference table is hosted in an external online spreadsheet.
  • Hardware / simulator: 14 unique tactile sensor products. Sensor-type distribution (Fig 5): finger optical/visuotactile (GelSight-family) 36% — the plurality; finger force 16%; whole-arm joint-torque 16%; wrist F/T 16%; finger audio 8%; whole-arm+finger 8%. Arms include Franka Panda, Flexiv Rizon, UR5E, Widow X; wrist sensors include ATI Mini 45, OptoForce; novel sensors include AnySkin (magnetometer), CoinFT, contact microphones. Simulation is rare — one outlier trains in Drake on 50,000 simulated GelSight contact-rich skills.
  • Baselines: not reported as such — this is a survey, not an empirical paper; "baselines" within reviewed works are force-ablated versions of each policy (e.g., position-only or uniform-stiffness controllers), which consistently underperform their force-aware counterparts.
  • Compute: not reported.

Higher-level takeaways:

  • Force "barely matters" empirically so far. Only a few tasks (pouring, peg-in-hole, delicate-object handling) demonstrably need force; the authors find imitation-learning performance is "not at a level of dynamics where force truly matters." Force magnitudes are reported in only 7/25 (28%) of works, so most are estimated by the authors.
  • Explicit force control yields concrete wins when used. Reviewed force-aware policies report large gains over force-ablated versions: Hou et al.'s variable-compliance policy drops 81% in absolute success when force is removed; Zhou et al. cut task time 17% vs no-force and 26% vs teleop; Liu et al. / Noseworthy et al. reach 1.3×–4.5× speedups; Wu et al. (TacDiffusion) hit <0.5mm peg insertion in under 2s at >90% success.
  • Kinesthetic teaching and handheld grippers reduce the human–robot embodiment gap by letting demonstrators impart real forces; teleoperation is most popular but force-blind.
  • Force representation is "easy" but underexplored. Force data is low-dimensional and directly interpretable (raw, with minor gravity compensation, FFT-to-spectrogram, or a small MLP), whereas visuotactile (GelSight) representation learning gets the most attention, e.g. TVL encoder fine-tuned on 44k vision-touch-language samples.
  • No tactile foundation model exists yet that does both high-level reasoning and low-level reactive control. FuSe (the one paper fine-tuning a foundation backbone, Octo, on 26,866 tactile trajectories) reasons multimodally but is largely limited to grasping/button-pressing; "no work has fully explored first-class large force pretraining."
  • Compositional vs end-to-end. π0, Helix, and Gemini Robotics all use compositional control (low-frequency model decomposes instructions into atomic skills for a high-frequency low-level controller), suggesting foundation models can be "functionally equivalent to several smaller, specialized policies."

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Scope is deliberately narrow: only transformer/diffusion end-to-end methods; classical force-control RL and analytic-controller literature are largely set aside.
  • Force magnitudes and task times are estimated for the 72% of works that don't report them (from videos/presentations), so Figs 3–4 carry measurement uncertainty.
  • The central question — whether explicit force representations are needed, given that impedance/compliance can absorb force implicitly — is left genuinely open; they argue physiology's redundancy (touch can be functionally replaced by vision+proprioception) should not be read as "vision is sufficient."
  • Simulation of dynamic, frictional, large-magnitude forces remains an unsolved bottleneck for scaling tactile data.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Sample is small and heterogeneous: 25 papers, no shared benchmark, no shared metric. The percentage breakdowns (36%, 76%, 64%, …) are over n=25 unique papers, so each bar is a handful of works — the distributions are suggestive, not statistically robust. Several percentages are exact multiples of 4% (=1/25), underscoring how few papers populate each cell.
  • The headline force-vs-makespan scatter (Figs 3–4) is the survey's signature visual but rests on author-estimated force magnitudes for ~72% of tasks — the axis with the strongest narrative claim is the least directly measured.
  • No quantitative cross-paper comparison of success rates is possible because tasks/benchmarks don't align; all reported gains are within-paper force-ablation deltas, which can't be pooled.
  • The taxonomy is engineering-centric (sensor type, data-collection method, action space). It largely sidesteps the symbolic / planning question of which task-relevant predicates force sensing makes decidable — exactly the gap that matters for my work (see below).
  • "Foundation model" is used loosely: of the 25, only FuSe actually fine-tunes a pretrained generalist backbone; the rest are task-specific policies. The title's aspiration outruns the corpus.

Why I care

This survey is the cleanest external statement of the thesis underlying this whole 2026-06-24 batch: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — they live in touch and force. is_inserted (peg-in-hole), is_screwed_tight (nut threading), is_grasped (grasp event detection in Fig 1), and delicate-object compliance are precisely the survey's contact-rich exemplars, and it documents that vision/position-only policies fail on them while force-conditioned policies succeed. For BLADE, this is directly load-bearing: BLADE's "What I noticed" flagged that continuous parameters (pour amount, grasp pose, force) sit entirely inside the diffusion policy — the abstraction layer is purely categorical, which may be a limitation for force-modulated tasks. This survey is the empirical map of exactly that gap. A force-aware predicate classifier (turned_tight(bolt), seated(peg)) grounded in wrist-F/T or fingertip signals — rather than pixels — is the natural extension of BLADE's every-frame predicate-labeling recipe into the tactile regime. The survey also sharpens the structure-vs-scale debate BLADE sits inside: it observes that π0/Helix/Gemini are compositional (slow planner + fast controller), which is structurally BLADE-shaped, and notes "no work has fully explored first-class force pretraining" — an open lane for symbolic-abstraction-over-force-skills work.

On-theme and central, not tangential. It is a survey rather than a method, so it anchors the cluster rather than competing with BLADE methodologically.
