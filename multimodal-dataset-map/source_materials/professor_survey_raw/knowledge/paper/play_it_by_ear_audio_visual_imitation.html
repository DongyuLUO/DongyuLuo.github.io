Play it by Ear: Learning Skills amidst Occlusion through Audio-Visual Imitation Learning

Maximilian Du*, Olivia Y. Lee*, Suraj Nair, Chelsea Finn · Stanford University · RSS 2022 · arXiv:2205.14850 · PDF

One-liner. When the target object is fully hidden from the camera (keys inside a grocery bag), a cheap microphone on the gripper turns contact sounds into the missing state signal — and a memory-augmented audio-visual policy, behavior-cloned from teleop demos then fine-tuned with a handful of human interventions, doubles success over a vision-only policy on a real Franka.

Problem & motivation

Vision-based manipulation breaks under occlusion: some tasks (extracting keys from a bag) leave the target permanently out of sight, so vision alone cannot solve them. Tactile sensing could help but robust contact tactile remains an open hardware problem. The authors argue a gripper-mounted microphone is a cheap, robust alternative: contact events (the gripper brushing keys) emit sounds that localize the target even when nothing is visible. Two hard sub-problems follow: (1) sensors are intermittently informative — a key sound several timesteps ago may be what disambiguates the current grasp, so the policy needs memory; (2) the high-dimensional, temporally correlated audio-visual-history observation space makes offline behavior cloning brittle to state-distribution shift, motivating online interactive fine-tuning. This is squarely a partial-observability problem where the missing predicate (roughly, are the keys grasped / where are they) lives in sound, not pixels.

Method

Three components, summarized in Algorithm 1 and the architecture of Fig 2.

1. Audio-visual-proprioceptive fusion. The agent operates in a controlled Markov process with a partially-observed, multi-modal observation o = [ov, os, op] (vision, sound, proprioception). It learns a memory policy πθ: Ov(t-H):t × Os(t-H):t × Op(t-H):t → A trained with a behavior-cloning MSE loss (Eq 1). Vision: 84×84 RGB from a third-person camera (target object obscured from this view), upsampled to 224×224 and encoded by a ResNet18 [22] into a 512-d embedding, with random-crop / color-jitter / affine augmentation. Sound: last 2 seconds of waveform from the gripper microphone, converted to a spectrogram of shape [L=57, S=160] and passed through a 1D conv (for temporal equivariance) to an audio embedding of size 264. Proprioception: end-effector position + gripper status, shape (1,7).

2. Early + late fusion across time. Single-timestep inputs give poor performance under partial observability, so history is encoded two ways (Fig 2). Early fusion: the past 3 timesteps of images are channel-stacked (9 input channels into a modified ResNet18) to capture short-timescale arm motion. Late fusion: per-step visual+audio+proprio embeddings [ev, es, op]t are concatenated, MLP-fused into a joint embedding zt, and a sequence of zt over the past H=10 timesteps is fed to a one-layer LSTM [23] (hidden dim D=50). The LSTM output goes to a 2-hidden-layer MLP producing a continuous delta end-effector action plus a discrete gripper action.

3. Online interactive fine-tuning (HG-DAgger). Offline BC suffers distribution shift in this high-dim space, so the policy is fine-tuned with human-gated DAgger [35] (Fig 3): the policy is first trained offline on demos D to convergence, then rolled out while a human watches and chooses when to intervene, supplying expert actions at* = πhuman(o(t-H):t). Intervention tuples go into a separate online buffer Do, and the policy is fine-tuned on balanced batches drawn from both Do and the original demos D (Eq 2). The authors deliberately reject the RL-finetuning alternative (residual RL / online RL): it needs a hard-to-specify real-robot reward and is sample-inefficient (they run residual RL [31] 5× longer and it still fails to beat offline BC).

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Real key-extraction task (Easy: keys centered below gripper; Hard: keys over a ~3 cm radius, must move within the bag to make contact), 50 teleop demos. Object-differentiation task (pick/drop a mystery bag, grasp only if it sounds like screws vs. packing peanuts), 30 demos, evaluated over 20 trials. Zero-shot unseen-object test (Table I). Two simulated tasks: Occluded Grasp with Visual Indicator and Occluded Pick-and-Place, 50 scripted-expert demos each.
  • Hardware / simulator: Franka Emika Panda 7-DoF arm; third-person uncalibrated RGB camera; microphone attached to the gripper. Teleop via Oculus Quest 2 VR controller. Simulation in MuJoCo [63] on top of robosuite [72]; since MuJoCo has no sound sim, "sound" is approximated by external contact forces on the target object.
  • Baselines: Ablations of the full system — (-Audio), (-Vision), (-Memory), and (-Interventions) (offline BC only); plus (-Interventions)(+Residual RL) [31] using SAC [21] on top of the offline BC policy. (No external multimodal-manipulation baseline is run.)
  • Compute: Offline policy trained 10,000 steps (real) / 100,000 steps (sim); fine-tuned 1,000 steps after each online episode. Otherwise not reported.

Results

Headline: on the real Franka key-extraction task the full system reaches a 70% success rate on Easy (from a 30% offline-only start, after just ~20 episodes / ~1 hour of human intervention) and 50% on Hard (after ~40 episodes) — "more than double the success rate of an agent using only vision," and the abstract reports 50% higher than a no-audio policy.

Real-robot ablations (Fig 5, success rate over online fine-tuning):

MethodEasyHard
Ours (full)~70%~50%
Ours (-Vision)~50% (peak 60% w/o interv.)~10%
Ours (-Audio)~10% (never >20%)~0% (all fail)
Ours (-Memory)~10% (40% w/o interv., then degrades)~10%

Where each modality matters: (-Audio) collapses because the agent can't tell whether a sound came from the bag vs. the keys, or where the keys are; (-Vision) survives on Easy (sound + proprio suffice to drop straight down) but fails on Hard, where seeing the bag deform helps disambiguate gripper-vs-key contact; (-Memory) can't track which task phase it's in, so it drops keys right after grasping. Notably, (-Memory) without interventions reaches 40% on Easy — sometimes higher than with interventions, because once trained on corrections it can't tell whether it has grasped, so memory and interventions interact.

Object differentiation (Fig 6): trained on 30 demos to grasp the bag if it sounds like screws and leave it if it sounds like packing peanuts; 14/20 overall (7/10 each case) — sound alone drives the action choice.

Zero-shot unseen objects (Table I): a policy trained only on keys generalizes to objects that make a loud enough contact sound — Chip Bag 10/10, Aspirin Bottle 5/10, Pens 5/10, Candies 7/10 — but Cloth 0/10, because soft objects make no sound and the gripper never closes.

Simulation (Fig 7): removing interventions costs ~15% (grasp) and ~25% (pick-and-place); removing memory hurts most among modality ablations (~10%); removing audio costs ~5% on both; residual RL on top of BC is ineffective and often destroys BC performance even at 5× the budget.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Heavy dependence on human supervision, both for offline demos and online corrections; smarter human-gating (e.g. ThriftyDAgger [25]) could ease the burden.
  • A single microphone gives only incomplete audio information; two microphones (like human ears) could enable directional/binaural localization of an object.

What I noticed reading it:

  • No external baseline. Every comparison is an ablation of the authors' own system; there is no comparison against a competing multimodal or tactile manipulation method, so the absolute difficulty of the task relative to prior art is hard to calibrate.
  • Tiny-N statistics. Real-robot rates are over 10 trials per evaluation; object differentiation is 14/20; unseen-object generalization is out of 10. "70% on Easy" is 7/10 — wide confidence intervals that the success-rate curves smooth over.
  • Sim "sound" is a proxy. MuJoCo has no audio, so the simulated audio signal is approximated by external contact forces — effectively a clean force/contact-event channel, not a noisy spectrogram. The sim ablations therefore test "contact-event memory" more than "audio" per se.
  • Memory×intervention confound. The (-Memory)-without- interventions case sometimes beats the full intervention pipeline, suggesting the intervention distribution and the memory module interact in non-obvious ways the paper doesn't fully untangle.
  • The audio signal is implicit and end-to-end — there is no explicit "is-grasped" or "keys-located" predicate; the contact-state inference is buried in the LSTM, which limits interpretability and reuse.

Why I care

This paper is a clean, early instantiation of the batch's central thesis: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — they live in sound and contact. "Are the keys grasped?", "did the gripper contact the target?" are exactly the kind of is_grasped / made_contact predicates that BLADE would need to evaluate, and that a camera cannot read off an occluded scene. Where BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers and assumes the relevant state is observable in pixels, Play it by Ear shows a whole task family where the determining signal is acoustic. The natural connection to my line of work: a BLADE-style symbolic layer whose precondition/effect predicates are grounded in audio (or fused audio-visual) classifiers, so the planner can reason about occluded contact states it currently can't see. The paper itself stays fully end-to-end / implicit (LSTM memory, no symbols), which is precisely the gap a structured-abstraction approach could fill: turn "the LSTM heard a key sound 8 steps ago" into an explicit, re-planner-visible predicate. It is a real-robot manipulation paper, on-theme, and a strong anchor for the contact-audio-as-state argument.
