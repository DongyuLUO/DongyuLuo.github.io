Reactive Diffusion Policy: Slow-Fast Visual-Tactile Policy Learning for Contact-Rich Manipulation

Han Xue*, Jieji Ren*, Wendi Chen*, Gu Zhang, Yuan Fang, Guoying Gu, Huazhe Xu‡, Cewu Lu‡ · Shanghai Jiao Tong University / Tsinghua IIIS / Shanghai AI Lab / Shanghai Qi Zhi · 2025 (arXiv:2503.02881v3; RSS 2025) · arXiv:2503.02881 · PDF · project page

One-liner. RDP splits a visuo-tactile imitation policy into a slow latent diffusion planner (1–2 Hz, models complex non-Markovian behavior) and a fast asymmetric-tokenizer decoder (>20 Hz, closes the loop on high-frequency tactile/force feedback), so a robot can both plan a chunked contact-rich trajectory and react instantly to contact changes — the thing vanilla action-chunking diffusion policies structurally cannot do.

Problem & motivation

Visual imitation-learning policies (Diffusion Policy, ACT, π0) rely on action chunking to model temporally consistent, non-Markovian behaviors (pauses, oscillatory motion). But a chunk is executed open-loop: during chunk execution the policy can't respond to real-time tactile/force signals, so these methods are confined to low-precision tasks (pick-and-place, push-pull) that need neither force control nor fast response. Prior visuo-tactile IL work adds touch mostly at the observation level (tactile as extra channels) while still chunking actions open-loop — so it inherits the same reactivity ceiling. The paper's claim: humans do contact-rich tasks via two coupled processes — (1) feedforward/predictive action and (2) closed-loop fine-tuning on tactile feedback — and a policy needs both. A second obstacle is data: most teleoperation rigs give the operator no fine-grained tactile feedback, so they can't collect high-quality contact-rich demos in the first place.

Method

Two contributions: a teleoperation system (TactAR) and a policy architecture (RDP).

TactAR (data collection, Fig 2). A low-cost ($500, Meta Quest 3) AR teleoperation system that renders real-time tactile/force feedback as a 3D deformation field — a sensor-agnostic representation — "attached" to the robot end-effector in AR space so the operator perceives contact in 3D. For optical tactile sensors, normalized marker positions Dt are tracked and a score-based optical flow Ft = [dx, dy] = Flow(D0, Dt) (Eq. 1) yields the field Vt, with a z-offset added and rendered as 3D arrows. EE pose comes from hand-pose tracking; supports single-arm and bimanual, multiple sensors simultaneously.

RDP slow-fast policy (Fig 6). A two-level hierarchy mirroring human predictive-action + closed-loop-finetuning:

Slow policy = Latent Diffusion Policy (LDP). Runs at 1–2 Hz on low-frequency observations (RGB + tactile + proprioception). Instead of denoising raw actions, it denoises a latent action chunk Z = E(A) ∈ Rt×d (a CNN-encoded downsampling of the action chunk A ∈ RT×D). Trained as a standard FiLM-conditioned 1D U-Net DDPM (Eq. 3), it preserves diffusion's multi-modal, non-Markovian modeling power but over the compact latent.

Fast policy = Asymmetric Tokenizer (AT). A 1D-CNN encoder E + GRU decoder D. The decoder reconstructs the real action Â = D(concat([Z, Freduced])), where Freduced is the high-frequency tactile/force representation. The asymmetry is the key trick: tactile is fed only to the decoder, never to the latent encoder, so the latent chunk Z retains only high-level strategy while precise reactive correction lives in the fast decoder. Trained with L1 reconstruction + small KL penalty (Eq. 2). At inference it autoregressively decodes actions at 24 Hz, fine-tuning the slow policy's latent chunk against the latest tactile/force (Fig 6b) — it acts like a learnable impedance controller. Inference: <1 ms (AT) vs 100 ms (LDP) vs 120 ms (vanilla DP) on RTX 4090 (Table I).

Tactile/force representation. For optical sensors, PCA (d=15 components) over the marker deformation field — robust to texture/lighting/gel-replacement, and the top components turn out to be explicable (tangential force, torsional torque, normal force; Fig 18). For force/torque sensors, just concatenate the 6-D wrench. Implementing suggestions: relative-EE-pose trajectories (per UMI) and latency matching (discard the first few predicted steps to align sent actions with execution latency) are both reported essential.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Self-collected real-world demos via TactAR — 60 demos (Peeling), 80 (Wiping), 50 (Bimanual Lifting), with proactively-recorded reactive behaviors. Three contact-rich tasks: Peeling (peel a hand-held cucumber), Wiping (wipe handwriting off a hand-held vase with rotation), Bimanual Lifting (two arms clamp + lift a paper cup along a curve without crushing it). Evaluation under No / Before-contact / After-contact perturbations. No public benchmark.
  • Hardware / simulator: Two Flexiv Rizon 4 arms w/ joint torque sensors + Flexiv Grav grippers; RealSense D435/D415 cameras. Three sensor types swapped across experiments: GelSight Mini (8MP, 25 FPS), improved MCTac (2MP, 30 FPS, $50 lab-built), and built-in joint torque sensors (wrench via Flexiv RDK, 120 Hz → 24 FPS). Workstation: i9-14900K + RTX 4090. No simulator (real-world only).
  • Baselines: Diffusion Policy (visual only); DP (tactile image); DP (tactile embedding, PCA); vs. RDP (tactile embedding) and RDP (force). Plus internal comparators: temporal-ensemble DP (varying τ), small-chunk DP, absolute-action and no-latency-matching ablations.
  • Compute: not reported (training compute/time not given; inference latencies in Table I).

Wiping (Table III): RDP (Force) 0.87 vs best DP 0.57. Bimanual Lifting (Table IV): RDP (GelSight+MCTac) 0.80 / RDP (Force) 0.70 vs DP ≤0.10. Key findings:

  • Q1 — naively adding tactile to observation doesn't help. DP-tactile-embedding (0.39–0.50) is no better, sometimes worse, than visual DP — because open-loop chunking can't exploit the signal. The win comes from the reactive fast policy, not from tactile-as-input.
  • Q4 — reactivity. The gap is largest under perturbation-after-contact (Peeling: RDP 0.80 vs DP 0.15). When the robot loses contact, DP plays out the stale chunk open-loop; RDP re-corrects.
  • Q3 — sensor-agnostic. Works with GelSight, MCTac, and force/torque, and even mixes two different optical sensors (different LEDs, gels, marker arrays, resolutions, FPS) on the two grippers in Bimanual Lifting. Force often does best (lower latency, lower dimensionality).
  • Q5 ablations (Table V). Shrinking chunk size 8→2 drops grasp success 100%→20% (small chunks can't model non-Markovian pauses). Temporal ensemble is hyper-sensitive to τ (best Wiping score only 0.15 vs RDP 0.50). Latency matching contributes ~50.6% and relative-trajectory ~59.5% of Peeling score (Fig 10).
  • Ablations (Table VI). Normal-force-only drops 0.95→0.48; a symmetric tokenizer (tactile into encoder too) drops 0.95→0.58 — validating both shear/torsion and the asymmetric design.
  • Data quality (Q6/Q7). TactAR feedback raises stable-contact-force ratio 0.58→0.87; ≥70% of 10 users found AR feedback "very helpful." RDP trained on no-feedback VR data scores >30% lower (Fig 14).

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • TactAR's AR feedback is less intuitive/efficient than direct human-hand contact; teleoperation efficiency limited by sensor + system latency.
  • System is built for two-finger grippers; extending to dexterous hands with per-finger tactile is future work.
  • The fast policy reacts to high-frequency tactile/force but not high-frequency images (vision stays slow) — limits tasks needing fast visual reaction.
  • RDP is currently single-task; they propose folding the AT idea into a VLA by replacing the VLA action tokenizer with an asymmetric one.

What I noticed reading it:

  • Statistics are thin. Bimanual Lifting is 10 trials/variation and Peeling/Wiping scores are means with no variance/seeds reported in the main tables — a much smaller statistical claim than the score gaps suggest. All eval is real-world single-blind, hard to reproduce.
  • No public benchmark. Three bespoke tasks, self-collected data, custom sensor (improved MCTac). Cross-paper comparison is impossible; the only baselines are the authors' own DP variants.
  • Where does the reactivity actually come from? The fast policy is trained to reconstruct demo actions from latent+tactile; its "reactive" corrections are only as good as the reactive behaviors operators happened to record. The paper proactively recorded such behaviors — so part of the win may be data curation, not architecture alone.
  • The latent action chunk is purely strategic and the abstraction is continuous; there's no symbolic notion of contact state. The policy knows "push harder" but never names is_in_contact or is_peeled — relevant to the predicate angle below.

Why I care

This is squarely on-theme for the thesis behind the 2026-06-24 batch: many manipulation predicates — is_in_contact, force_is_stable, is_peeled, is_clamped_without_crushing — are not visually evaluable; they live in touch and force. RDP is a clean demonstration that the control side of that thesis is real: open-loop visual chunking collapses on exactly the contact transitions a vision-only predicate would miss (perturb-after-contact 0.15→0.80), and the fix is high-frequency force in the loop.

Relative to BLADE: RDP is the controller-layer complement to BLADE's planner layer. BLADE composes diffusion-policy controllers via symbolic preconditions/effects but flags (in its own "Why I care") that continuous parameters like force sit entirely inside the diffusion policy and that its segmentation depends on gripper state, breaking on contact-rich tasks. RDP is precisely a better such low-level controller: a force-reactive diffusion policy that BLADE-style bi-level planning could call as a behavior body. The open question RDP sharpens for my own line: can the slow latent chunk be made symbolic / predicate-grounded so that a force-aware contact predicate (learned, like BLADE's visual classifiers, but over the tactile/force stream) gates when the fast reactive policy switches modes? That would marry BLADE's abstraction layer to RDP's reactivity — a concrete project seed.
