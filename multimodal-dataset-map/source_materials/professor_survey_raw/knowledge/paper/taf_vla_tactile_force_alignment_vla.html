Tactile-Force Alignment in Vision-Language-Action Models for Force-aware Manipulation

Yuzhe Huang*, Pei Lin*, Wanlin Li*, Daohan Li, Jiajun Li, Jiaming Jiang, Chenxi Xiao†, Ziyuan Jiao† · Beihang University / ShanghaiTech / BIGAI / HKU · 2026 · arXiv:2601.20321 · PDF · project page

One-liner. TaF-VLA argues that feeding tactile images into a VLA as "more vision" is the wrong move — tactile observations should be grounded in the physical force quantities they represent — so it pretrains a contrastive adapter that aligns sequential vision-based-tactile (VBTS) images to 6-axis force/torque and matrix-pressure codes, then injects those force-aligned tokens into a flow-matching VLA, beating a tactile-vision-aligned baseline by ~22% on contact-rich tasks.

Problem & motivation

VLAs are visually fluent but "force-blind": RGB cameras suffer occlusion and have no direct read on interaction forces, friction, or compliance, which are exactly what govern fragile-object handling, precise insertion, and tool use. The mainstream fix — treat vision-based tactile sensor (VBTS) frames as extra visual tokens aligned to scene images via a ViT (the authors call this "tactile-vision alignment") — aligns tactile data with visual appearance rather than physical dynamics. The paper's thesis is a paradigm shift: tactile representations must be grounded in the physical quantities they represent, i.e. tactile-force alignment, because a single static tactile frame can't disambiguate a stable grasp from incipient slip — that distinction is history-dependent and lives in the force trajectory. Three obstacles motivate the design (Q1) tactile–force paired data is scarce; (Q2) tactile is high-dimensional/geometric while force is low-dimensional/dynamic, so naive force regression generalizes poorly across sensors; (Q3) how to fuse the aligned representation into a VLA backbone without modality collapse.

Method

Three stages (Fig 1, Fig 3): a tactile–force data device, a contrastive TaF-Adapter, and integration into a flow-matching VLA.

1. TaF-Device & TaF-Dataset (Q1). A 3D-printed (25×20×45 cm), Dynamixel-actuated rig with a Stewart-platform lower stage and a yaw/Z upper stage applies identical forces simultaneously to a VBTS and to a co-located ATI Axia80 6-axis F/T sensor + 12×12 piezoresistive matrix-force array, via mechanically coupled motor-gear DoF pairs that enforce kinematic coherence. A "press-and-perturb" protocol sweeps Z-pressure while driving roll/pitch/yaw to span static indentation through incipient slip. With swappable indenters (>60 distinct: 40 rigid, 20 deformable) and 6 different VBTS units (4 custom-built + 2 GelSight Mini), it yields ~100K synchronized frames/hour and a final TaF-Dataset of >10M synchronized visuotactile / 6-axis F/T / matrix-force tuples.

2. TaF-Adapter: tactile-force alignment (Q2). Raw force at step i is a tuple Fi = (F'i, F''i) with F' ∈ R12×12 (pressure map) and F'' ∈ R6 (global wrench). Over a sliding window of length N=5, two MLP encoders project the pressure and wrench sequences to latents z', z'', each vector-quantized against its own separate codebook (VQ-VAE), and concatenated into a force code c* = concat((c')*, (c'')*). On the tactile side, per-frame ViT features over a window are aggregated by a causal Transformer with a learnable summary token s whose output z* is the holistic tactile embedding. A symmetric InfoNCE loss LNCE pulls z* toward its paired force code c* and away from force codes at other timesteps; total loss adds quantization and reconstruction terms (L = LNCE + λ1Lquant + λ2Lrecon). The decoupled dual codebook, the temporal window, and the discrete quantization are the three load-bearing choices — quantization suppresses high-frequency noise and hardware-specific artifacts, and reconstruction prevents codebook collapse.

3. TaF-VLA policy integration (Q3). The frozen TaF-Adapter is implanted into the π0.5 flow-matching backbone. A VLM encodes a third-person image, a wrist image, and the instruction into ϕ; the adapter extracts a local tactile latent ztac from a window of VBTS frames; both condition the flow-matching action expert LFM (velocity field over noised actions atτ, integrated from noise at inference). Crucially, the training demos use force-aware language instructions ("gently secure", "lift softly", "light controlled release") generated by an LLM via physics-informed chain-of-thought (Fig 9, Fig 10), supplying the directives that standard semantic-only datasets omit.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: TaF-Dataset (>10M synchronized tactile–force frames, 6 VBTS units, >60 indenters) for adapter pretraining; a curated force-aware manipulation dataset of >10,000 real-world episodes across 20+ contact-rich tasks (master-puppet teleop, FR3-isomorphic exoskeleton) with LLM-generated force-aware instructions for policy fine-tuning. Evaluation on a suite of 8 real-world force-critical tasks (Tube Insertion, Tweezer Weight Pick, Power Bank Extraction, Whiteboard Erasing, Jelly Slicing, Heavy Part Lifting, Tongs Food Pick, Chocolate Grasping); Table I reports 7.
  • Hardware / simulator: Real robot only (no simulator). Franka Research 3 arm via a mechanically isomorphic exoskeleton teleop interface; VBTS = 4 custom GelSight-style sensors (4×4 / 7×7 / 9×9 marker densities) + 2 GelSight Mini, at 30 Hz; ATI Axia80 F/T + 12×12 piezoresistive matrix array. Inference on NVIDIA RTX 4090; data device on a separate 3D-printed rig.
  • Baselines: ACT (RGB + proprioception); FreeTacMan (tactile-vision alignment on ACT, the key foil); ACT + TaF-Adapter; Diffusion Policy (DP); DP + TaF-Adapter; π0.5 (vision-only VLA backbone fine-tuned on the same data).
  • Compute: not reported (training cost / GPU-hours not given; only that inference runs on a single RTX 4090).

Results

Headline: TaF-VLA averages 64.8% success across 7 tasks vs 37.1% for the vision-only π0.5 backbone, 42.8% for FreeTacMan, and 47.6% for the best ACT+adapter variant — roughly doubling vision-only and beating the tactile-vision baseline by ~22 points (Table I).

Other findings:

  • Plug-and-play. Bolting the adapter onto ACT and DP improves both consistently (absolute +6.7% to +33.3%); ACT+TaF-Adapter even surpasses FreeTacMan on the same backbone (+20.0% on Power Bank Extraction), arguing tactile-force alignment beats tactile-vision alignment independent of backbone.
  • Cross-sensor robustness (Table II). On an unseen VBTS, TaF-Adapter holds ~60.3% mean while an explicit force-prediction (regression) baseline collapses to 30.0%; on an unseen GelSight, 53.3% vs 23.3%. On seen sensors the adapter still edges regression (75.0% vs 66.7% on Custom-built A). Latent contrastive alignment transfers; pixel-to-force regression does not.
  • Data efficiency (Fig 7). TaF-VLA at 100 demos matches vision-only π0.5 at 200 demos on Tongs Food Pick / Power Bank / Chocolate Grasping — the force-aligned codebook acts as a structured physical prior, roughly halving demo requirements.
  • Where it doesn't win much: on geometry-dominated Tube Insertion, vision-only baselines already do well (53.3%) and TaF-VLA's margin is small; on Chocolate Grasping (lightweight, gentle) vision-only is competitive — force sensing is, by the authors' own framing, task-dependent.
  • Ablations (Table III). Removing history (N=1), shrinking the codebook, merging the two codebooks into one shared codebook, or corrupting force labels with noise each drops all three benchmark tasks sharply (e.g. Whiteboard Erasing 60.0 → 26.7 with no history, → 13.3 with a shared codebook). Temporal context + decoupled discrete codebooks + clean force data are each necessary.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • Most failures occur in the pre-contact phase — an "air gap" where the manipulator stops millimeters short and never triggers the tactile–force loop (esp. Tweezer Weight Pick); some failures are insufficient applied force (Fig 8).
  • Receding-horizon action output limits closed-loop correction frequency, hurting high-frequency dynamic events (force spikes in Jelly Slicing); faster inference architectures are needed.
  • Cross-sensor generalization is only shown across visually-similar VBTS units; transfer to capacitive / magnetic / piezoresistive (non-visual) sensors is untested.
  • The 3D-printed, consumer-grade device injects fabrication-induced biases into ground-truth force labels; direct-drive / CNC hardware could improve fidelity.
  • The adapter feeds the action expert only; humans also use touch to update plans (e.g. "this is slippery, replan") — a fully unified vision-tactile-language-action backbone is left to future (and would need data scaling beyond current limits).

What I noticed reading it:

  • Success rates are count-of-15 (15 trials/task), so each cell moves in ~6.7% quanta with no variance reported. Several headline gaps (e.g. Jelly Slicing 53.3 vs 33.3) are ~3 successful trials; no error bars or seeds means statistical confidence is weak relative to BLADE's seed-averaged CALVIN table.
  • The central "tactile-force > tactile-vision" claim rests almost entirely on the FreeTacMan comparison — a single tactile-vision baseline reimplemented on ACT by these authors. No independent tactile-vision VLA (e.g. an RGB-token-aligned π) is tested, so the comparison isn't fully apples-to-apples at the VLA tier.
  • The TaF-Dataset and the force-aware-instruction LLM annotation are arguably the biggest contributions, but they're entangled: TaF-VLA gets both the force-aligned adapter and force-aware language, while π0.5 presumably gets neither force tokens nor (clearly) the force-aware instructions — the ablations don't isolate how much of the gain is the language rewriting vs the tactile tokens.
  • ">10M frames" is impressive but it's a tactile-force alignment corpus on indenters, not manipulation data; whether 10M indenter contacts is the right scale for the adapter (vs overkill or insufficient diversity) is asserted, not ablated.
  • Force is treated as ground truth from the F/T sensor, yet the device couples sensor and indenter mechanically — the "identical force" assumption depends on the compliant Z element and gear coherence holding exactly; any drift would silently corrupt the alignment target.

Why I care

This paper is a direct, well-argued instance of the batch thesis that many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — they live in touch/force. Where BLADE learns visual predicate classifiers (turned-on(faucet)) and pushes all continuous force/parameter content down into the diffusion-policy body, TaF-VLA is essentially building the sensory substrate BLADE's "Why I care" flagged as a gap: is_grasped, is_screwed_tight, grasp_is_stable_vs_slipping are exactly the history-dependent, force-grounded states TaF-Adapter's contrastive codebook is designed to represent. The "static frame can't distinguish stable grasp from incipient slip" argument is the force-domain echo of BLADE's "every-frame vs first/last-frame" insight — both say the discriminative signal is in the temporal trajectory, not a snapshot.

Two threads worth tracking forward into my own work: (1) force-aware language — the LLM rewriting of instructions into "gently secure / lift softly" is a cheap way to inject force-modulation directives, and it's a natural symbolic interface; one could imagine BLADE-style force predicates (e.g. compliant-contact, grip-secure) being learned exactly the way TaF-Adapter learns its codebook, then exposed to a planner. (2) The discrete force codebook as a predicate vocabulary — a quantized, decoupled latent over force/pressure is a stone's throw from a learned symbolic abstraction; the gap between "VQ codebook entry" and "planning predicate" is the same gap UniPred/InterPreT-style predicate-invention work is closing on the visual side. This is the force-grounded analogue of predicate invention. The paper is on-theme and useful as a sensory front-end for the long-horizon / planning-abstraction line, not a planning paper itself.

  • ForceVLA (Yu et al. 2025, arXiv:2505.22159) — force-aware MoE VLA; closest sibling in the C cluster (already a batch paper, see below).
  • Octopi (Yu, Lin, Soh, RSS 2024) — tactile-language property reasoning; a batch paper, cited here as tactile-LM context.
  • Neural Discrete Representation Learning / VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al. NeurIPS 2017) — the quantization machinery the TaF-Adapter codebooks rest on.

Newly ingested in the 2026-06-24 batch — directly relevant to this work:

  • ForceVLA — nearest peer: also injects 6-axis F/T into a VLA for contact-rich tasks, but via an explicit force-aware MoE rather than a contrastive tactile-force codebook; clean architectural contrast.
  • Tactile-VLA — tactile tokens for physical-knowledge generalization in a VLA; same C-cluster goal, different fusion.
  • OmniVTLA and VLA-Touch — other tactile/force VLA policies; comparison points for how to wire touch into the language-action stream.
  • FAVLA — force-adaptive fast/slow VLA; relevant to TaF-VLA's stated inference-frequency limitation.
  • FuSe (Beyond Sight) — fuses heterogeneous sensors with language; the multisensory-VLA superset of TaF-VLA's vision+force+language fusion.
  • Reactive Diffusion Policy and Tactile-Conditioned Diffusion Policy — the DP-side analogues; TaF-VLA's DP+TaF-Adapter variant is the bridge to this F-cluster line.
  • Sparsh and AnyTouch — tactile representation/foundation models; alternative pretrained tactile encoders to the TaF-Adapter.
  • Towards Forceful Robotic Foundation Models (survey) — the survey framing the whole force-aware-VLA push TaF-VLA sits in.
