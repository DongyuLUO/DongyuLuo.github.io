FACTR 2: Learning External Force Sensing for Commodity Robot Arms Improves Policy Learning

Steven Oh*, Jason Jingzhou Liu*, Tony Tao*, Philip Han, Kenneth Shaw, Satoshi Funabashi, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Deepak Pathak · Carnegie Mellon University / Waseda University · 2026 · arXiv:2606.12406 · PDF · project page

One-liner. FACTR 2 learns to estimate external joint torque on cheap force-sensor-less arms from just 10 minutes of free-motion data (NEXT), then uses that estimated force signal both to drive force-feedback teleoperation and to re-weight imitation-learning data toward pre-contact and contact phases (FIRST) — bringing force-aware manipulation to off-the-shelf robots with zero added sensing hardware.

Problem & motivation

Contact-rich manipulation — insertion, fastening, deformable handling — needs force sensitivity that humans rely on but that vision alone can't reliably infer. Dedicated force-torque (FT) sensors live mostly on expensive arms (Franka, Flexiv, KUKA); low-cost arms (AgileX Piper $2,500, YAM) lack them, and retrofitting end-effector FT sensors can cost more than the arm itself. Existing sensorless force-estimation approaches lean on analytical inverse-dynamics + system identification, which is fragile to friction dead zones, torque ripple, hysteresis, and other history-dependent actuator effects; learning-based methods exist but typically need paired FT-sensor labels or simulation contact data. FACTR 2 wants the force signal on commodity hardware with no FT supervision at all.

Method

The combined system has two pieces: NEXT (force estimation) and FIRST (force-informed policy training). It generalizes the prior FACTR (which assumed dedicated joint-torque sensors) to arms without them.

NEXT — Neural External Torque estimation. External joint torque is the residual τ_ext = τ_m − τ_f, where measured motor torque τ_m = K I_m comes from motor current and τ_f is the torque that would be needed to realize the observed motion in free space. Rather than computing τ_f from a model-based rigid-body inverse-dynamics (M q̈ + C q̇ + g + τ_other, which demands accurate inertial/friction/gravity ID), NEXT learns a free-space inverse dynamics network τ̂_f = f_θ(x) directly from joint states. The input x_i = [q_{i−H:i}, q̇_{i−H:i}, Δq_{d,i−H:i}] is a temporal history of joint position, velocity, and tracking error (commanded minus current position), with history length H = 50. Because the training data is collected without contact, measured motor torque equals free-space torque, so τ_m is its own supervision target (L2 regression, Eq. 4). f_θ is a 2-layer LSTM (hidden 128) with a 2-layer MLP head; a stateless sliding-window formulation avoids hidden-state drift over long trajectories. At deployment, external torque is the residual between measured motor torque and the predicted free-space torque (Fig 2). Training needs only ~10 min of free-motion data (collected by teleop, motion planning, or replay) and ~1 min on an RTX 3090; inference runs at 568 Hz (run at 100 Hz).

Force-feedback teleoperation. The NEXT external torque estimate is plugged into the FACTR bilateral control law τ_feedback = K_{f,p} τ_ext (Eq. 8), giving haptic feedback on low-cost leader-follower setups that previously had none.

FIRST — Force-Informed Re-Sampling Training. A flow-matching behavior-cloning policy (conditioned on M camera images, joint position q_t, and estimated external torque τ̂_ext,t; velocity-prediction objective, Eq. 5) is trained, but FIRST changes which data points get sampled. Each demonstration timestep is labeled free motion, pre-contact, or contact using the scalar contact score f_t = ||τ̂_ext,t||_1 with hysteresis thresholding (turns on at T_high, off at T_low). The 1-second window before each contact onset is labeled pre-contact; everything else is free motion (Eq. 6–7). FIRST then assigns phase-dependent sampling weights w_F, w_PC, w_C with w_PC, w_C > w_F, so mini-batches over-represent the failure-prone pre-contact and contact phases (Fig 7 shows these phases have higher validation loss; up-sampling them lowers it). Motivation: failures concentrate not in free space but in the brief pre-contact alignment intervals and the contact phase requiring controlled, corrective interaction.

Setup

  • Datasets / benchmarks: Force-estimation eval uses 5 min held-out contact data + 5 min free-space data on a Franka (built-in FT sensor as ground truth) and free-space data on an AgileX Piper (zero-torque as GT). FIRST is evaluated on five long-horizon contact-rich tasks — LEGO Assembly, NIST Belt Assembly, NIST Insertion, Tool Clean Up, Cap Screwing (Fig 3) — with 250 demonstrations per task, 20 rollouts per task, scored by task progress rate (stage decomposition in Table 7).
  • Hardware / simulator: Franka Panda (for force-eval ground truth + a 20-participant teleop user study), AgileX Piper and YAM (low-cost target arms). Bimanual Piper setup with four RGB cameras (two wrist, two overhead) for policy learning. DINOv3-Base image encoder; all real-robot, no simulator.
  • Baselines: Force estimation — FILIC (model-based MuJoCo inverse dynamics residual), Disturbance Observer (DO, momentum-based), and the dedicated external sensor itself. Policy learning — Base Policy (no force), Base Policy + Torque (NEXT torque as extra input), FACTR (force + image blur curriculum), TA-VLA (auxiliary torque-reconstruction objective). Run with both flow-matching and ACT policy architectures.
  • Compute: NEXT trains in ~1 min on an NVIDIA RTX 3090 (24 GB); inference 1.76 ms/forward (568 Hz) on an i9-10920X CPU. Policies trained on an A6000 (48 GB), 12–24 h wall-clock.

Results

Headline: NEXT matches a dedicated FT sensor's quality on the Franka, achieving 0.547 Nm average L1 contact error — 87.6% lower than FILIC and 62.8% lower than the disturbance observer (Table 1). In free space (true external torque should be zero) NEXT reaches 0.414 Nm, beating even the dedicated sensor (0.449 Nm) by learning hardware-specific effects from on-robot data. On the low-cost Piper, NEXT hits 0.018 Nm free-space error (Table 5), far below both baselines.

Teleoperation user study (20 participants, Franka wiping task): NEXT-based feedback rated easier to use than no-feedback, DO, and position-position baselines, and comparable to FACTR Teleop which uses dedicated joint-torque sensors; it also lowered applied joint torque (less unnecessary exertion, Fig 4). Repeated on the sensorless Piper (Fig 9), NEXT again beats the baselines.

FIRST policy learning: across all five tasks FIRST gives the highest task progress, with the abstract claimed improvement of over 17% in task progress over prior force-aware policies (Fig 6, flow matching; Fig 11, ACT). The up-sampling ablation (Table 2) shows up-sampling pre-contact (PC) gives the best average task progress (0.818) vs contact-only (0.670) or PC+C (0.811); pre-contact alone wins on 3 of 5 tasks, while Cap Screwing (sustained contact) prefers PC+C (0.825).

Ablation (Table 9) separates the two contributions: phase-based up-sampling helps even without force as a policy input, and force-conditioned policies beat their no-force counterparts; the two combined (NEXT torque as input + FIRST re-sampling) are best. NEXT-estimated torque also beats raw motor current as policy input on both NIST Insertion and Cap Screwing (Table 10), showing the learned free-space subtraction yields a cleaner contact signal than current alone.

Limitations & open questions

From the authors:

  • NEXT's absolute torque scale depends on the motor torque constant K; if K is inaccurate, absolute scale needs calibration against an external FT sensor. Less limiting for teleop (forces scaled for comfort) and policy learning (inputs normalized).
  • NEXT is robot-specific and may require retraining across arms due to hardware variation.

What I noticed reading it:

  • NEXT is only validated against ground truth on the Franka (which has a sensor); on the actual low-cost target arms, contact accuracy is never directly measured — only free-space error (where GT is trivially zero). The whole pitch is force on cheap arms, yet contact-phase fidelity on the Piper/YAM is assessed only indirectly through the teleop user study and downstream policy success, not against a reference signal.
  • FIRST's contact labeling is itself a function of NEXT's noisy estimate via a hysteresis threshold; threshold sensitivity (T_high, T_low) is never ablated, so the phase labels — and thus the whole re-sampling benefit — rest on hand-tuned thresholds.
  • The "+17%" headline aggregates task progress (partial-stage credit), not binary success; success-rate tables (Table 9) cover only 2 of 5 tasks. The strongest claim is on the softer metric.
  • Up-sampling magnitude saturates/degrades past m≈5 (Fig 12) — a real risk of over-biasing the data distribution that is acknowledged but means the method has a task-specific knob to tune.
  • Free-space data collection assumes you can reliably generate truly contact-free trajectories; for cluttered workspaces or compliant arms that sag under gravity, "no contact" may be harder to guarantee than implied.

Why I care

This sits squarely on the thesis I keep returning to from BLADE: many manipulation predicates are not visually evaluable — is_inserted, is_screwed_tight, is_grasped, contact_made live in force/touch, not pixels. FACTR 2 is a clean demonstration that the force channel needed to ground those predicates can be recovered for free on commodity hardware, no FT sensor required. NEXT's external-torque residual is, in effect, a learned in_contact / contact_score signal — and FIRST already discretizes it into free-motion / pre-contact / contact phases via hysteresis, which is precisely the kind of categorical, force-derived state that BLADE's purely-vision predicate classifiers cannot produce. A natural BLADE extension: segment demos and label force-grounded predicates (e.g. is_inserted) from NEXT-style torque rather than from cropped pixels, attacking BLADE's own limitation that "continuous parameters (force) sit entirely inside the diffusion policy." FACTR 2's relevance is direct, not tangential: it is the sensing substrate that would let the BLADE abstraction layer become non-purely categorical for force-modulated tasks. It also lands on the structure-vs-scale axis differently than BLADE — it is pure flat BC with a smarter data distribution, no symbolic plan — so it's a useful foil for what abstraction buys you on long-horizon contact tasks.
